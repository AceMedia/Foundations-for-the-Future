# Culture, Media, Sport and Civil Society

*A forward-looking proposal, judged by what works, not by left or right.*

> **Summary for the reader**
> - **The problem:** public broadcasting is funded by a flat licence fee that fits the streaming
>   age poorly, arts and grassroots sport are squeezed, gambling harm is rising, and online safety
>   is unresolved.
> - **The options:** keep the licence fee, reform it, or move the BBC toward subscription and a
>   tighter remit.
> - **Lead option:** **move public broadcasting toward subscription and a slimmer core public
>   remit**, sustain arts and grassroots sport, regulate gambling for harm, and lead on online
>   safety through parents and devices.
> - **Timeline:** reform broadcasting funding over a parliament; act on gambling and sport sooner.
>
> *Costings are indicative; the detailed figures live in the budget files.*

---

## The problem

Culture, sport, and a healthy media are part of what makes a country worth living in, and they
carry real economic weight. Several parts of the system are under strain.

**The licence fee fits the age badly.** A flat charge of around £170 on every household raises
roughly £3.7 billion a year, the bulk of BBC funding, but it sits awkwardly in a world of streaming
and choice, evasion is rising, and it is increasingly hard to justify or collect fairly.[^bbc]

**Arts and grassroots sport are squeezed.** Funding pressure falls hardest outside London, where
local culture and sport do the most for communities and young people.

**Gambling harm is rising.** Britons lose around £15.6 billion a year to gambling, and online
gambling in particular has outpaced its rules, with real harm to vulnerable people, which connects to
the regulated-vice approach in [vice taxation](../health/vice-taxation-and-betterment.md).[^gambling]

**Online safety is unresolved.** The argument over protecting children online keeps reaching for
heavy-handed, privacy-invading tools.

**The do-nothing baseline.** Leave this and the licence fee erodes without a plan, local culture
and sport keep shrinking, gambling harm grows, and online safety gets fought over case by case.

---

## Evidence base

- **Subscription public broadcasters exist.** Other countries fund public-service content through
  subscription or general taxation rather than a flat licence fee. *Transferable* as a fairer,
  more modern funding base, with care to protect public-service content.
- **Grassroots sport pays back.** Investment in community sport improves health and cuts costs
  elsewhere. *Transferable* as good value.
- **Device-level online safety works.** Operating-system controls protect children while
  protecting privacy, as Apple's 2026 tools show.[^apple] *Transferable* as the right model.

---

## Options appraisal

**Option 1: Keep the licence fee.** *Pros:* funds the BBC as now. *Cons:* increasingly unfair and
hard to enforce in the streaming age. The do-nothing baseline.

**Option 2: Reform the funding model.** Replace the licence fee with progressive or
general-taxation funding. *Pros:* fairer. *Cons:* ties public broadcasting more tightly to the
Treasury.

**Option 3: Subscription and a slimmer remit *(lead option)*.** Move public broadcasting toward
subscription, with a tighter core public-service remit that the state still guarantees. *Pros:*
ends the flat compulsory charge, lets people choose, and focuses public money on genuine
public-service content. *Cons:* must protect the things only public-service media reliably
provides, such as impartial news and content for minority audiences. **Recommended.**

---

## The proposals (under the lead option)

1. **Move the BBC toward subscription.** End the flat licence fee over time and move to a
   subscription model, while the state guarantees a slimmer core of genuine public-service content
   (impartial news, content the market will not provide).
2. **Define and protect the public-service core.** Guarantee and fund the things only public-service
   media reliably provides: impartial national and regional news, the World Service, children's
   content, and programming for minority audiences and the nations, so they survive the funding change
   rather than being left to a shrinking subscriber base.
3. **Sustain arts and grassroots sport.** Back local culture and community sport, especially
   outside London, as good value for health and community.
4. **Regulate gambling for harm.** Bring in affordability checks, stake and speed limits on the most
   harmful online products, and curbs on advertising and sponsorship, tying the approach and its
   revenue to [vice taxation](../health/vice-taxation-and-betterment.md) so harm falls and the money
   helps fund treatment.
5. **Online safety through parents and devices.** Lead on protecting children with operating-system
   controls and parental tools, as Apple's 2026 features show,[^apple] rather than mandatory age or
   identity verification, in line with [civil liberties](../governance/civil-liberties.md).
6. **Back local journalism and civil society.** Support local and public-interest journalism against
   the collapse of local news (a quiet threat to democracy), and back the charities and community
   groups that hold civic life together and that the rest of this framework relies on to deliver.

---

## Costs and benefits

Moving to subscription reduces a compulsory charge and focuses public money on content only
public-service media provides. Backing grassroots sport and culture is modest spending with strong
returns in health and community. The figures belong in the budget files.

---

## Delivery and timeline

- **Years 1-2:** define the protected public-service core; set out the path from licence fee to
  subscription; tighten gambling rules.
- **Years 2-5:** complete the funding transition; sustain arts and sport funding; embed
  device-level online safety.
- **Years 5-10:** a modern, fairer settlement for public media, culture, and sport.

---

## Risks and mitigations

- **Losing valuable public-service content.** *Mitigation:* define and fund a protected core
  before changing the funding model.
- **Subscription pricing out poorer households.** *Mitigation:* keep core public-service content
  freely available.
- **Gambling reform lobbied down.** *Mitigation:* tie it to the vice-taxation framework and harm
  evidence.

---

## Consultation questions

1. What public-service content must be protected and guaranteed, whatever the funding model?
2. How should the move from licence fee to subscription be designed and phased?
3. How far should online gambling be tightened, and how should the revenue be used?
4. How do we protect children online through devices and parents without mandatory ID checks?
5. What is the right protected public-service core, and how should subscription fund it?
6. How do we sustain local journalism and civil society as they come under strain?
