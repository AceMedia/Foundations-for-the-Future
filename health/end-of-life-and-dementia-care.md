# End of Life and Dementia Care: Dignity Before Asset Depletion

Old age should not become a forced liquidation event.

The current UK care system asks too many older people and families to do something most people would regard as morally backwards: spend down a lifetime of savings, sell the family home where necessary, and leave only a narrow residue before the state offers full help. For dementia patients this feels especially unjust. Dementia is a medical condition, but much of the long-term support it requires is treated as chargeable social care rather than NHS care.

The result is a system where one family can receive NHS-funded treatment for a short, acute illness, while another spends years paying care home bills because their illness is slower, degenerative, and classed as care need. That distinction may make administrative sense inside government, but it does not make human sense.

This chapter argues for a **Dementia and End-of-Life Care Guarantee**: a funded settlement that protects ordinary savings, covers the dementia premium, improves palliative care outside hospital, and gives older people enough financial dignity to enjoy life, support loved ones, or leave money to family, friends, or charity.

---

## The current settlement is too harsh

In England, the 2026/27 social care charging rules still leave the upper capital limit at **£23,250** and the lower capital limit at **£14,250**. A person above the upper limit is normally responsible for the full cost of care home fees. Between the limits, they contribute from income and a tariff on capital. Below the lower limit, they still usually contribute from income, but their capital is ignored.

That is a very low protection floor. A person who worked, saved, bought a modest home, or simply put money aside for retirement can be treated as able to pay until most of that buffer is gone.

The devolved systems differ, but the basic question is shared across the UK:

| Nation | Current care-home capital rule |
|---|---:|
| England | Upper limit £23,250; lower limit £14,250 |
| Scotland | Higher limits, plus free personal and nursing care entitlements |
| Wales | Residential care capital limit £50,000 |
| Northern Ireland | Upper limit £23,250; lower limit £14,250 |

These thresholds matter because care home costs are now high enough to burn through savings quickly. In England, local authority adult social care data for 2024/25 put the average cost of a week of residential or nursing care at **£1,185.55**, or about **£61,649 per year**. Private self-funder market figures are often higher. Recent sector data reported average self-funder costs of about **£1,298 per week** for residential care, **£1,535 per week** for nursing care, **£1,343 per week** for residential dementia care, and **£1,564 per week** for nursing dementia care.

At those rates, even a relatively ordinary stay can consume tens of thousands of pounds. A two-year stay in nursing dementia care at £1,564 per week is about **£162,656** before extras. For many families, that is the house, the savings, the inheritance, the charity donation, and the money that might have been spent making the person's last years more joyful.

The NHS does provide some routes of help, but they are limited. NHS Continuing Healthcare can fully fund care where a person is assessed as having a primary health need, but many dementia patients are treated as having social care needs instead. NHS-funded nursing care helps with registered nursing costs in a nursing home; in England the standard rate from 1 April 2025 was **£254.06 per week**. That is useful, but it does not come close to removing the wider care home bill.

## Dementia exposes the unfairness most clearly

Dementia is one of the clearest examples of the boundary problem between health and social care.

Alzheimer's Society estimates that dementia costs the UK **£42 billion** a year, rising to **£90 billion** by 2040 without action. It also estimates that **63%** of dementia costs are borne by people with dementia and their families, and that a person with dementia spends around **£100,000** on care over their lifetime on average. Dementia care is estimated to be about **15% more expensive** to deliver than standard social care.

That extra cost is not luxury. It reflects real need: specialist staffing, secure environments, behaviour support, higher supervision, communication support, and more complex personal care. Yet too often the family sees it as a dementia surcharge on top of already unaffordable fees.

The principle should be simple:

- dementia care should be treated as a health-and-care need, not a private misfortune;
- the extra dementia premium should be publicly funded;
- families should not be forced to choose between safe care and preserving a lifetime's modest assets;
- public support should protect ordinary savings while still asking more from very high wealth where that is fair.

This is not about turning care homes into free inheritance-protection schemes for the richest households. It is about stopping catastrophic care costs from stripping ordinary people of everything they built.

## End-of-life care is already paid for, but often in the wrong place

The UK already spends large sums on people in the final year of life. Nuffield Trust and Health Economics Unit analysis, commissioned by Marie Curie, estimated at least **£22 billion** of UK public expenditure on people in their last year of life in 2022, around **£34,000 per person who died**. More than half was health care, and hospital care dominated that spending.

That matters because many people do not want their final weeks to be built around avoidable emergency admissions. Marie Curie has reported that over a third of deaths in England and Wales occur in hospital, while only a small minority of people express a preference to die there. NHS England also has statutory guidance requiring integrated care boards to commission palliative and end-of-life care.

The conclusion is not that hospital care should be denied. It is that more of the end-of-life budget should be available earlier, closer to home, and through hospices, care homes, community nursing, respite, and family support. A better system would reduce avoidable hospital pressure while giving people more control over their final months.

## Other end-of-life problems the system should fix

End-of-life policy is not only about care home fees. People often face a cluster of practical problems at the same time: cold homes, poor food, loneliness, paperwork, transport, unpaid caring pressure, pain, fear, funeral costs, and unsuitable housing. A serious plan should treat these as connected failures.

The wider policies in this repo already point toward practical fixes.

| End-of-life issue | What it causes | Linked fix in this framework |
|---|---|---|
| Fuel poverty and cold homes | Worse symptoms, anxiety, avoidable admissions, undignified final months | Essential-bills support, retrofit, social tariff for terminal illness |
| Food insecurity and malnutrition | Weakness, falls, infections, poor recovery, carer stress | Bulk food procurement, tailored nutrition, community kitchens |
| Unsafe or unsuitable homes | Falls, delayed discharge, forced care-home moves | Housing retrofit, adaptations, decent homes, repair workforce |
| Loneliness and isolation | Depression, distress, avoidable crisis calls, dying alone | Well-being budgets, community venues, volunteer and befriending routes |
| Carer burnout | Family breakdown, crisis admissions, poverty, mental ill-health | Respite, carer income support, food/energy help, practical home services |
| Benefits and admin delays | People spend final months fighting paperwork | Automatic fast-track support, SR1 trigger, single end-of-life passport |
| Poor pain and symptom control | Distress, emergency admissions, loss of trust | Hospice-at-home, palliative advice lines, regulated medical relief |
| Transport and appointment burden | Missed care, exhaustion, expensive travel | Community transport, local clinics, home visits, digital where useful |
| Funeral poverty and death admin | Debt, family conflict, pressure to overspend | Funeral cost transparency, modest grants, advance planning support |
| Workforce shortages | Missed visits, rushed care, poor continuity | Care workforce pathway, second-chance skills, public-service rehabilitation |

### Fuel poverty and cold homes

Many people need more heat, electricity, laundry, and equipment use after a terminal diagnosis. They may spend more time at home, need medical devices, or require warmer rooms because illness makes temperature harder to tolerate. Marie Curie and Loughborough University estimated that around **128,000** people died in fuel poverty in the UK in 2023, and that a 50% social tariff could lift a large share of dying people out of fuel poverty.

The fix should be automatic. A terminal diagnosis or advanced frailty assessment should trigger:

- an end-of-life energy social tariff;
- protection from disconnection and aggressive debt recovery;
- fast-track boiler, insulation, damp, mould, and heating repairs;
- council-funded emergency heating and laundry support where needed.

This links directly to the welfare and housing chapters: secure essential bills, retrofit homes, and prevent cold housing from becoming a health crisis.

### Food, hydration, and tailored nutrition

End-of-life food is not just about calories. People may need softer food, smaller meals, fortified meals, culturally familiar food, help with swallowing, or meals prepared for carers as well as the patient. Poor nutrition can worsen weakness, pressure sores, falls, infections, confusion, and recovery after hospital discharge.

The bulk food procurement system should therefore include an **end-of-life nutrition route**:

- dietitian-designed soft, fortified, diabetic, renal, low-salt, and culturally appropriate meal packs;
- delivery to homes, hospices, supported housing, and care homes;
- emergency food support for unpaid carers;
- public kitchens and community centres able to provide warm meals locally;
- food production and packing supported by supervised rehabilitation work pathways where appropriate.

This is a clear example of one repo policy strengthening another. Bulk procurement lowers cost and improves reliability; the justice rehabilitation chapter can add supervised food growing, packing, kitchens, and logistics; health policy gets fewer nutrition-related crises.

### Housing adaptations, falls, and delayed discharge

Many end-of-life problems are really housing problems. A person may be medically stable enough to leave hospital but unable to return home because they need a ramp, a downstairs bed, a wet room, heating repairs, grab rails, or space for equipment. If those adaptations are delayed, hospital beds stay occupied and people spend precious time in the wrong place.

The housing programme should include a **rapid end-of-life adaptations service**:

- 72-hour installation routes for rails, ramps, key safes, commodes, and bed moves;
- fast-track wet-room and downstairs-living adaptations where prognosis and need justify it;
- repair teams for heating, damp, mould, trip hazards, lighting, and unsafe stairs;
- equipment recycling and repair so wheelchairs, beds, hoists, and mobility aids are not stuck in procurement delays.

The justice rehabilitation proposal can help here too, but only in non-sensitive roles: repairing equipment, maintaining gardens, building ramps under supervision, refurbishing furniture, and supporting public estate repair. That gives people skills while reducing practical bottlenecks.

### Loneliness, fear, and dying alone

Loneliness is not a soft issue. NHS advice notes that older people are especially vulnerable to loneliness and social isolation, and Age UK has reported that large numbers of older people have little or no regular contact. At the end of life this becomes sharper: people fear being alone, relatives may live far away, friends may have died, and mobility may be limited.

The welfare well-being budget should therefore extend into later life and end-of-life care:

- befriending and sitting services;
- transport to social spaces, faith groups, clubs, and family visits;
- digital inclusion where it helps people stay connected;
- small personal budgets for outings, hobbies, music, books, and social contact;
- community venues funded through local betterment money.

The Personal Dignity Allowance in this chapter should not only cover toiletries and haircuts. It should help people keep relationships alive.

*[See the welfare and well-being reform chapter](../welfare/welfare-and-wellbeing-reform.md)*

### Carer burnout and family poverty

Unpaid carers hold the system together, but many are financially and emotionally exhausted. Carers UK found in 2024 that many unpaid carers were worried about living costs, cutting back on essentials, and reducing social contact. When carers collapse, the patient often ends up in hospital or residential care earlier than necessary.

End-of-life support should include a **Carer Stability Offer**:

- automatic benefits and council-tax checks for carers;
- respite hours that can be booked before crisis point;
- food and heating support for the whole household;
- paid leave and flexible work protections for family carers;
- counselling and bereavement support before and after death;
- practical help with laundry, cleaning, transport, equipment, and paperwork.

This is not only compassionate. It is cheaper than leaving families unsupported until everything breaks.

### Benefits, paperwork, and the end-of-life passport

The UK already has Special Rules for End of Life benefits. GOV.UK guidance says people nearing the end of life can receive certain benefits more quickly and at a higher rate, usually supported by an SR1 form from a medical professional. The problem is that families still have to navigate too many separate systems while under extreme pressure.

Create a single **End-of-Life Support Passport**. Once triggered by an SR1 form, palliative diagnosis, advanced dementia assessment, or severe frailty review, it should automatically notify the relevant systems:

- DWP fast-track benefits;
- local authority care assessment;
- energy social tariff;
- Blue Badge and transport help;
- equipment and home adaptation teams;
- hospice-at-home and palliative care coordination;
- carer support and respite services;
- funeral and legal planning advice.

The patient and family should not have to retell the same story ten times.

### Pain, symptom control, and medical relief

Fear of pain is one of the deepest end-of-life fears. The answer is not only hospital admission. It is fast access to symptom control wherever the person is.

The health and vice-taxation chapters already create a useful principle: regulated medical relief should be easier to access than unsafe or chaotic coping. For end-of-life care this means:

- 24/7 palliative advice lines;
- rapid access to pain relief, anti-nausea medication, breathlessness support, and syringe drivers;
- pharmacy delivery and anticipatory prescribing;
- low- or zero-duty prescribed cannabis or related regulated products where clinically appropriate;
- mental health, spiritual, and psychological support as ordinary parts of care.

Patients should not be left in unmanaged pain because of stigma, patchy prescribing, or slow logistics.

*[See the vice taxation and betterment framework](./vice-taxation-and-betterment.md)*

### Funeral poverty and family pressure

The financial pressure does not stop at death. Funeral costs can push families into debt, and many people do not know their loved one's wishes. SunLife's 2026 Cost of Dying report put the average simple attended funeral at **£3,828**, with traditional attended funerals higher and direct cremation lower.

The state should not dictate how families grieve, but it should stop grief becoming a market trap. Practical reforms:

- mandatory plain-language funeral price lists and standard low-cost options;
- stronger Funeral Expenses Payment support for low-income households;
- advance funeral wishes included in the End-of-Life Support Passport;
- permission for Personal Dignity Allowance savings to be used for chosen funeral, memorial, or charitable wishes;
- community venues and faith/community groups supported to host low-cost memorials.

This protects dignity without encouraging overspending.

### Workforce shortages and the quality of care

End-of-life care depends on people: nurses, care assistants, hospice staff, social workers, cooks, cleaners, drivers, pharmacists, volunteers, and family carers. If the workforce is thin, care becomes rushed and fragmented.

The answer is not one workforce policy. It is a pipeline:

- better pay and progression in adult social care;
- care apprenticeships and health-science routes in the education chapter;
- non-personal care support roles for suitable prison leavers and community-sentence participants;
- bulk procurement and public kitchens to reduce pressure on care staff;
- housing repair teams to prevent avoidable admissions and discharge delays;
- local betterment funding for volunteer coordination and community support.

End-of-life care should be treated as a national workforce priority, not charity overflow.

## A Dementia and End-of-Life Care Guarantee

The UK should create a dedicated guarantee with eight parts.

### 1. Publicly fund the dementia premium

Where a person has a diagnosed dementia-related need, the additional dementia premium above standard residential or nursing care should be met through a national Dementia Care Supplement.

This would:

- stop care homes charging families extra simply because dementia support is complex;
- make local authority rates more realistic;
- reduce hidden cross-subsidy between self-funders and council-funded residents;
- encourage specialist dementia provision without punishing families for needing it.

The supplement should follow the person, not the provider. It should be paid only where assessed dementia needs are being met to an inspected standard.

### 2. Restore a lifetime care cap, then improve it for dementia and terminal need

The abandoned adult social care charging reforms included an **£86,000** lifetime cap on eligible personal care costs and a more generous means test. Government analysis estimated the steady-state public cost at about **£3.7 billion in 2027/28**, including illustrative Barnett consequentials.

That is a useful scale marker. A serious reform should:

- restore a lifetime cap on eligible personal care costs;
- ensure dementia-related assessed care counts properly toward the cap;
- create a faster route to protection for people with terminal or advanced degenerative conditions;
- keep daily living costs transparent, capped, and affordable from income where possible.

The cap should protect against catastrophic care bills. It should not be designed so narrowly that most families still lose everything before help arrives.

### 3. Raise the personal dignity allowance

Care home residents supported by a local authority are left with a Personal Expenses Allowance. In England this remains only a small weekly amount. That is not enough for a dignified life.

A reformed system should create a **Personal Dignity Allowance** for care home residents, set high enough to cover ordinary human wants:

- clothes, toiletries, haircuts, phone and internet;
- small gifts for grandchildren, friends, partners, or carers;
- transport, hobbies, outings, books, music, subscriptions, and personal comforts;
- voluntary donations to a chosen charity or faith/community group.

The point is not extravagance. It is the recognition that a person in care is still a person, not a balance sheet attached to a bed.

### 4. Build hospice-at-home and care-home palliative support

Hospices and community palliative teams should be funded as core infrastructure, not optional charity patchwork. The state should shift more funding into:

- 24/7 palliative advice lines;
- hospice-at-home teams;
- community nursing and rapid-response medication support;
- respite for family carers;
- specialist end-of-life support in care homes;
- advance care planning that follows the person across NHS, social care, and ambulance systems.

This connects directly to the wider health prevention argument in this repo: spend earlier, spend closer to people, and avoid expensive crisis care where possible.

*[Read the wider NHS and public health chapter](./nhs-and-public-health.md)*

### 5. Protect ordinary assets without writing a blank cheque for wealth

The system should protect a meaningful floor of ordinary savings and housing wealth. People should be able to use retirement for joy, relationships, and legacy, not only as a reserve fund for institutional care.

At the same time, reform has to be fair to younger workers and renters who may own little themselves. The right model is therefore progressive protection:

- protect ordinary savings much more generously than the current English floor;
- cap catastrophic personal care costs;
- prevent forced sales during a spouse or dependent's lifetime;
- allow modest gifts, charitable donations, and personal spending without aggressive deprivation-of-assets assumptions;
- consider a small progressive contribution from very high-value estates only above a high threshold, if needed, instead of unlimited care fees on ordinary families.

This is a better moral trade-off than the present system, which often protects people only after they have already been financially hollowed out.

### 6. Create an End-of-Life Support Passport

End-of-life support should not depend on exhausted families knowing which form, tariff, council team, NHS service, or charity to contact. A single passport should be triggered by an SR1 form, advanced dementia diagnosis, palliative care assessment, or severe frailty review.

The passport should coordinate:

- fast-tracked benefits and pension support;
- energy social tariff and essential-bills help;
- local authority care and carer assessments;
- equipment, adaptations, and transport;
- hospice-at-home, palliative care, and pharmacy support;
- funeral, legal, and advance care planning advice.

This would turn a fragmented system into a single route through the final year of life.

### 7. Guarantee food, warmth, and home safety

Nobody should spend their final months choosing between heating, food, transport, and care. The guarantee should include an essentials package:

- automatic energy social tariff for terminal illness and advanced frailty;
- tailored food and meal delivery through bulk procurement;
- rapid home repairs and adaptations;
- equipment recycling, delivery, and maintenance;
- emergency support for unpaid carers in the same household.

These services should be treated as health prevention, not optional welfare extras.

### 8. Build a community care workforce around the person

End-of-life care needs paid professionals, trained volunteers, family carers, and practical support workers. The guarantee should fund local care networks that include:

- hospice and community nursing;
- care assistants with progression and training;
- carer respite and sitting services;
- food, laundry, transport, and maintenance support;
- carefully safeguarded non-personal care support roles from public-service rehabilitation pathways.

The aim is continuity. People should not be passed between disconnected services while dying.

## How to fund it

This repo already argues that vice taxation should fund repair, not disappear into Treasury opacity. Older people's dignity should be one of the visible uses of the Health and Care Betterment Fund.

*[Read the vice taxation and betterment framework](./vice-taxation-and-betterment.md)*

### A dedicated Older People's Dignity Fund

Create an **Older People's Dignity Fund** within the wider Health and Care Betterment Fund. It should have published receipts and allocations, so people can see that taxes on activities with social costs are paying for care, recovery, community life, and dignity.

The fund could draw from four sources.

| Funding stream | Why it fits | Illustrative scale |
|---|---|---:|
| Ring-fenced share of existing vice duties | Alcohol, tobacco, and gambling already generate large receipts; a visible share should repair social harm and fund dignity | OBR estimates for 2025/26: alcohol duties £11.9bn, tobacco duties £8bn, betting and gaming duties £4bn |
| Future regulated adult-use markets | If cannabis or other products are regulated and taxed, part of receipts should fund health, recovery, and care | TBD, not counted until legal design exists |
| Reallocation from avoidable emergency end-of-life care | Better community and hospice care can reduce some avoidable hospital demand | Nuffield/Marie Curie estimate £6.6bn emergency hospital spend in final-year health care |
| Lower delivery costs through public-service rehabilitation | Carefully supervised, non-sensitive work can support food packing, equipment repair, gardens, laundry, logistics, and maintenance | Not a main funding source; should reduce some support costs while building skills |
| Mature savings from wider repo reforms | Housing, welfare, nutrition, and admin reform reduce downstream pressure | Existing repo estimate: £3-6bn annual steady-state savings |

A modest earmark is meaningful. Ten percent of alcohol, tobacco, and betting/gaming duties would be about **£2.4 billion** on 2025/26 OBR estimates. Fifteen percent would be about **£3.6 billion**, close to the government's previous estimate for restoring the adult social care charging reforms. That does not solve every social care problem, but it shows the scale is not fantasy.

The funding split could be:

- **35%** for dementia supplements and specialist dementia care;
- **25%** for lifetime cap and means-test protection;
- **15%** for hospice-at-home, community palliative care, and care-home end-of-life support;
- **10%** for Personal Dignity Allowances, family respite, and bereavement support;
- **10%** for food, warmth, adaptations, equipment, and carer essentials;
- **5%** for End-of-Life Support Passports, advice, advocacy, and local coordination.

This should sit alongside, not replace, core social care funding. Councils should not have to fund a national moral commitment from already strained local budgets.

The policy priority is to protect ordinary savings while improving care quality. That means the main funding pressure should be met through health-and-care-focused vice-tax receipts, smarter procurement, better home support, and carefully supervised public-service rehabilitation where it lowers practical delivery costs. Families should not be treated as the first resort for every cost simply because they saved responsibly.

## Budget treatment

This is not a conventional "saving" in year one. It is a fairness and insurance reform with long-term system benefits.

The direct cost should be shown separately from the repo's existing **£3-6 billion** annual savings estimate. Some offsetting savings are plausible, especially where better palliative support reduces avoidable emergency admissions, where dementia care stabilises families earlier, and where local authorities spend less time in adversarial financial disputes. But those savings should be modelled cautiously.

The honest fiscal treatment is:

- core reform cost: **TBD**, using the previous £3.7bn charging reform estimate as a scale marker;
- earmarked vice-tax contribution: **£2.4-£3.6bn** if 10-15% of current alcohol, tobacco, and gambling duties is allocated;
- additional future revenue: **TBD** from regulated adult-use markets;
- wider-system savings: **TBD**, with conservative modelling through the health and welfare savings files.

## Why this belongs in Foundations for the Future

The wider argument of this repo is that the state should stop paying for failure late and start building stability early. End-of-life and dementia care fits that argument exactly.

Housing reform protects people from insecurity. Welfare reform protects people from destitution and humiliation. Vice taxation turns unavoidable adult behaviour into visible public repair. Health reform shifts attention from crisis treatment to prevention, dignity, and honest support.

Older people's care should be governed by the same principle. A decent country does not ask people to save all their lives only to watch those savings vanish into the final corridor of care. It should pool catastrophic risk, fund complex dementia support properly, and leave people with enough freedom to spend, give, enjoy, and leave something behind.

The end of life should be a chapter of dignity, not a financial extraction process.
