# Vice Taxation and Betterment: Regulate Freedom, Fund Repair

Britain's vice policy is stuck between two failed instincts: moral panic and quiet hypocrisy. People drink, smoke, gamble, and use recreational drugs whether politicians approve or not. In hard times especially, many people seek comfort, relief, stimulation, ritual, or escape. Pretending this can be abolished by disapproval does not remove demand. It merely changes who profits and where the harms land.

A practical state should start from reality. Adults should be free to make imperfect choices, but those choices should take place in regulated markets rather than criminal ones. And where vice creates social costs, part of the price should be recycled into public repair: treatment, mental health, social care, end-of-life dignity, and community health.

This is not a case for celebrating unhealthy habits. It is a case for governing them honestly.

---

## The core principle

Vice should be:

- legal where regulation is possible and prohibition clearly fails;
- taxed according to likely social harm;
- sold under strict rules on age, quality, packaging, venue licensing, and advertising;
- tied to published reinvestment into betterment, not quietly absorbed into general Treasury opacity.

The goal is to replace three bad outcomes at once:

- black-market profit without standards;
- public-health costs without dedicated funding;
- hollow moralism that punishes users while tolerating the demand that created the market.

---

## Moral panic is not policy

Prejudice and moral panic are poor tools of government. Adults make personal choices that others may dislike, but dislike is not a sound basis for criminal law. The state should regulate harms, protect minors, and provide treatment where needed, while respecting adult autonomy and dignity.

That means distinguishing clearly between **use** and **abuse**. A free society does not criminalise every risky or unhealthy behaviour simply because it is imperfect. It monitors patterns of harm, sets rules for safe sale and consumption, intervenes where there is exploitation or clear danger, and leaves room for lawful adult choice everywhere else.

This is the same principle that should guide many parts of public life: government is there to prevent coercion, exploitation, fraud, and public harm, not to punish adults for failing to live according to someone else's moral preferences.

---

## Why this approach fits Britain

Britain already taxes some vices heavily, but does so without a coherent philosophy. Alcohol and tobacco are taxed. Gambling is licensed. Yet other common intoxicants are left to criminal markets, where there is no age control, no quality assurance, no potency labelling, and no tax capture.

A better system would admit a simple fact: vice is not going away. The serious question is whether it happens in pubs, shops, and licensed adult-use venues under rules, or in street markets and criminal supply chains with no rules at all.

There is also a cultural point. Vice is often social. The pub, at its best, is not just a place to consume alcohol. It is a civic institution: a meeting point, a place of routine, conversation, and local memory. Britain should not push all vice into isolated, illicit, or antisocial spaces. It should bring adult behaviour back into supervised and taxable environments.

---

## Reducing stigma improves mental health and recovery

Stigma creates its own harms. Many adults who use substances for relief, routine, or temporary comfort are not primarily damaged by the substance itself, but by the fear, secrecy, and instability that come with being treated like criminals. Someone using cannabis to manage pain, sleep problems, anxiety, or the side effects of other conditions may be living with a second burden: not just the underlying problem, but the constant fear of being caught, judged, or pushed into an unsafe market.

That fear is not trivial. It increases anxiety, drives people away from honest conversations with doctors and family, and leaves them buying unknown products from people with no standards and no accountability. A regulated system removes part of that stress. People can make adult choices openly, know what they are buying, and seek advice earlier when something is going wrong.

The same principle matters even more in recovery. People with serious dependency problems often delay treatment because they expect to be vilified, shamed, or treated as morally weak rather than medically unwell. That is exactly backwards. If we want people to enter recovery sooner, stay in treatment longer, and rebuild their lives, we have to remove the atmosphere of humiliation around asking for help.

A better vice policy should therefore aim not only to regulate markets, but also to normalise honest engagement:

- users should be able to seek health advice without assuming confession leads to punishment;
- families should be encouraged to talk about risky use early, before crisis point;
- treatment services should be presented as ordinary healthcare, not social disgrace;
- public messaging should separate concern about harm from contempt for the person.

Reducing prejudice does not mean pretending every vice is harmless. It means recognising that shame often makes harm worse. People are easier to help when they are not hiding.

---

## A betterment fund, not a blank cheque

Revenue from vice taxation should be ring-fenced into a **Health and Care Betterment Fund**, published annually with clear allocations. The purpose is to show the public that this is not a cynical cash grab. It is an offset model: if vice carries costs, vice should help pay for the health and care repair.

Illustrative allocations:

- addiction treatment and recovery services;
- NHS mental health and crisis support;
- social care and end-of-life dignity support;
- local authority community health, prevention, and recovery programmes;
- prescribed medical access, pain-management alternatives, and early intervention.

This has two advantages. First, it is fairer. Second, it is politically durable. People will tolerate vice taxation more easily when they can see where the money goes.

Detailed revenue scenarios sit in [../budget/health-and-care-betterment-revenue.md](../budget/health-and-care-betterment-revenue.md).

## How the fund should be split

Ring-fencing only works if the public can see the formula. The fund should therefore be allocated by published rules rather than annual horse-trading inside Whitehall.

For this framework, vice-tax receipts should be treated first as a **Health and Care Betterment Fund**. National resilience remains important, but it should be funded through defence and resilience budgets rather than taking the first call on vice receipts.

An illustrative health-and-care split would be:

- **40% national health and recovery funding** for addiction services, mental health, prescribing support, pain management alternatives, and NHS prevention work;
- **25% social care and end-of-life dignity funding** for dementia premiums, palliative care, hospice-at-home, care caps, and Personal Dignity Allowances;
- **20% local community health funding** for councils and combined authorities, distributed partly by population and partly by local harm indicators, to support youth clubs, sport, culture, recovery spaces, community kitchens, and healthier social venues;
- **15% prevention and medical access funding** for prescribed medical relief, smoking cessation, alcohol treatment, pain management, and early intervention.

Within the local share, at least part should be visibly neighbourhood-facing. If a town hosts regulated vice sales, that town should see some return in the form of stronger health, care, recovery, and community infrastructure.

The fund should be reported in a simple annual statement:

- how much was raised by each vice category;
- how much went to health, social care, end-of-life care, prevention, and local community health;
- which local authorities received support;
- what measurable outcomes were achieved.

This matters because trust depends on visibility. People are far more likely to accept vice taxation if they can see that it is repairing real damage in health and care.

## Licensing and market rules must be strict, clear, and local

Legalisation without control would be unserious. The point of a regulated market is that it replaces criminal supply with standards, accountability, and visible adult boundaries.

A workable licensing framework should include:

- a national rulebook for product safety, potency bands, packaging, advertising limits, and traceability;
- local licensing powers for councils to control outlet density, opening hours, venue conditions, and neighbourhood fit;
- separate licences for cultivation, processing, wholesale, retail sale, and on-site consumption;
- strong penalties for sales to minors, diversion into illicit resale, tax evasion, and product contamination;
- mandatory testing and clear labelling for strength, ingredients, and health warnings.

Different products should sit in different channels:

- **beer, tobacco, and similar products** through existing retail and licensed hospitality structures, with adjusted tax and venue rules;
- **cannabis** through licensed dispensaries, pharmacies where appropriate, and tightly regulated social-consumption venues if introduced;
- **psychedelic products** only through specialist licensed routes with much tighter controls, purchase limits, and supervision requirements.

The point is not to make every vice equally available. It is to match regulation to harm profile and social setting.

---

## How vice should be taxed

The tax model should be based on six rules:

1. **Higher-risk products should carry higher rates.** A weak beer should not be treated the same as a high-strength spirit or a heavily potent drug product.
2. **Taxes must not be so high that they preserve the black market.** The legal market must be safe and competitive enough to displace criminal supply.
3. **Simple products can be taxed by unit or pack.** Beer and cigarettes fit this model.
4. **Potency-sensitive products should be taxed by active content bands.** Cannabis and psychedelic products fit this better than flat taxes alone.
5. **Social venues should get relief where they provide safer, regulated consumption.** Independent pubs should not be taxed like large chains or anonymous off-licence volume sellers.
6. **A share of receipts should go back to the places that host the trade.** Town centres and local services should see visible benefit.

---

## Illustrative tax examples

These are examples of policy design, not final Treasury costings. They show the direction of travel: legalise, regulate, tax by harm, and keep legal prices low enough to beat illegal supply.

### Beer

Beer is already culturally embedded and usually consumed socially rather than covertly. Tax policy should recognise the difference between a low-strength pint in a pub and high-volume supermarket alcohol pricing.

Illustrative model:

- low-strength beer: low duty rate per pint equivalent;
- standard pub-strength beer: moderate duty;
- high-strength beer: higher duty per unit of alcohol;
- independent pub relief: reduced duty for beer sold in licensed pubs below a size threshold;
- supermarket floor pricing: prevent below-cost alcohol dumping that undercuts pubs and encourages high-volume home drinking.

Illustrative example:

- a weak session beer might carry around **10p-15p** of vice duty per pint;
- a typical pint might carry around **25p-40p**;
- a strong premium beer might carry around **50p+**.

The principle is simple: drink in supervised social venues should be treated more favourably than bulk discount drinking at home.

### Cigarettes

Cigarettes impose clear long-term health costs, so they should remain highly taxed. But the regime should also be honest: the state already tolerates legal tobacco while profiting from it. The missing piece is a clearer link between tobacco tax and visible reinvestment.

Illustrative model:

- maintain a high excise per pack;
- add a public-health surcharge clearly identified as funding lung health, cancer prevention, and smoking cessation;
- impose a minimum retail floor to stop predatory discounting;
- allow reduced-risk nicotine alternatives to be taxed more lightly than cigarettes.

Illustrative example:

- a standard pack of cigarettes could include **GBP2-GBP3** in vice-specific excise and surcharge before VAT;
- stronger anti-discount rules would stop the market racing to the bottom;
- a lower-risk nicotine product could face a much smaller duty to encourage harm reduction.

This keeps the deterrent effect while making the social contract visible: if smoking remains legal, part of that price should openly pay for the damage.

### Cannabis / weed

Cannabis is the clearest case for regulated legalisation. It is widely used, already normalised across much of the country, and currently enriches illegal markets while offering users no protection on potency, contamination, or age control.

Illustrative model:

- legal adult sale through licensed dispensaries or tightly regulated venues;
- tax partly by retail value and partly by THC potency band;
- lower rates for lower-potency flower;
- higher rates for concentrates and very high-potency products;
- strict limits on advertising, packaging, and youth-oriented branding.

Illustrative example:

- low-potency flower could carry around **GBP1 per gram** in excise;
- standard retail flower could carry around **GBP2-GBP3 per gram**;
- high-potency concentrates could carry materially higher rates, whether by gram or by THC-content band.

That gives government room to steer the market away from the strongest products without pushing ordinary users back to dealers.

Cannabis also needs a clearer split between medical and recreational access. One market can support the other, but they should not be treated as identical.

### LSD

If substances such as LSD are ever legalised, they should not be folded into the same model as alcohol or cannabis. They would require a more tightly controlled framework, with licensed sale, standardised products, very clear labelling, and strong restrictions on venue type and public use.

Illustrative model:

- sale only through licensed specialist outlets or clinical-adjacent regulated channels;
- tax by standardised dose band rather than loose product value;
- higher compliance burden than cannabis because accidental overuse or unsuitable settings can produce acute harm even where physical dependency risk is low.

Illustrative example:

- a standard legal unit could carry a modest excise, perhaps **GBP1-GBP2** per unit sold;
- multipacks or higher-strength products would face escalating duty bands and tighter purchase limits.

The state should not aim to make psychedelics a mass retail impulse buy. If legalised, they should sit inside a narrow and visibly cautious framework.

### Psilocybin mushrooms / shrooms

Psilocybin raises similar questions. It likely fits better with a controlled legal regime than outright prohibition, but again the point is not casual commercialisation. The point is quality control, adult access, and tax capture under strict rules.

Illustrative model:

- licensed sale only;
- taxation by psilocybin-content band or standardised product class;
- clear warnings, age restrictions, and purchase limits;
- no youth branding, no sweets-style packaging, no mass advertising.

Illustrative example:

- low-potency legal products could carry a light excise;
- standard adult-use products could carry around **GBP2-GBP5** in duty per retail unit depending on format and strength;
- stronger or novelty products would face higher rates and tighter controls.

For psychedelics, legitimacy must come with restraint. Legal should mean controlled, labelled, and taxed, not aggressively marketed.

## Bring vice back into safer social spaces

Britain's pub industry has been hollowed out by changing habits, cheap supermarket alcohol, punitive operating costs, and the loss of its place as a broad adult social venue. If the state wants vice regulated rather than hidden, it should help licensed venues survive.

That means treating pubs as part of the answer, not part of the problem.

### A new pub settlement

Pubs should receive:

- lower beer duty than supermarket alcohol sales;
- targeted business-rate relief for independent venues;
- simpler licensing for low-risk live music, food, and community events;
- access to local betterment-fund grants if they function as community anchors.

### Smoking in pubs at the owner's discretion

One option is to allow licensed pubs to choose whether to permit smoking, provided the choice is explicit, regulated, and visible before entry. The current all-or-nothing model treats every venue the same even when adults may want different environments.

A workable framework would require:

- owner choice rather than national compulsion;
- clear external signage so customers know before entering;
- staff opt-in protections so nobody is forced to work in a smoking venue against their will;
- strong ventilation and air-quality standards;
- the ability for councils to limit density where necessary so non-smoking options remain available locally.

The argument is not that smoking is healthy. It is not. The argument is that supervised adult social spaces are often safer and more civilised than pushing every vice outside into alleyways, doorways, and fragmented private settings. If smoking remains legal, licensed venues should have some room to cater to adult preference under clear rules.

This would also help some pubs differentiate themselves again instead of being undercut by cheap off-trade alcohol and an increasingly sterile licensing culture.

## Link vice reform to agriculture and domestic production

Britain should not only regulate vice markets. It should also capture the production value of them. If substances such as cannabis, psilocybin products, and other tightly controlled psychoactive inputs are legalised or medicalised, as much of the lawful supply chain as possible should be grown, processed, and taxed in the UK.

That would create a new line of work for British farmers, greenhouse operators, horticultural businesses, mushroom growers, laboratories, and rural manufacturers at a time when many agricultural businesses are under severe pressure. Instead of leaving this value to overseas producers or criminal import networks, Britain could build a regulated domestic sector with standards, traceability, and export potential.

The most obvious opportunities are:

- **cannabis cultivation** in licensed glasshouse, indoor, or secure farm settings;
- **psilocybin mushroom production** through regulated specialist growers;
- **hemp and related plant products** for both industrial and medical-adjacent markets;
- **tightly controlled pharmaceutical precursor crops and fungal derivatives** produced only under high-security licensing, where clinically or industrially justified.

This should be treated as part of a wider rural renewal strategy. A lawful vice economy can support:

- diversification for farms facing volatile commodity markets;
- new processing and packaging jobs in rural areas;
- research partnerships between growers, universities, and pharmaceutical manufacturers;
- exportable British standards in testing, packaging, and controlled-environment agriculture.

Britain is well placed for this. It already has agricultural expertise, strong life-sciences capacity, and a climate suited to a range of controlled horticulture and mushroom-growing systems. If regulation is clear and quality is trusted, the UK could become an exporter not just of product, but of high-standard regulated supply.

*[See how this fits the wider growth strategy](../economy/entrepreneurship-and-renewal.md)*  

### Domestic production should reduce prices for patients

If a substance is grown and regulated domestically, some of the efficiency gains should be used to lower costs for patients and those with genuine medical need. The state should not charge a person in chronic pain, severe anxiety, treatment-resistant conditions, or palliative care the same effective rate as a recreational consumer buying for leisure.

A fair system would separate the markets:

- **medical users** should receive lower prices, reduced tax, or direct subsidy where a product is clinically prescribed;
- **recreational users** should pay the fuller vice-duty rate;
- **domestic supply chains** should be used to keep legal medical products affordable and reliable;
- **patients should not be pushed toward opioids or other heavy painkillers** simply because the safer or more manageable option is priced out of reach.

This matters particularly in a cost-of-living crisis. People who are unwell are often already under financial strain, less able to work consistently, and more likely to suffer if relief becomes unaffordable. If regulated cannabis or related products can reduce pain, improve sleep, or help someone function without the same degree of impairment caused by stronger painkillers, there is a clear social case for making that option cheaper for patients than for casual adult use.

This is also a welfare issue, not just a health one. If people can stay functional, manage pain better, and avoid being priced into heavier medication or complete inactivity, the state saves on crisis support while individuals retain more independence and earning power.

*[See the linked welfare case](../welfare/welfare-and-wellbeing-reform.md)*  

### A clearer model for prescribed cannabis

If prescribed cannabis is to be a serious part of British healthcare rather than a niche luxury, the model needs to be explicit.

It should work as follows:

- prescriptions only through registered clinicians and regulated dispensing channels;
- zero or minimal vice duty on clinically prescribed products;
- a domestic-supply preference where UK-grown, quality-tested products meet medical standards;
- capped patient pricing for chronic pain, palliative care, severe nausea, sleep disruption linked to illness, and other approved indications;
- income-based support or NHS-linked subsidy for low-income patients;
- routine review so prescribing remains clinical rather than casual.

This creates a cleaner settlement. Recreational users finance the broader vice-duty system. Patients pay less, because they are not buying leisure. They are buying function, relief, and the ability to live with less pain and less impairment.

That is not special pleading. It is basic fairness. A welfare state should not force ill people to pay vice-market prices for symptom relief that may help them stay out of deeper poverty, heavier medication, or long-term worklessness.

## Local authorities should have a visible role

Councils should not be mere bystanders in a vice-reform system designed in Westminster. They are the level of government that sees the consequences first: nuisance, decline, empty high streets, pressure on treatment services, and the loss of community venues.

Local authorities should therefore be able to:

- approve or refuse premises based on local need and venue density;
- attach conditions around ventilation, signage, training, and neighbourhood management;
- receive a predictable local share of Betterment Fund receipts;
- use those receipts to support treatment, youth provision, street cleaning, culture, and hospitality renewal;
- publish local outcome reports so residents can see what regulated vice has paid for.

This is not just administration. It is democratic legitimacy. If vice is regulated locally, some of the repair should also happen locally.

## Implementation strategy: phase reform, do not lurch

Vice reform should be rolled out in phases. That reduces political panic, gives regulators time to learn, and allows the legal market to displace criminal supply without administrative chaos.

### Phase 1: establish the legal architecture

In year one, government should:

- pass the enabling legislation for the Health and Care Betterment Fund;
- create the national licensing rulebook and testing standards;
- set local authority powers on density, conditions, and venue approval;
- establish the medical-cannabis access framework with low or zero vice duty for prescribed patients;
- expand addiction treatment and recovery capacity in advance of wider market reform.

This phase is about trust. The public should see that the system begins with safety, health, and medical fairness, not with a commercial free-for-all.

### Phase 2: launch the first regulated channels

In years two to three, government should:

- implement reformed beer, tobacco, and hospitality rules, including pub relief and local fund allocations;
- open licensed domestic cultivation and processing routes for cannabis and related medical products;
- begin local Betterment Fund disbursement with published annual reporting;
- pilot tightly regulated adult-use cannabis markets in selected areas before national scale-up;
- use outcome data to adjust tax rates, potency bands, and licensing conditions.

This phase is about proving that regulation can outcompete criminal supply while delivering visible local benefit.

### Phase 3: expand cautiously and export standards

Once the system is stable, government can:

- expand lawful domestic cultivation and export capacity;
- consider tightly limited additional product categories, including specialist psychedelic routes, only where safeguards are credible;
- scale local authority funding formulas and neighbourhood renewal programmes;
- publish long-run health, welfare, crime, and fiscal outcomes to show whether the model is working.

The strategic aim is not just to collect tax. It is to build a lawful British industry, a credible medical-access regime, and a local public consent model strong enough to last.

### Limited home growing can be compatible with regulation

For some products, particularly cannabis plants and certain mushroom products, a legal framework could allow very limited home growing for personal use under clear adult rules. The purpose would not be to create an unregulated grey market, but to respect personal autonomy and reduce dependence on criminal supply for small-scale users.

Any such model would need firm boundaries:

- adults only;
- small possession and cultivation limits;
- no unlicensed commercial sale;
- no marketing to minors;
- clear penalties for diversion into illegal distribution.

Some jurisdictions also allow limited non-commercial gifting between adults. If Britain ever moved in that direction, it should do so cautiously and transparently, keeping the principle that personal cultivation is about autonomy and affordability, not about recreating a shadow market outside taxation and quality control.

---

## What this would achieve

A coherent vice-tax regime could:

- reduce black-market revenue;
- improve product safety and age controls;
- fund treatment and prevention honestly;
- support rural jobs, research, and domestic production;
- lower costs for legitimate medical users where clinically appropriate;
- revive pubs and other supervised adult venues;
- create visible local benefits from vice taxation;
- accept human reality without surrendering public standards.

That is the right balance for a serious country. Freedom without denial. Regulation without hysteria. Taxation with a purpose.

---

## Conclusion

Britain does not need to choose between puritanism and chaos. It can choose regulation. Adults can be trusted with freedom, but markets must be structured so that profit does not float free of responsibility.

Vice will never disappear. The sensible question is whether it is licensed, taxed, supervised, and made to contribute back. If we are honest about that, then beer, cigarettes, cannabis, and even tightly controlled future psychedelic markets can become part of a broader national bargain: if society tolerates the vice, the vice should help fund the repair.
