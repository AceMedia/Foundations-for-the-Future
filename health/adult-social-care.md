# Adult Social Care

*A forward-looking proposal, judged by what works, not by left or right.*

> **Summary for the reader**
> - **The problem:** adult social care is chronically underfunded, the workforce is underpaid and
>   stretched, the boundary with the NHS causes delayed discharges, and families face catastrophic
>   costs and a confusing system.
> - **The options:** keep patching it, or settle the funding, fix the workforce, and join it up
>   with the NHS.
> - **Lead option:** **a sustainable funding settlement, a properly paid and trained workforce, and
>   genuine integration with the NHS**, building on the end-of-life and dementia proposals.
> - **Timeline:** stabilise early; settle funding and workforce over a parliament.
>
> *Costings are indicative; the detailed figures live in the budget files.*

---

## The problem

Social care lets older and disabled people live with dignity, and it is one of the weakest links
in the whole system.

**It is chronically underfunded.** Councils carry the cost of rising demand on shrinking budgets,
as set out in [local government](../governance/local-government.md), so care is rationed and
quality suffers.

**The workforce is stretched and underpaid.** Care work is skilled and demanding but poorly paid,
with high turnover, so providers struggle to recruit and keep staff.

**The NHS boundary causes harm.** The split between free NHS care and means-tested social care
causes delayed discharges, with people stuck in hospital because the care to send them home is not
there, which is bad for them and expensive for the NHS.

**Families face catastrophic costs.** People can lose almost everything they own to care costs,
through a system few understand until they hit it.

**Working-age disabled adults are half the story.** Around half of all adult social care spending
goes on working-age disabled adults, not older people, yet the debate usually ignores them. A serious
settlement has to cover both.

**The provider market is fragile.** Council fee rates often sit below the actual cost of care, so
self-funders quietly cross-subsidise council-funded residents, and a large provider failure (a
Southern Cross-style collapse) is a standing risk to vulnerable people.

**The do-nothing baseline.** Leave this and pressure piles back onto hospitals, the workforce
crisis deepens, and more families face ruin, while the [end-of-life and dementia](./end-of-life-and-dementia-care.md)
proposals lack the system around them to work.

---

## Evidence base

- **Other countries fund care more fairly.** Germany and Japan run social insurance systems that
  share the cost of care across society rather than leaving individuals exposed. *Transferable* as
  a model for a fairer settlement, adapted to Britain.
- **Better pay and training cut turnover.** Where care work is valued and paid properly, retention
  and quality improve. *Transferable* as basic workforce sense.
- **Integration cuts delayed discharges.** Joining up health and care frees hospital beds and
  improves outcomes. *Transferable* as the prize from getting the boundary right.

---

## Options appraisal

**Option 1: Keep patching.** Annual top-ups and emergency funding. *Pros:* avoids hard choices.
*Cons:* the crisis deepens. The do-nothing baseline.

**Option 2: Means-tested status quo with a cap.** Keep the model but cap lifetime costs. *Pros:*
limits catastrophic costs. *Cons:* leaves the funding and workforce problems unresolved.

**Option 3: Settle funding, fix the workforce, integrate *(lead option)*.** A sustainable funding
settlement (a hybrid of a dedicated care contribution and general taxation, with a cap), a properly
paid and trained workforce, and genuine NHS integration. *Pros:* dignified care, fewer delayed discharges, a stable workforce, and an end to
catastrophic costs. *Cons:* needs a serious funding decision and reform. **Recommended.**

---

## The proposals (under the lead option)

1. **Settle the funding through a hybrid model.** Put adult social care on a sustainable footing with
   a **modest dedicated care contribution that everyone pays into, topped up from general taxation,
   and a lifetime cap** so no one loses everything. This shares the cost across society and across
   generations rather than leaving individuals exposed, draws on the German and Japanese care-insurance
   models (see [international comparisons](../budget/international-comparisons.md)), and builds on the
   [end-of-life and dementia](./end-of-life-and-dementia-care.md) proposals and the care cap.
2. **Value the workforce and steady the market.** Pay care workers properly, with training and a
   career path (the [care-workforce ladder](../education/education-and-skills.md)), so providers can
   recruit and retain staff. A properly paid domestic workforce also reduces the sector's heavy
   reliance on the overseas care-worker visa, which was opened wide and then abruptly cut, leaving
   providers exposed (see [immigration](../immigration/immigration.md)); pair it with fair council fee
   rates and oversight of large providers so the market does not fail vulnerable people.
3. **Integrate with the NHS.** Join up health and care so people can leave hospital when they are
   ready, cutting delayed discharges and improving outcomes.
4. **Support unpaid carers.** Back the millions of family carers who hold the system up, with
   respite, recognition, and financial support.
5. **Make the system understandable.** Give people clear information and a single route in, so they
   are not lost in a confusing system at the worst time.

## Costs and benefits

A proper settlement costs money, but it is cheaper than the hidden costs of delayed discharges, a
collapsing workforce, and families in crisis. The detailed costings build on
[budget/social-care-and-end-of-life-savings.md](../budget/social-care-and-end-of-life-savings.md)
and the budget files.

---

## Delivery and timeline

- **Years 1-2:** stabilise funding and pay; set out the long-term settlement; start NHS
  integration.
- **Years 2-5:** implement the funding settlement and workforce reforms; embed integration to cut
  delayed discharges.
- **Years 5-10:** dignified, sustainable care with a stable workforce and no catastrophic costs.

---

## Risks and mitigations

- **Funding decision ducked.** *Mitigation:* treat the settlement as the core of the chapter, not
  an optional extra.
- **Workforce reform too slow.** *Mitigation:* pay and training first, since they unlock
  everything else.
- **Integration in name only.** *Mitigation:* measure delayed discharges and hold the system to
  real joining-up.

## Consultation questions

1. How should the cost of care be shared: social insurance, general taxation, or a mix?
2. What pay and career structure would fix the care workforce?
3. How do we make NHS and care integration real rather than just structural?
4. What support would most help unpaid family carers?
5. How should the settlement cover working-age disabled adults as well as older people?
6. How do we steady the provider market and end the self-funder cross-subsidy?
