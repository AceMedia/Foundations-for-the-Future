# Mental Health

*A forward-looking proposal, judged by what works, not by left or right.*

> **Summary for the reader**
> - **The problem:** mental health services are hard to access, waits are long, prevention is
>   underdone, and crisis care too often falls to the police and A&E.
> - **The options:** more of the same, or a shift toward access, prevention, and proper crisis
>   care in the right settings.
> - **Lead option:** **cut waits and widen access, invest in prevention and early help, and move
>   crisis care out of police cells and A&E** into services designed for it.
> - **Timeline:** expand access and crisis care early; build prevention over a decade.
>
> *Costings are indicative; the detailed figures live in the budget files.*

---

## The problem

Mental ill health is one of the largest causes of lost work and human misery, and the system
meant to help is stretched thin.

**Access is hard and waits are long.** Mental ill health costs the UK economy at least £118 billion a
year, yet around a million people are waiting for mental health services.[^cost] Children and young
people wait longest: over 385,000 were on the waiting list in early 2025, and tens of thousands have
waited more than a year, some more than two.[^cyp]

**Prevention is underdone.** Early help, in schools, workplaces, and communities, is the cheapest
and kindest intervention, yet it is the first thing squeezed.

**Crisis care lands in the wrong place.** Too often a mental health crisis ends up handled by the
police or in A&E, neither of which is designed for it, which is bad for the person and expensive
for the system.

**Coercion falls unequally.** There were nearly 53,000 detentions under the Mental Health Act in
2024-25, and Black people are detained about four times as often as white people, and placed on
community treatment orders about eight times as often, a disparity that is widening.[^mha] A system
that relies so heavily on compulsion, and so unequally, is not working as it should.

**The do-nothing baseline.** Leave this and demand keeps rising and landing on the most expensive
parts of the system, on families, and on the police, while preventable distress becomes lasting
illness.

---

## Evidence base

- **Early intervention pays back.** Investing in early help and talking therapies reduces later,
  costlier crises and keeps people in work. *Transferable* as strong value for money.
- **Dedicated crisis services work better than cells and A&E.** Crisis houses, helplines, and
  mental-health response teams resolve crises more safely and cheaply. *Transferable* as the right
  model.
- **Mental and physical health are linked.** Treating them together improves both, which connects
  to the wider [NHS and public health](./nhs-and-public-health.md) chapter.

---

## Options appraisal

**Option 1: More of the same.** Keep the current model and hope funding catches up. *Pros:* no
change needed. *Cons:* waits and crises keep growing. The do-nothing baseline.

**Option 2: Crisis-focused only.** Pour resources into acute care alone. *Pros:* addresses the
sharpest end. *Cons:* without prevention, demand keeps rising to meet it.

**Option 3: Access, prevention, and proper crisis care *(lead option)*.** Widen access and cut
waits, invest in prevention and early help, and build dedicated crisis services. *Pros:* better
outcomes, lower long-run cost, less pressure on police and A&E. *Cons:* needs sustained investment
and workforce. **Recommended.**

---

## The proposals (under the lead option)

1. **Cut waits and widen access.** Expand talking therapies and specialist care, with the shortest
   possible waits for children and young people.
2. **Invest in prevention and early help.** Put mental health support into schools, workplaces, and
   communities, where early action prevents later crises.
3. **Move crisis care to the right settings.** Build crisis houses, helplines, and mental-health
   response teams so crises are not handled in police cells or A&E.
4. **Treat mind and body together.** Join up mental and physical health care, in line with the
   [NHS and public health](./nhs-and-public-health.md) chapter.
5. **Reform the Mental Health Act.** Modernise detention law to reduce coercion, give patients more
   say over their care, and tackle the racial disparity in detention and community treatment orders.
6. **Back severe mental illness, not just mild to moderate.** Fund community mental health for people
   with psychosis and other severe conditions, and proper inpatient capacity, so the system does not
   only serve the easiest cases. This includes joining up addiction and mental-health care for people
   with a dual diagnosis, in line with the [drugs-as-health approach](./nhs-and-public-health.md).
7. **Connect to the wider framework.** Link mental health support to work, housing, welfare, and
   justice, since these shape and are shaped by it.

---

## Costs and benefits

Prevention and early help are cheaper than the crises they avoid, and proper crisis services cost
less than police time and A&E. The return is people kept well, in work, and out of the most
expensive settings. The figures belong in the budget files and the health-savings
file.

---

## Delivery and timeline

- **Years 1-3:** expand access and cut waits; stand up crisis services; put support into schools.
- **Years 3-7:** scale prevention and early help; join up mental and physical care.
- **Years 7-10:** shorter waits, less crisis demand, and measurable gains in wellbeing and work.

---

## Risks and mitigations

- **Workforce shortages.** *Mitigation:* train and retain more staff, tied to the skills plan.
- **Prevention cut when budgets tighten.** *Mitigation:* protect early-help funding as the
  cost-saving investment it is.
- **Crisis services under-used or poorly sited.** *Mitigation:* design with local services and the
  police so the right door is always open.

## Consultation questions

1. What waiting-time guarantees should apply, especially for children and young people?
2. Where does early help give the best return: schools, workplaces, or communities?
3. What crisis services would best take pressure off the police and A&E?
4. How should mental health support join up with work, housing, and justice?
5. How do we reform the Mental Health Act to cut coercion and the racial disparity in detention?
