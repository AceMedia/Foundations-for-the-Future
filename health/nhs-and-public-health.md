# The NHS and Public Health

*A forward-looking proposal, judged by what works, not by left or right.*

> **Summary for the reader**
> - **The problem:** the NHS is a national treasure at breaking point. Waiting lists are around 7.4
>   million, A&E and delayed discharges are chronic, the workforce is short-staffed and burnt out, and
>   the system is built to treat illness rather than prevent it.
> - **The options:** more money in the same model, more privatisation, or prevention-led reform with
>   restored core capacity.
> - **Lead option:** **shift decisively toward prevention** (warm homes, nutrition, mental health,
>   drugs as a health issue, social-care integration) while restoring core capacity and the workforce.
> - **Timeline:** restore capacity and cut waits early; build prevention over a decade.
>
> *Figures here are first-draft and will be checked in the budget-accuracy pass (#21).*

## The problem

The NHS is both a national treasure and a system under enormous strain.

**Waits are too long.** The waiting list for hospital treatment in England stands at around 7.4
million, with people waiting months for care that gets more expensive the longer it is delayed.[^waits]

**The front door and back door are blocked.** A&E is overwhelmed and patients are stuck in hospital
because the social care to discharge them safely is not there, which is bad for patients and expensive
for the system, connecting to [adult social care](./adult-social-care.md).

**The workforce is short and burnt out.** Tens of thousands of vacancies across nursing and other
roles leave staff stretched, which harms care and retention.[^workforce]

**It treats illness rather than preventing it.** Too much of the system waits for people to become ill
rather than stopping them becoming ill in the first place, even though prevention is cheaper and
kinder.

**The do-nothing baseline.** Leave this and waits, workforce shortages, and demand all worsen, while
preventable illness keeps flowing into the most expensive parts of the system.

---

## Evidence base

- **Prevention pays.** Warm homes, good nutrition, and early mental-health support reduce demand far
  more cheaply than treating the resulting illness, the logic running through the
  [housing](../housing/housing-policy.md), [welfare](../welfare/welfare-and-wellbeing-reform.md), and
  [mental health](./mental-health.md) chapters. *Transferable* as the organising idea.
- **Treating drugs as health works.** Portugal's decriminalisation with strong public-health support
  reduced harm, crime, and cost, and cannabis is regulated and taxed across Canada and many US
  states.[^portugal] *Transferable* as the basis for a health-led drugs policy.
- **Integration cuts delayed discharge.** Joining up health and social care frees beds and improves
  outcomes.[^ltp] *Transferable* as the prize from getting the boundary right.

---

## Options appraisal

**Option 1: More money, same model.** Keep funding a treatment-led system. *Pros:* avoids reform.
*Cons:* demand keeps rising to absorb it, and prevention stays neglected. The do-nothing baseline.

**Option 2: More privatisation.** Outsource more care. *Pros:* claims efficiency. *Cons:* fragments
care and does not fix prevention or the workforce.

**Option 3: Prevention-led reform with restored capacity *(lead option)*.** Shift toward prevention
while restoring core capacity, the workforce, and social-care integration. *Pros:* lower long-run
demand, shorter waits, healthier population. *Cons:* needs investment now for returns over years.
**Recommended.**

---

## The proposals (under the lead option)

1. **Prevention first.** Treat warm homes, good nutrition, and early mental-health support as health
   policy, in step with [housing](../housing/housing-policy.md),
   [bulk food provision](../welfare/bulk-food-procurement.md), and [mental health](./mental-health.md),
   so fewer people become ill in the first place.
2. **Restore core capacity and cut waits.** Invest to bring the waiting list down and keep it down,
   with better use of diagnostics, community care, and technology.
3. **Fix the workforce.** Train, value, and retain staff, tied to the care-workforce ladder in
   [education and skills](../education/education-and-skills.md).
4. **Integrate with social care.** Join up health and care to end delayed discharges, in step with
   [adult social care](./adult-social-care.md).
5. **Treat drugs as a health issue.** Follow the Portugal model: health-led, with cannabis regulated
   and taxed, freeing police and NHS resources for care rather than policing lifestyle.[^portugal]
6. **Fund repair through regulated vice.** Ring-fence vice-tax receipts into a betterment fund for
   treatment, mental health, social care, end-of-life dignity, and community health, in step with
   [vice taxation](./vice-taxation-and-betterment.md).
7. **Well-being, not over-control.** Keep public health educating rather than controlling: strong
   advice, but fun and freedom are part of life, and moderate adult choices are not the government's
   business.
8. **Dignity at the end of life.** Publicly fund the dementia premium and palliative care so people are
   not forced to exhaust their savings, in step with
   [end-of-life and dementia care](./end-of-life-and-dementia-care.md).

## Costs and benefits

Prevention and integration cost money now but reduce the far larger downstream costs of avoidable
illness and delayed discharge, while regulated vice funds care directly. The detailed savings belong in
the budget-accuracy pass (#21) and the [health-savings file](../budget/health-savings.md).

---

## Delivery and timeline

- **Years 1-3:** invest to cut waits and rebuild the workforce; begin the prevention shift; legislate
  the health-led drugs approach and the betterment fund.
- **Years 3-7:** scale prevention and social-care integration; embed regulated vice funding.
- **Years 7-10:** shorter waits, a stable workforce, and a healthier population with lower avoidable
  demand.

---

## Risks and mitigations

- **Prevention cut when budgets tighten.** *Mitigation:* protect prevention funding as the
  cost-saving investment it is.
- **Workforce shortages persisting.** *Mitigation:* the care-workforce ladder and proper pay and
  training.
- **Drugs reform misread.** *Mitigation:* frame it clearly as health-led harm reduction, with
  regulation and tax, not a free-for-all.
