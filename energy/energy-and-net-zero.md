# Energy Security and Net Zero

*A forward-looking proposal, judged by what works, not by left or right.*

> **Summary for the reader**
> - **The problem:** Britain's energy is too expensive and too exposed to foreign shocks. The
>   2022 gas price spike showed how badly an import-dependent system hurts households and
>   industry, the grid cannot connect new power fast enough, and bills stay high.
> - **The options:** affordable net zero with nuclear and renewables, a renewables-led sprint,
>   security-and-cost-first with slower targets, or a nuclear-heavy baseload.
> - **Lead option:** **back new nuclear and renewables together, keep the net zero goal, and
>   make cutting bills and energy security the priority.**
> - **Timeline:** a rolling programme; grid and bills action in years 1-3, new capacity over a
>   decade and beyond.
>

## The problem

Energy sits underneath everything: the cost of living, the competitiveness of industry, and
our exposure to events abroad.

**Bills are too high and too volatile.** Because so much of our power and heat still depends on
gas, a global price spike feeds straight through to households and factories, as the 2022 energy
crisis showed.

**The grid is a bottleneck.** New wind, solar, and storage projects wait years to connect, so
clean power that could be cutting bills sits stranded. The network, not the willingness to build,
is now the constraint.

**Net zero is law, but the path is contested.** The 2050 target is legally binding,[^netzero] yet
the route to it is argued over, and people worry, reasonably, about who pays and whether the
lights stay on.

**The do-nothing baseline.** Leave this and Britain stays hostage to volatile gas prices, misses
its targets, loses industry to cheaper-energy countries, and faces repeated bill crises.

---

## Evidence base

- **France's nuclear baseload** gives it relatively low-carbon, stable electricity. *Transferable*
  in the case for firm low-carbon power, adapted to British costs and timescales.
- **Offshore wind is a British strength.** The North Sea has made the UK a leader in offshore
  wind, which is now among the cheapest new power. *Directly transferable*, the question is how
  fast we can connect it.
- **Small modular reactors** offer a faster, more standardised route to new nuclear than giant
  one-off plants. *Promising but to be proven*, which argues for backing them while not betting
  everything on them.

---

## Options appraisal

**Option 1: Affordable net zero, nuclear and renewables *(lead option)*.** Build firm low-carbon
power (new nuclear, including small modular reactors) alongside renewables and storage, fix the
grid, and judge the pace by bills and security. *Pros:* secure, lower-carbon, and bill-focused.
*Cons:* nuclear is slow and costly to build, so renewables and the grid must carry the near term.
**Recommended.**

**Option 2: Renewables-led sprint.** Go all-out on renewables and the grid. *Pros:* fast,
cheap power. *Cons:* needs firm backup for windless, dark periods, which is where nuclear and
storage come in.

**Option 3: Security and cost first, ease the targets.** Prioritise cheap, secure energy now and
relax deadlines. *Pros:* short-term bill relief. *Cons:* locks in gas dependence and the
volatility that caused the crisis.

**Option 4: Nuclear-heavy baseload.** Make new nuclear the backbone. *Pros:* firm low-carbon
power. *Cons:* too slow and expensive to rely on alone for the next decade.

---

## The proposals (under the lead option)

1. **Fix the grid first.** Treat grid connections and storage as the priority, so clean power
   already built or planned can actually reach homes and businesses and start cutting bills.
2. **Back new nuclear, including SMRs.** Commit to a steady nuclear programme for firm low-carbon
   power, backing small modular reactors as a faster, more standardised route alongside larger
   plants.
3. **Build out renewables.** Keep expanding offshore wind and solar, where Britain has a real
   advantage and the cheapest new power.
4. **Cut bills and exposure.** Reduce dependence on volatile gas, and pair the transition with
   warm-homes retrofit (see [housing](../housing/housing-policy.md)) so demand falls and bills
   come down.
5. **Keep the goal, judge the pace by bills and security.** Hold to net zero, but measure progress
   by affordability and reliability, not slogans, so the public stays with it.

## Costs and benefits

Up-front investment in the grid, nuclear, and renewables is large, but it buys lower, steadier
bills, energy security, industrial competitiveness, and lower emissions. The detailed costings,
including how investment is financed, belong in the budget-accuracy pass (#21) and a matching
budget file.

## Delivery and timeline

- **Years 1-3:** clear the grid-connection queue and back storage; commit the nuclear programme;
  keep building renewables; expand retrofit.
- **Years 3-10:** new nuclear and SMRs come online; renewables and grid scale; gas dependence
  falls.
- **Beyond:** firm, low-carbon, affordable supply as the backbone of the economy.

---

## Risks and mitigations

- **Nuclear delay and cost overruns.** *Mitigation:* standardise on SMRs, do not rely on nuclear
  alone in the near term, and lean on renewables plus storage.
- **Grid build-out resistance.** *Mitigation:* fair community benefit and faster, clearer planning.
- **Bills staying high through the transition.** *Mitigation:* prioritise the cheapest measures
  (grid, renewables, retrofit) and reduce gas exposure.
- **Public support fraying.** *Mitigation:* judge the pace by affordability and security, and be
  honest about trade-offs.
