# Childcare, Early Years and Families

*A forward-looking proposal, judged by what works, not by left or right.*

> **Summary for the reader**
> - **The problem:** childcare in Britain is among the most expensive in the developed world,
>   which keeps parents out of work, the early years are underfunded, and the SEND system is
>   overwhelmed.
> - **The options:** subsidise demand, target help at those who need it most, or provide childcare
>   directly.
> - **Lead option:** **direct public and community childcare provision**, in line with the
>   framework's direct-provision approach, alongside strong early years and a fixed SEND system.
> - **Timeline:** build capacity over a parliament; fix SEND urgently.
>
> *Costings are indicative; the detailed figures live in the budget files.*

---

## The problem

The early years shape everything that follows, and the system around them is failing families.

**Childcare costs too much.** Britain has among the most expensive childcare in the developed world,
third only to Switzerland and New Zealand for average earners and the most expensive of all for
lower-income families. It swallows around a quarter of household income, with a full-time nursery
place for an under-two now averaging over £305 a week, against about 7% of income in France.[^childcare]
This keeps parents, mostly mothers, out of work, and the free-hours offer helps only on paper where
there are no places and where the funding rate sits below what provision actually costs, closing
nurseries.

**The early years are underfunded.** The period that most shapes a child's life chances gets too
little investment, storing up problems for schools and services later.

**SEND is overwhelmed.** The number of children with an Education, Health and Care Plan has more than
doubled since 2015 to around 576,000, and councils' high-needs deficits are heading past £5 billion,
which is failing families and driving councils toward bankruptcy, as set out in
[local government](../governance/local-government.md).[^send]

**The do-nothing baseline.** Leave this and parental potential is wasted, child poverty persists,
the SEND crisis deepens, and councils sink further.

---

## Evidence base

- **Direct provision delivers where subsidy alone fails.** Subsidies are worth little if there are
  no places. Publicly and community-provided childcare guarantees supply, which is the framework's
  general approach in [welfare reform](../welfare/welfare-and-wellbeing-reform.md). *Transferable*,
  with quality safeguards.
- **Early years investment pays back.** Good early education improves life chances and reduces
  later costs. *Transferable* as strong long-term value.
- **Childcare unlocks work.** Affordable, available childcare lets parents work, raising incomes
  and the tax base. *Transferable* as plain economics.

---

## Options appraisal

**Option 1: Subsidise demand.** Pay parents or providers more. *Pros:* simple. *Cons:* in a market
short of places, subsidy can inflate prices without creating supply.

**Option 2: Target those who need it most.** Focus help on lower-income families. *Pros:* fair and
cheaper. *Cons:* leaves the wider work-and-cost problem unsolved.

**Option 3: Direct public and community provision *(lead option)*.** Guarantee childcare places
through public and community provision, with strong early years and a fixed SEND system. *Pros:*
guarantees supply, lets parents work, invests in children, and fits the framework's direct-provision
approach. *Cons:* needs capacity-building and quality assurance. **Recommended.**

---

## The proposals (under the lead option)

1. **Provide childcare directly, and fix the funded rate.** Build public and community childcare so
   places are guaranteed, not just subsidised on paper, and set the funded-hours rate at the real cost
   of provision so nurseries stop closing. Guaranteed places let parents work and give children a
   strong start.
2. **Invest in the early years.** Fund high-quality early education as the highest-return
   investment in the whole system.
3. **Reform SEND properly.** Fund mainstream schools to include more children with additional needs
   well, so an Education, Health and Care Plan is not the only route to support; simplify the plan
   process; and put the high-needs block on a sustainable footing, so the system serves children and
   stops bankrupting councils, in step with [local government](../governance/local-government.md).
4. **Support families and tackle child poverty.** Join up childcare with welfare, housing, and
   health so families get coherent support.
5. **Quality and workforce.** Train and value the early-years workforce so provision is good as
   well as available.

---

## Costs and benefits

Direct provision costs more up front than thin subsidies, but it guarantees the places that let
parents work, raises the tax base, and improves children's life chances, while a fixed SEND system
relieves council budgets. Figures belong in the budget files.

---

## Delivery and timeline

- **Years 1-2:** start building public and community childcare capacity; begin SEND reform.
- **Years 2-5:** scale provision and early-years investment; embed SEND reform.
- **Years 5-10:** guaranteed, affordable childcare and a working SEND system.

---

## Risks and mitigations

- **Capacity taking time to build.** *Mitigation:* phase the rollout and use community provision to
  scale.
- **Quality slipping under expansion.** *Mitigation:* train and value the workforce, with clear
  standards.
- **SEND reform stalling.** *Mitigation:* treat it as urgent, given the harm to families and
  council finances.

## Consultation questions

3. How should the SEND system be reformed to serve children and stop bankrupting councils?
4. How do we recruit and value the early-years workforce?
5. What should the funded-hours rate be, and how do we keep nurseries open?
