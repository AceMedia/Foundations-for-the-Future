# Housing: Secure, Affordable, and Sustainable Homes for All

*A forward-looking proposal, judged by what works, not by left or right.*

> **Summary for the reader**
> - **The problem:** Britain spends almost **£30bn a year** propping up rents through
>   housing support[^hb-spend] and a further **£2.3bn a year** on temporary accommodation[^ta-cost],
>   while building far too few homes[^supply] and leaving 2.6 million households in poor-quality
>   stock that costs the NHS **£1.4bn a year**[^bre-nhs]. The money treats the symptoms, not the cause.
> - **The options:** (1) optimise the current rent-subsidy model; (2) expand demand-side
>   support; (3) **a supply-led public housebuilding and retrofit programme** *(lead option)*;
>   (4) planning liberalisation alone.
> - **Indicative impact:** redirecting the *growth* in housing support into capital, plus
>   ending hotel/B&B placements, could save **£1–2bn a year** within a decade while building a
>   permanent public asset. See [budget/housing-savings.md](../budget/housing-savings.md).
> - **Timeline:** 10-year programme; pilots and capital reallocation in years 1–3.

---

## The problem

Housing is not just shelter; it is the foundation of stability, family life, work, and
health. Britain's housing system is failing on four fronts at once, and each failure feeds
the others.

**Supply falls short, year after year.** England added 221,070 net dwellings in 2023–24
and 208,600 in 2024–25, well below the ~300,000 a year long held to be needed and the
government's own 1.5-million-over-a-parliament ambition.[^supply] Chronic under-supply is
the engine of unaffordability.

**Homes cost far more than people earn.** In 2024 the median home in England cost **7.7
times** median full-time earnings; in London the ratio reached **10.6**, against **5.0** in
the North East.[^affordability] A generation is locked out of ownership and pushed into a
private rented sector with, until recently, little security.

**Quality is poor and it lands on the NHS.** Around **2.6 million homes (11% of stock)**
contain serious hazards; BRE estimates this costs the NHS about **£1.4bn a year** in
first-year treatment costs alone (£857m of it from cold homes) and up to **£18.5bn a year**
to wider society.[^bre-nhs] The Grenfell tragedy exposed how far safety regulation had been
allowed to fail.

**The taxpayer pays for the failure, not the fix.** Government spends almost **£30bn a year**
on Housing Benefit and the Universal Credit housing element[^hb-spend], much of it flowing
to private landlords for housing the state does not own, and a further **£2.3bn a year** on
temporary accommodation, a bill that rose 29% in a single year and roughly doubled in five.[^ta-cost]

**The do-nothing baseline.** On current trends, none of these costs falls. Housing support
and temporary-accommodation bills keep rising with rents and homelessness; the maintenance
backlog and its health costs compound; and the state accumulates no asset in return for the
spending. Inaction is not a saving. It is an escalating liability.

## Evidence base

**What works abroad, with a transferability note for each:**

- **Vienna (Austria):** sustained municipal investment means over 60% of residents live in
  social or subsidised housing, with stable rents and high quality, while the city ranks
  among the world's most liveable.[^vienna] *Transferable* in its mixed-income, long-term
  stewardship model; less directly in its century-old land holdings, which Britain would
  have to rebuild over time.
- **Singapore:** more than 80% of residents live in well-maintained, government-built flats,
  financed through compulsory savings.[^singapore] *Partly transferable*: the build quality
  and ownership ladder, less so the centralised land and savings system.
- **Germany:** long tenancies and tenant protections give families security that the UK's
  short tenancies historically denied; Berlin's rent cap was later struck down by the courts,
  a caution on heavy-handed price control.[^germany] *Transferable* in tenancy security;
  a warning on blunt rent caps.
- **Canada:** refugees are often resettled in smaller towns with structured support, aiding
  integration and using spare capacity.[^canada] *Transferable* to how Britain houses new
  arrivals (see [immigration/immigration.md](../immigration/immigration.md)).

**What has already changed at home.** The **Renters' Rights Act 2025** (Royal Assent
October 2025; main provisions in force from 1 May 2026) abolished Section 21 "no-fault"
evictions and assured shorthold tenancies, converting tenancies to periodic assured ones
with reformed possession grounds.[^rra] Tenant security is therefore now largely a question
of *implementation*, and this Green Paper builds on it rather than re-proposing it.

## Options appraisal

Four broad strategies are on the table. They are not all mutually exclusive, but they imply
very different uses of public money.

**Option 1: Optimise the current rent-subsidy model.**
Keep housing support as the main lever, tweak planning, and rely on private developers to
raise output. *Pros:* low disruption, no upfront capital. *Cons:* costs keep rising, no
public asset is created, supply and quality problems persist. This is effectively the
do-nothing baseline.

**Option 2: Expand demand-side support.**
Boost subsidies to buyers and renters (Help-to-Buy-style schemes, higher allowances).
*Pros:* politically straightforward, quick to announce. *Cons:* in a supply-constrained
market this largely inflates prices and rents, subsidising demand without adding homes
makes affordability worse. **Not recommended.**

**Option 3: Supply-led public housebuilding and retrofit *(lead option)*.**
Treat the *growth* in housing support as misdirected capital: progressively redirect it,
plus the temporary-accommodation bill, into building and acquiring mixed-tenure social
housing and retrofitting existing stock. *Pros:* builds a permanent public asset, cuts
long-run subsidy and NHS costs, improves security and quality, supports construction jobs
and regional renewal. *Cons:* significant upfront capital and delivery capacity needed;
benefits accrue over a decade, not a parliament. **Recommended as the spine of policy.**

**Option 4: Planning liberalisation alone.**
Deregulate planning sharply and leave delivery to the market. *Pros:* could raise starts and
is low-cost to the Exchequer. *Cons:* on its own it risks poor quality, weak affordability,
and a repeat of safety failures; market output alone has never hit the affordability target.
*Best used as a component* of Option 3, not a substitute for it.

**Lead approach:** Option 3 as the spine, taking the planning reforms of Option 4 to speed
delivery, and building on the tenant security already delivered by the Renters' Rights Act.

## The proposals (under the lead option)

1. **Build the right homes, beautifully.** Fund councils and housing associations to deliver
   mixed-tenure, mixed-income communities on the Vienna model, avoiding concentrated
   deprivation. Require high standards of safety, insulation, and design: attractive,
   mid-rise (5–8 storey) development rather than either sprawl or grim towers.[^riba]
2. **Retrofit the existing stock.** Britain has some of Europe's oldest, leakiest homes.
   A national retrofit programme (insulation, glazing, modern heating) would cut bills by
   several hundred pounds a year per home, reduce the £1.4bn NHS cost of cold housing, and
   create steady regional work.[^bre-nhs][^ifg] Procure at scale alongside schools, hospitals,
   and prisons to drive down unit costs.
3. **Make the new tenancy regime work.** With Section 21 abolished,[^rra] focus on
   *implementation*: a functioning possession route through the courts, a fair-rent dispute
   mechanism, and enforcement of standards so security does not come at the cost of supply.
4. **Reform finance and ownership.** Prioritise first-time UK buyers in new developments,
   curb speculative bulk purchases, and expand shared-equity and cooperative models so people
   can build a stake without taking on unmanageable debt.
5. **Use public land productively.** Develop surplus public land for affordable homes rather
   than selling it cheaply; give local authorities the funds and autonomy to build for local
   need.
6. **House new arrivals fairly.** Allocate on the basis of genuine spare capacity, not at
   the expense of citizens already in need, using under-used stock in towns that want
   regeneration, with refurbishment and integration support, as Canada does.[^canada]

## Costs and benefits

Detailed costings, baselines, and scenarios are in
[budget/housing-savings.md](../budget/housing-savings.md). In summary (all figures indicative):

| Mechanism | Baseline pressure | Indicative annual saving |
|---|---|---|
| End hotel/B&B temporary accommodation | £2.3bn/yr and rising[^ta-cost] | £0.6–1.0bn |
| Redirect housing-support *growth* into capital | ~£30bn/yr support bill[^hb-spend] | £1.0–2.0bn (long run) |
| Prevent future safety/cladding bills | £5bn+ historic remediation | £0.3–0.6bn (avoided) |
| Healthier homes → lower NHS demand | £1.4bn/yr NHS cost[^bre-nhs] | £0.3–0.6bn |

The decisive point is **asset versus subsidy**: today's spending rents housing the state
does not own; the lead option converts a rising revenue cost into a permanent public asset
that keeps paying back through lower rents, lower NHS demand, and stronger local economies.

## Delivery and timeline

- **Years 1–3:** legislate the capital-reallocation mechanism; launch council/housing-
  association building pilots and a first retrofit wave; bed in the new tenancy regime and
  possession process; assemble public-land sites.
- **Years 3–7:** scale building and retrofit; shift a growing share of housing-support
  *growth* into capital; stand up regional delivery capacity and supply chains.
- **Years 7–10:** steady-state output of mixed-tenure homes; measurable falls in temporary-
  accommodation use, housing-support leakage, and cold-home NHS costs.

Lead delivery sits with local authorities and housing associations, backed by national
capital and standards. Dependencies and sequencing are set out in
[implementation/phasing-and-delivery.md](../implementation/phasing-and-delivery.md).

## Risks and mitigations

- **Construction capacity and skills shortages.** *Mitigation:* tie the programme to the
  skills and apprenticeship plan ([education/education-and-skills.md](../education/education-and-skills.md))
  and to justice work-pathways ([justice/rehabilitation-through-public-service.md](../justice/rehabilitation-through-public-service.md)); phase demand to build supply chains.
- **Upfront capital cost before savings land.** *Mitigation:* phase reallocation from
  housing-support growth; prioritise the highest-return measures (ending B&B placements,
  cold-home retrofit) first.
- **Local delivery variability.** *Mitigation:* national standards and capital with local
  autonomy; share models from high-performing authorities.
- **Tenant-security reforms reducing rental supply.** *Mitigation:* a fast, fair possession
  route for genuine grounds; monitor PRS supply and adjust.
- **Quality and safety regression under speed pressure.** *Mitigation:* non-negotiable safety
  and design standards, independent inspection, and real developer liability.

---

## Consultation questions

1. Is redirecting the *growth* in housing support into capital the right primary mechanism,
   or should reallocation go further or slower?
2. What is the right tenure mix for new public housing, and how should "mixed-income" be
   defined and protected over time?
3. Where should the first retrofit waves be targeted: by health impact, by region, or by
   carbon?
4. How should public land be valued and released to maximise affordable output without
   undercutting quality?
5. What safeguards best balance the new tenant protections with maintaining rental supply?
