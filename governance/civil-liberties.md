# Civil Liberties, Equalities and Human Rights

*A forward-looking proposal, judged by what works, not by left or right.*

> **Summary for the reader**
> - **The problem:** the balance between security, free speech, and privacy is struck case by
>   case rather than on principle, equalities law is contested, and the human rights framework is
>   politically charged.
> - **The options:** leave the ECHR, keep everything as is, or stay in the Convention and reform
>   how rights apply at home.
> - **Lead option:** **stay in the ECHR and reform the Human Rights Act**, protect free speech by
>   keeping insults civil rather than criminal, rule out a national digital ID, and lead on online
>   safety through parents and devices rather than mandatory checks.
> - **Timeline:** legislate the reforms over a parliament; set the principles now.

---

## The problem

A free country has to keep getting the same balance right: protect people from harm without
hollowing out the freedoms that make it worth living in.

**Free speech is being chilled.** Too much offence and insult is treated as a matter for the
police rather than for civil remedies that already exist. Criminalising upsetting language
weakens trust in the law and distracts from real threats.

**Surveillance and data use creep.** The state and large platforms gather ever more data, often
without clear limits, and the convenience of digital services is used to justify schemes that
catalogue people.

**The rights framework is fought over.** The European Convention on Human Rights and the Human
Rights Act are treated as all-or-nothing, when the real question is how rights are balanced and
applied in domestic law.

**The do-nothing baseline.** Leave this and the line between security and liberty keeps being
drawn by accident, speech stays chilled, data gathering grows, and the rights debate stays stuck
in a shouting match.

---

## Evidence base

- **Civil remedies for speech already exist.** Defamation and harassment law give real redress
  without criminalising insult. *Transferable* as the right home for most disputes about speech.
- **Privacy can be designed in.** Online safety and service delivery can be achieved with data
  minimisation and device-level controls rather than central databases, as set out in
  [governance](./democracy-and-reform.md). *Transferable* as a design principle.
- **Most democracies stay in international rights frameworks and still legislate freely.** ECHR
  membership and a sensible domestic settlement are not contradictory. *Transferable* as the
  basis for reform rather than exit.

---

## Options appraisal

**Option 1: Leave the ECHR.** Withdraw and write a purely domestic bill of rights. *Pros:* full
domestic control. *Cons:* a major rupture with allies and the peace settlement, for gains that can
mostly be had through reform. Not the lead here.

**Option 2: Keep everything as is.** *Pros:* no disruption. *Cons:* leaves the speech, privacy,
and balance problems unaddressed. The do-nothing baseline.

**Option 3: Stay in the ECHR and reform the Human Rights Act *(lead option)*.** Remain a member of
the Convention, but rebalance how rights are weighed and applied in domestic law, while protecting
speech and privacy directly. *Pros:* keeps Britain's word to its allies and neighbours while
fixing the domestic problems. *Cons:* requires careful drafting to get the balance right.
**Recommended.**

---

## The proposals (under the lead option)

1. **Stay in the ECHR, reform the Human Rights Act.** Remain in the Convention, but reform how
   rights are balanced at home, so courts and Parliament have clearer guidance and the system
   commands public confidence.
2. **Protect free speech.** Keep insults and offence as civil matters, not criminal ones, relying
   on the defamation and harassment law that already exists. Free speech is the cornerstone of a
   democracy, and the state should be slow to criminalise words.
3. **No national digital ID.** Rule out a central identity register. People should never need a
   single government identity to live their lives, in line with the
   [governance chapter](./democracy-and-reform.md).
4. **Online safety through parents and devices.** Lead on protecting children through parental
   controls and the tools built into operating systems, as Apple's 2026 child-safety features
   show,[^apple] rather than mandatory age or identity verification that catalogues everyone. This
   connects to [education and skills](../education/education-and-skills.md).
5. **Clear, fair equalities law.** Apply equalities law plainly and even-handedly, so it protects
   people without becoming a source of confusion or resentment.

---

## Costs and benefits

This is mostly about principle and the quality of law rather than spending. The benefit is a
country that is both safe and free: speech protected, privacy respected, rights settled on a basis
people accept, and no costly central identity system to build and secure.

---

## Delivery and timeline

- **Years 1-2:** set the principles; legislate Human Rights Act reform and the speech and privacy
  protections; rule out a national digital ID in law.
- **Years 2-5:** embed the online-safety approach with platforms and device makers; review
  equalities law for clarity.
- **Years 5-10:** a settled framework that balances liberty and security on principle.

---

## Risks and mitigations

- **Reform read as weakening rights.** *Mitigation:* stay in the ECHR and be clear the aim is a
  fairer balance, not fewer protections.
- **Speech protections shielding genuine threats.** *Mitigation:* keep criminal law for real
  threats and harassment, civil law for offence.
- **Pressure for mandatory age or identity checks.** *Mitigation:* device-level controls and
  parental tools instead, with no central register.

---

## Consultation questions

1. How should the Human Rights Act be reformed while staying in the ECHR?
2. Where exactly should the line sit between criminal threats and civil matters of speech?
3. What limits and safeguards should apply to state and platform data use?
4. How do we protect children online without a digital nanny state or mandatory identity checks?
