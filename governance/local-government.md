# Local Government and Public Service Funding

*A forward-looking proposal, judged by what works, not by left or right.*

> **Summary for the reader**
> - **The problem:** councils have been cut hard and loaded with rising demand. Core funding
>   is about 9% lower in real terms than in 2010, and 18% lower per person.[^ifs-funding] More
>   councils issued effective-bankruptcy notices in 2023 than in the previous thirty years
>   combined.[^s114] Social care, children's services, special educational needs, and
>   homelessness are overwhelming budgets.[^lga-pressure]
> - **The options:** keep firefighting with one-off bailouts, or fix the funding model and
>   the demand drivers and devolve real power.
> - **Lead option:** multi-year funding tied to need, action on the cost drivers, council tax
>   and business rates reform, and Greater Manchester-style devolution extended across England.
> - **Timeline:** stabilise in years 1-2; reform the model over a parliament.

---

## The problem

Local government runs the services people touch every day: social care, bin collections,
roads, libraries, planning, child protection, homelessness. It has been quietly pushed to
the edge.

**The money was cut, deeply.** Councils' core funding is around 9% lower in real terms than
in 2010, and about 18% lower per person, after central government grants were cut by roughly
40% over the 2010s. The cuts fell hardest on the most deprived areas, which lost the most per
person.[^ifs-funding]

**Councils are failing financially.** A section 114 notice is the nearest a council comes to
declaring itself bankrupt. There were more of them in 2023 than in the previous thirty years
put together, with Birmingham, Nottingham, Woking, and Thurrock among those affected.[^s114]

**Demand is swamping budgets.** Between 2022/23 and 2024/25, councils overspent on average by
5.2% on adult social care, 14.2% on children's social care, 25.1% on home-to-school transport
for children with special educational needs, and 51.9% on homelessness, while a £5bn
high-needs deficit hangs over the system.[^lga-pressure] A handful of statutory services now
consume the budget, and everything else gets cut to pay for them.

**The do-nothing baseline.** Carry on and more councils fail, the services that are not legally
required (parks, libraries, youth provision, road maintenance) keep disappearing, and the
state nearest to people becomes the one that visibly stops working.

---

## Evidence base

- **Greater Manchester proves devolution works.** A decade of real devolution, with an elected
  mayor and genuine control over transport, skills, and investment, helped Manchester grow at
  roughly double the UK rate and pass £100 billion in output.[^gm-localgov] Where power and
  funding are real, results follow. This is the model to extend, set out in the
  [constitution and devolution chapter](./constitution-and-devolution.md).
- **The demand drivers are shared.** Adult social care, children's services, SEND, and
  homelessness are national pressures landing on local budgets. They cannot be fixed council
  by council; they need the reforms in the [adult social care](../health/end-of-life-and-dementia-care.md)
  and [housing](../housing/housing-policy.md) chapters.

---

## Options appraisal

**Option 1: Keep firefighting.** Carry on with annual settlements and one-off bailouts for
councils that hit the wall. *Pros:* avoids hard reform. *Cons:* more failures, more emergency
spending, no stability. The do-nothing baseline.

**Option 2: Bail out and centralise.** Rescue failing councils but pull more control back to
Whitehall. *Pros:* short-term grip. *Cons:* removes local accountability and does nothing
about the demand pressures that caused the failures.

**Option 3: Fix the model and devolve *(lead option)*.** Give councils multi-year funding
matched to need, tackle the national cost drivers at source, reform council tax and business
rates, and extend genuine devolution. *Pros:* stability, fairer distribution, better services,
real local accountability. *Cons:* needs reform of taxes that have been left alone for
decades, and a serious grip on social care and homelessness costs. **Recommended.**

---

## The proposals (under the lead option)

1. **Multi-year funding tied to need.** Replace stop-start annual settlements with multi-year
   funding distributed by an up-to-date measure of need and deprivation, so councils can plan
   and the worst-hit areas are not punished twice.
2. **Tackle the demand drivers at source.** The fastest way to save council budgets is to fix
   what is breaking them: a sustainable settlement for adult social care, reform of the SEND
   system, and ending the use of expensive temporary accommodation through the
   [housing programme](../housing/housing-policy.md).
3. **Reform council tax and business rates.** Council tax still rests on 1991 property values
   and business rates punish the high street. Both need replacing, in step with the
   [tax and fiscal reform chapter](../economy/entrepreneurship-and-renewal.md), so local
   funding is fair and stable.
4. **Extend real devolution.** Take the Greater Manchester model to every part of England that
   wants it: real powers and funding, not delegated administration, with the option of an
   elected mayor and combined authority.
5. **Clear central-local responsibilities.** Set out plainly what national government funds and
   what local government delivers, so accountability is visible and duplication is removed.

---

## Costs and benefits

Stabilising councils costs money up front, but it is cheaper than repeated emergency bailouts
and the hidden costs of failed services. Fixing the demand drivers, especially homelessness
and social care, is where the large savings sit, and those are detailed in the housing and
care chapters and the budget files. Council tax and business rates reform is about fairness
and stability rather than raising the overall take.

---

## Delivery and timeline

- **Years 1-2:** move to multi-year settlements; stabilise councils at risk; begin the
  council tax and business rates review.
- **Years 2-5:** implement tax reform; roll out devolution deals; deliver the social care,
  SEND, and homelessness reforms that relieve budgets.
- **Years 5-10:** stable, well-funded local government with clear accountability and restored
  non-statutory services.

---

## Risks and mitigations

- **Tax reform backlash.** *Mitigation:* phase changes, protect those on low incomes, and be
  clear it is about fairness, not a bigger overall bill.
- **Demand reforms slipping.** *Mitigation:* treat social care, SEND, and homelessness reform
  as the core of this chapter, not optional extras, since they drive the costs.
- **Devolution without capacity.** *Mitigation:* build local capacity and share models from
  high-performing authorities.
- **Deprived areas losing out again.** *Mitigation:* distribute funding by an honest measure of
  need, reviewed regularly.

---

## Consultation questions

1. What measure of need should drive the funding formula, and how often should it be updated?
2. What should replace council tax and business rates?
3. Should every area be offered a mayor and combined authority, or only those that want one?
4. How do we stop the statutory demand pressures from crowding out everything else councils do?
