# Public Participation: A Listening State

*A forward-looking proposal, judged by what works, not by left or right.*

> **Summary for the reader**
> - **The problem:** democracy has shrunk to voting for a party once every few years. Between
>   elections people are rarely heard, and when they are, through polls, petitions, and
>   consultations, the result is too often ignored, even when a clear majority, not a minority,
>   wants something different. That breeds the feeling that no one is listening.
> - **The options:** keep consultation as a box-ticking exercise, or build genuine, ongoing
>   public participation that actually feeds into decisions.
> - **Lead option:** structured participation that is taken seriously: citizens' assemblies on
>   the big questions, a petitions and ideas system with guaranteed responses, and a duty on
>   government to show how public feedback changed a decision, or to explain honestly why it
>   did not. All of it open to everyone, with **no digital ID required to take part**.
> - **Timeline:** start small and prove it; build the duty to respond into law over a parliament.

---

## The problem

Voting for a party every few years is a blunt instrument. It bundles hundreds of issues into
one cross on a ballot, and then asks people to wait years before they are consulted again. In
between, the main ways people try to be heard, petitions, consultations, opinion polling, are
frequently run and then set aside.

The damage is not that government sometimes disagrees with the public. It is that government
often does not appear to listen at all. When a settled majority opposes a change and it
happens anyway, with no real explanation, people reasonably conclude their views do not count.
These are not fringe complaints from small groups; they are often the majority view. Over time
that erodes the basic trust a democracy runs on.

People have a right to share their opinions and to expect those opinions to be weighed. A
healthy state treats that as a strength, not a nuisance.

**The do-nothing baseline.** Leave this and disillusionment deepens. Turnout drifts, trust
falls, and politics polarises, because the ordinary channels for being heard feel pointless.

---

## Evidence base

- **Citizens' assemblies work on hard questions.** Ireland used a Citizens' Assembly of 99
  randomly selected people to deliberate on abortion over five months in 2016-17. After hearing
  evidence from all sides, 87% favoured change, which led to the 2018 referendum in which 66.4%
  voted to reform the law.[^ireland-ca] A representative group, given time and good information,
  reached a considered view that the wider public then endorsed. *Transferable* as a way to
  handle contentious issues with genuine public buy-in.
- **Participatory budgeting and open petitions** in many countries show people will engage
  seriously when their input actually decides something. The key is that participation has to
  be real, not decorative.

## Options appraisal

**Option 1: Consultation as theatre.** Keep running consultations and petitions with no duty
to act or even to respond properly. *Pros:* costs nothing to change. *Cons:* deepens the sense
that no one listens. The do-nothing baseline.

**Option 2: More referendums.** Decide more by direct national vote. *Pros:* clear public
mandate. *Cons:* blunt for complex issues, easily reduced to slogans, and exhausting if
overused.

**Option 3: Structured, ongoing participation *(lead option)*.** Use deliberation, petitions,
and open idea-gathering as a standing part of policymaking, with a duty on government to show
how it responded. *Pros:* better decisions, restored trust, and a route for people to
contribute between elections. *Cons:* needs genuine commitment so it does not become more
theatre. **Recommended.**

---

## The proposals (under the lead option)

1. **Citizens' assemblies on the big questions.** Use representative, randomly selected
   assemblies, on the Irish model, to work through contentious or long-term issues, with their
   findings published and formally answered by government.
2. **A petitions and ideas system that gets a real response.** Anyone can raise an idea or
   challenge a policy. Proposals that reach a threshold of support get a substantive,
   published government response and, where relevant, a debate. No idea is quietly buried.
3. **A duty to show your working.** When government makes a decision that runs against a clear
   weight of public feedback, it must publish its reasons. Disagreeing with the public is
   allowed; ignoring them without explanation is not.
4. **Low barriers, no digital ID.** Taking part must be open to everyone, online or offline,
   without signing up to a national digital identity. Participation is a right, not a gated
   service.
5. **This document practises what it preaches.** *Foundations for the Future* is itself open
   to public contribution. Anyone can submit an idea, challenge a figure, or give feedback
   through the project's issues, and each chapter ends with consultation questions. The point
   is to take ideas beyond party lines and let people genuinely shape them.

---

## Costs and benefits

Participation is cheap relative to its value. Citizens' assemblies and a working petitions
system cost little against the price of decisions that lack public consent and have to be
unpicked later. The real return is trust: a state people believe is listening is easier to
govern and more stable.

---

## Delivery and timeline

- **Years 1-2:** pilot citizens' assemblies on a small number of big questions; upgrade the
  petitions system with guaranteed responses.
- **Years 2-4:** legislate the duty to respond and to show reasons; embed participation in
  how departments make policy.
- **Years 4-10:** routine, trusted public participation as a normal part of government.

---

## Risks and mitigations

- **Participation as theatre.** *Mitigation:* a legal duty to respond and to explain, so input
  cannot be ignored without cost.
- **Capture by organised lobbies.** *Mitigation:* random selection for assemblies and clear
  rules on transparency.
- **Pressure for mandatory ID to take part.** *Mitigation:* keep participation open and
  low-barrier by design, with no digital ID requirement, in line with
  [governance](./democracy-and-reform.md).
- **Overload.** *Mitigation:* focus deliberation on the questions that most need it, and use
  thresholds for petitions.

---

## Consultation questions

1. Which kinds of decision should go to a citizens' assembly?
2. What support threshold should trigger a guaranteed government response to a petition or idea?
3. How strong should the duty to explain decisions that go against public feedback be?
4. How do we keep participation open to everyone without a digital ID, online and offline?
