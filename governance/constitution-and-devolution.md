# The Constitution, Devolution and the Union

*A forward-looking proposal, judged by what works, not by left or right.*

> **Summary for the reader**
> - **The problem:** power is hyper-centralised in Westminster. The House of Lords has around
>   800 unelected members, the second largest legislature in the world.[^lords-size] The
>   voting system produced the most disproportionate result in British history in 2024, when
>   Labour won 63% of seats on 34% of the vote.[^election-2024] Devolution is uneven and the
>   Union is strained.
> - **The options:** tidy the present settlement piece by piece, or take a coherent package
>   of Lords reform, a fairer voting system, deeper devolution, and a permanent northern base
>   for government.
> - **Lead positions:** a largely elected, much smaller second chamber; a more proportional
>   voting system put to the public; deep devolution on the Greater Manchester model; and a
>   standing second base for Parliament and government in the North.
> - **Timeline:** a parliament to legislate, a decade to embed.

---

## The problem

Britain's constitution has been patched rather than designed, and the joins are showing.

**Power sits in one place.** Decisions that shape the whole country are made in one expensive
part of London, by people who rarely live with the results elsewhere. England is one of the
most centralised countries in the developed world.

**The second chamber is unelected and bloated.** The House of Lords has around 800 members,
almost all appointed, making it the second largest legislative chamber in the world after
China's National People's Congress. The nearest comparable upper house, the French Senate,
is less than half its size.[^lords-size] A house of patronage that outnumbers the elected
Commons is hard to defend on any modern principle.

**The voting system is breaking down.** In 2024 the system delivered its most distorted
result ever. Labour took 411 seats, 63% of the Commons, on about 34% of the vote, the lowest
vote share for a majority government since 1830, and the disproportionality between votes and
seats reached its highest level in British history.[^election-2024] A system that can turn a
third of the vote into two thirds of the power, while leaving millions feeling unrepresented,
is losing public consent.

**Devolution is uneven.** Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland have their own settlements,
English devolution is a patchwork of mayors and deals, and the Union itself is contested.

**The do-nothing baseline.** Leave this alone and centralisation deepens, the Lords keeps
growing, the voting system keeps producing results people do not recognise as fair, and trust
in the whole settlement keeps draining away.

---

## Evidence base

- **Elected second chambers are the norm.** Most comparable democracies have an elected or
  largely elected upper house. An appointed chamber the size of ours is an outlier.
  *Transferable* in principle, with care over how an elected chamber relates to the Commons.
- **More proportional systems are common.** Most European democracies use some form of
  proportional representation and form stable governments under it. *Transferable*, with a
  choice of system that keeps a constituency link.
- **Federal and regional states disperse government.** Germany, for example, spreads
  national institutions across several cities rather than one capital. *Transferable* to the
  case for a northern base set out below.
- **Greater Manchester proves devolution works at home.** A decade of real devolution, with
  an elected mayor and genuine control over transport, skills, and investment, helped
  Manchester grow at roughly double the UK rate and pass £100 billion in output.[^gm-const]
  *Directly transferable* as the model for extending devolution across England.

---

## Options appraisal

**Option 1: Incremental tidying.** Trim the Lords a little, offer more devolution deals, leave
the voting system alone. *Pros:* low conflict. *Cons:* leaves every underlying problem in
place. The do-nothing baseline.

**Option 2: Single-issue reform.** Pick one change, for example Lords reform, and do only
that. *Pros:* achievable. *Cons:* reforms interact, and doing one in isolation often fails,
as the 2020 Lords relocation idea showed.

**Option 3: A coherent constitutional package *(lead option)*.** Reform the Lords, put a
fairer voting system to the public, deepen devolution, and build a permanent northern base
for government, as one connected programme. *Pros:* the parts reinforce each other and add up
to a genuine shift of power. *Cons:* ambitious, needs sustained will across a parliament.
**Recommended.**

**Lead approach:** Option 3, sequenced so the early wins build momentum for the harder
changes.

## The proposals (under the lead option)

### 1. Reform the House of Lords
Replace the appointed chamber with a much smaller, largely elected second chamber that
revises legislation and holds government to account, without challenging the primacy of the
Commons. Patronage appointments end; size falls to something defensible.

### 2. A fairer voting system
The 2024 result showed first past the post straining against a multi-party reality.[^election-2024]
Rather than impose a particular system from the top, set the criteria, a more proportional result
that keeps a strong constituency link, and let a **citizens' assembly** weigh the main options, such
as the Single Transferable Vote and the Additional Member System, before the choice goes to a
referendum. This is exactly the kind of contested, long-term question that deliberation handles well
(see [public participation](./public-participation.md)).

### 3. Deep devolution across England
Extend the Greater Manchester model to every part of England that wants it: real powers and
funding over transport, skills, housing, and investment, not just delegated administration.
This connects directly to [local government reform](./democracy-and-reform.md) and the
[economy chapter](../economy/entrepreneurship-and-renewal.md).

### 4. A second base for Parliament and government in the North
Devolving to mayors is necessary but not enough. The machinery of national government still
sits almost entirely in London. Britain should build a permanent second base for national
government in the North, anchored in Greater Manchester: the largest economy outside London,
the fastest growing city region in the country, now worth over £100 billion a year.[^gm-const]

There is precedent at every scale. The Places for Growth programme has already moved more
than 16,000 civil service roles out of London, with an economic campus in Darlington that
hosts Treasury teams and recruits most of its staff locally.[^places-const] The BBC proved
the same logic in broadcasting at Salford. What has been missing is the political core, the
ministers and Parliament, rather than only the back office.

The settled lead option here is a standing northern parliamentary and government campus in
Greater Manchester: a permanent venue for select committees, evidence sessions, and some
debates, with whole departments headquartered there for good, ministerial offices, and the
civil service capacity to run real policy from the North. The Commons and Lords remain
legally in Westminster, but national political life is genuinely shared. The test is simple.
A minister should be able to do the job from the North without it being treated as a special
occasion.

Manchester should be the anchor of a network, not a smaller London. Pair it with the east-west
transport links the North needs (see [transport](../transport/transport.md)) and place
specific functions in Leeds, Liverpool, Sheffield, and Newcastle, so the benefit spreads
across the region. The alternative of relocating a whole chamber, as was floated for the Lords
and York in 2020, was criticised for separating the two Houses and as symbolism without wider
reform, which is why the campus-plus-departments route is preferred.[^lords-york]

### 5. Strengthen the Union
Treat the Union as a partnership. Respect the devolved settlements, resolve disputes through
clear rules rather than turf wars, and make the case for the Union through what it delivers.

## Costs and benefits

Most of this is about where power and institutions sit rather than headline spending. The
northern base carries real capital and relocation costs, offset over time by lower London
property and staffing costs and by the regional growth it anchors, as the
[economy chapter](../economy/entrepreneurship-and-renewal.md) sets out. A matching budget
note will be added where figures are material.

---

## Delivery and timeline

- **Years 1-2:** legislate Lords reform; commit to the northern base and begin moving the
  first whole departments; agree the devolution framework.
- **Years 2-4:** put the voting system to the public; roll out deeper devolution deals; stand
  up the northern parliamentary campus.
- **Years 4-10:** embed the new institutions; complete departmental relocations; review and
  adjust.

---

## Risks and mitigations

- **Reform stalling in the Lords.** *Mitigation:* sequence the early, popular changes first to
  build momentum.
- **A second base becoming a token.** *Mitigation:* move whole departments and real political
  functions, not just back-office teams; set a clear test of ministerial presence.
- **Just shifting concentration to Manchester.** *Mitigation:* make Manchester the anchor of a
  network with functions across the northern cities and the transport to link them.
- **Voting reform stalemate.** *Mitigation:* put a clear choice to the public rather than
  leaving it to Westminster self-interest.

---

## Consultation questions

1. Should the second chamber be fully elected, largely elected, or reformed another way, and
   how small?
2. Which voting system best balances proportionality with a constituency link?
3. How far should the northern base go, and which departments should move first?
4. How do we extend Greater Manchester-style devolution to areas without a mayor?
5. What rules would best resolve disputes between Westminster and the devolved nations?
