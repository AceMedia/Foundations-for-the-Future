# Governance: A Simpler, Digital, Accountable State

*A forward-looking proposal, judged by what works, not by left or right.*

> **Summary for the reader**
> - **The problem:** government is fragmented, slow, and analogue in places, and it is often
>   opaque about where money goes. Overlapping bodies and duplicated administration waste both
>   money and trust.
> - **The options:** carry on as now, salami-slice budgets, or run a deliberate
>   digitise-and-simplify programme with transparent, earmarked funds.
> - **Lead option:** digitise services **without a national digital ID**, merge overlapping
>   bodies, set a clear national licensing framework with room for local judgement, and publish
>   where earmarked money is spent.

## The problem

Good government is not just about spending less. It is about a state that works: quick to
deal with, clear about who is responsible, and honest about where the money goes.

Britain's falls short on each. Services are still part-analogue, so people queue, repeat
themselves, and wait. Responsibilities are split across overlapping departments and a long
list of arm's length bodies, which duplicates cost and blurs accountability. And when the
state raises money for a purpose, people rarely get to see whether it was actually spent on
that purpose.

**The do-nothing baseline.** Leave this and administrative cost keeps rising, digital
laggards keep paying for paper processes, and public trust keeps falling as people conclude
the state is neither efficient nor straight with them.

---

## A principle: efficiency without surveillance

This chapter wants a more efficient state, not a more intrusive one. Britain should digitise
services **without building a national digital ID**. People should not need a single
government identity, tracked across every interaction, just to renew a licence or claim a
service. The convenience of joined-up services can be had through verification at the point of
use, strong privacy, and data minimisation, with no central identity register and no mandatory
scheme. A government that wants people's trust should not start by demanding they sign up to be
catalogued.

---

## Evidence base

- **Estonia's digital services.** Estonia delivers most public services online and has cut
  bureaucracy sharply. Britain should learn the service-design and efficiency lessons, while
  rejecting the mandatory national digital ID that underpins the Estonian system; good digital
  services do not require one.[^estonia] *Partly transferable:* the design and convenience, not
  the centralised identity.
- **Online safety lives in the device, not a database.** The strongest protections for
  children online now sit in the operating system. Apple's 2026 child-safety tools let parents
  approve new contacts and websites, set per-category time limits, and share a child's age
  range with apps without revealing a birthday.[^apple] This shows how to protect children and
  privacy at once, and it is where government should look rather than to mandatory age or
  identity checks.

## Options appraisal

**Option 1: Status quo.** Keep the current structure and processes. *Pros:* no disruption.
*Cons:* cost and frustration keep rising. The do-nothing baseline.

**Option 2: Salami-slice cuts.** Trim budgets across the board without changing how the state
works. *Pros:* quick savings on paper. *Cons:* cuts service quality and resilience while
leaving the duplication and analogue processes that cause the waste.

**Option 3: Digitise and simplify *(lead option)*.** Invest in digital services without a
national ID, merge overlapping bodies, clarify responsibilities, and make earmarked spending
transparent. *Pros:* lower running costs, better service, higher trust, no surveillance creep.
*Cons:* upfront investment and careful change management, with strong protections for people
who are not online. **Recommended.**

## The proposals (under the lead option)

1. **Digitise services, without a digital ID.** Make government services simple and online
   where people want them, using verification at the point of use rather than a single national
   identity that follows people across government. No mandatory digital ID, privacy protected by
   design, data minimised, and guaranteed offline routes so no one is shut out.
2. **Merge overlapping bodies.** Simplify the map of departments and arm's length bodies so
   responsibilities are clear and duplication is removed, with savings tracked in
   [budget/governance-and-admin-savings.md](../budget/governance-and-admin-savings.md).
3. **A national licensing framework with local judgement.** Where Britain regulates vice
   markets, Westminster sets the core rules on age limits, product testing, labelling, tax
   collection, and sanctions, while councils and mayors decide how many premises an area can
   support, where they sit, and what conditions they must meet. Local government is better
   placed than Whitehall to judge whether a high street needs another premises or tighter
   conditions. This is a move from muddled criminalisation to transparent regulation, not a
   free-for-all.
4. **Transparent earmarked funds.** Money raised for a purpose should be seen to serve it.
   The proposed Health and Care Betterment Fund should be governed by a published formula
   that directs vice-tax receipts into national health, recovery, social care, end-of-life
   dignity, and local community health, rather than disappearing into general spending and
   being redistributed by whim. That strengthens accountability and lets communities see that
   regulation produces real civic benefit.
5. **Online safety led by parents and devices, not a digital nanny state.** Keep parents in
   charge of children's online lives, supported by the strong controls now built into phones
   and operating systems, as Apple's 2026 tools show.[^apple] Government should set sensible
   duties, learn from these device-level controls, and resist both digital nannying and any
   drift towards mandatory age or identity verification. This connects to the online-safety
   thread in [education and skills](../education/education-and-skills.md).

## Costs and benefits

Digital government and simpler administration reduce running costs over time, while better
services and visible, earmarked spending rebuild trust. Doing it without a national ID also
avoids the large cost and risk of building and securing a central identity system. Detailed
savings are set out in
[budget/governance-and-admin-savings.md](../budget/governance-and-admin-savings.md), to be
firmed up in the budget-accuracy pass.

---

## Delivery and timeline

- **Years 1-3:** build priority online services with offline fallbacks and no national ID;
  map and begin merging overlapping bodies; legislate the licensing framework.
- **Years 3-7:** roll out digital services across government; complete simplification;
  operate the published betterment-fund formula.
- **Years 7-10:** steady-state digital, simpler administration, with measurable savings and
  rising public satisfaction.

---

## Risks and mitigations

- **Digital exclusion.** *Mitigation:* guaranteed non-digital routes and support for people
  who are not online.
- **Surveillance creep.** *Mitigation:* no national digital ID, no central identity register,
  data minimised, and verification only at the point of use.
- **Reorganisation for its own sake.** *Mitigation:* merge bodies only where it clarifies
  responsibility and cuts duplication, with savings tracked openly.
- **Earmarked funds raided over time.** *Mitigation:* a published formula and transparent
  reporting so diversions are visible.

## Consultation questions

1. How do we digitise services well without a national digital ID, and what privacy safeguards
   must come with that?
2. Which overlapping bodies should be merged first?
3. How should the national licensing framework split decisions between Westminster and local
   government?
4. How should the betterment-fund formula be set and published to keep it honest?
5. What duties on platforms and devices best support parents on online safety, without a
   digital nanny state or mandatory age and identity verification?
