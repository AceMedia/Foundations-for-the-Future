# Transport: Moving People and Goods, Connecting the Country

*A forward-looking proposal, judged by what works, not by left or right.*

> **Summary for the reader**
> - **The problem:** transport outside London is unreliable and dear. Local roads carry an
>   £18.6bn repair backlog[^roads], bus journeys outside London have fallen by about a fifth
>   since 2009[^buses], and northern rail cancels more than one train in twenty.[^northern-rail]
>   Poor links hold back the regions and add cost to everything we make and move.
> - **The options:** (1) patch and maintain; (2) flagship megaprojects; (3) **connectivity
>   first, with everyday transport and northern east-west links as the priority** *(lead
>   option)*; (4) demand management and fares reform.
> - **Indicative impact:** better regional connectivity is one of the highest-return ways to
>   close the productivity gap. Figures to be set in the budget pass.
> - **Timeline:** rolling 10-year programme; road maintenance and bus reform in years 1-3.

---

## The problem

Transport is the circulatory system of the economy. When it works, people reach jobs,
goods reach markets, and places thrive. Outside London, too often it does not work.

**Local roads are crumbling.** The repair backlog for local roads in England and Wales has
reached about £18.6bn, an estimated twelve years of work, with nearly one in six local
roads having under five years of structural life left.[^roads] Potholes are the daily proof
of decades of underinvestment.

**Buses have been hollowed out.** Local bus journeys outside London fell from about 4.6
billion in 2009 to 3.6 billion in 2024, a drop of roughly a fifth, while council-supported
mileage was cut in half and financial support fell by nearly two fifths over the 2010s.[^buses]
For people without a car, especially in towns and rural areas, that is lost access to work,
healthcare, and family.

**Northern rail is unreliable.** In 2024 both Northern and TransPennine Express cancelled
more than one train in twenty, and Northern's punctuality sat around 69% against a national
average of 86%.[^northern-rail] A railway people cannot rely on is one they stop using.

**The North trades as separate towns, not one market.** Manchester, Leeds, Liverpool,
Sheffield, and Newcastle sit close together but are poorly linked, so the North never gets
the agglomeration benefits that make London productive. This is the connectivity gap behind
the regional output gap set out in the [economy chapter](../economy/entrepreneurship-and-renewal.md).

**The do-nothing baseline.** On current trends the roads backlog grows, buses keep shrinking
into a spiral of cuts and lost passengers, and the North stays fragmented. The bill does not
disappear; it just moves from planned maintenance to emergency repair, lost output, and
poorer health.

## Evidence base

- **London (Transport for London):** a single integrated authority with control over buses,
  trains, and fares built a network people actually use. *Transferable* as a model of
  devolved, integrated transport, which is what the rest of the country lacks.
- **Greater Manchester (the Bee Network):** Manchester has brought its buses back under
  public control through franchising, with integrated ticketing and branding, the first city
  region outside London to do so. *Directly transferable* and already being copied.
- **Switzerland and Germany:** clockface timetables and integrated regional rail make public
  transport dependable enough to be the default. *Transferable* in planning and integration,
  if not in topography.

The lesson is consistent. Where one body controls the network and is accountable for it,
service improves. Where provision is fragmented and deregulated, it declines.

---

## Options appraisal

**Option 1: Patch and maintain.** Keep funding at roughly current levels and fix the worst
faults. *Pros:* cheap in the short term. *Cons:* the backlog keeps growing and the decline
continues. This is the do-nothing baseline.

**Option 2: Flagship megaprojects.** Concentrate money on a few large schemes. *Pros:*
visible, nationally significant. *Cons:* long timescales, high cost, and they do little for
the everyday journeys most people actually make.

**Option 3: Connectivity first *(lead option)*.** Prioritise the transport people use daily
and the links that unlock regional economies: a serious road-maintenance programme, bus
franchising powers for every area that wants them, and northern east-west rail. *Pros:*
high return, faster benefits, spreads gains across the country. *Cons:* needs sustained
funding and local delivery capacity. **Recommended.**

**Option 4: Demand management and fares reform.** Simplify fares, reform road and rail
pricing, and shift demand. *Pros:* can improve use of what already exists. *Cons:* not a
substitute for investment where the network is simply inadequate. *Best used alongside
Option 3.*

**Lead approach:** Option 3 as the spine, with the fares and pricing reforms of Option 4 to
get more from the network, and megaprojects judged case by case rather than as the strategy.

---

## The proposals (under the lead option)

1. **Maintenance first.** A multi-year, funded programme to clear the local-roads backlog,
   with money councils can rely on rather than annual pothole funds.
2. **Buses back under local control.** Give every combined authority and county the powers
   and funding to franchise buses on the Manchester Bee Network model, with integrated
   ticketing across bus and rail.
3. **Northern east-west rail.** Treat connecting the northern cities as economic policy.
   Faster, more frequent, more reliable links between Manchester, Leeds, Liverpool,
   Sheffield, and Newcastle, tied to the [second base for government](../governance/constitution-and-devolution.md).
4. **Simpler, fairer fares.** Reform the fares system so it is predictable and rewards
   regular use, on rail and across modes.
5. **Charge the switch to electric.** A planned rollout of charging infrastructure,
   especially outside cities and for people without driveways, so the move to electric
   vehicles does not stall.
6. **Active travel where it fits.** Safe walking and cycling routes in towns and cities,
   chosen on merit rather than imposed everywhere.

## Costs and benefits

Detailed costings will be set in the dedicated budget pass and a matching
`budget/transport.md` file. The core argument is that reliable everyday transport and
better northern connectivity are among the highest-return investments available for closing
the regional productivity gap, while a cleared maintenance backlog avoids the far higher
cost of emergency repair and vehicle damage.

---

## Delivery and timeline

- **Years 1-3:** fund and start the road-maintenance programme; legislate and roll out bus
  franchising powers; set the northern rail plan.
- **Years 3-7:** deliver northern east-west rail upgrades; scale charging infrastructure;
  reform fares.
- **Years 7-10:** steady-state reliable services, with measurable gains in punctuality, bus
  use, and northern connectivity.

Delivery sits with combined authorities and local transport bodies where possible, backed by
national funding and standards, in line with the devolution argument in
[constitution and devolution](../governance/constitution-and-devolution.md).

---

## Risks and mitigations

- **Funding stop-start.** *Mitigation:* multi-year settlements rather than annual pots, so
  councils and operators can plan.
- **Local delivery capacity.** *Mitigation:* build on authorities that already franchise and
  share their models; phase rollout.
- **Megaproject cost overruns.** *Mitigation:* judge large schemes case by case against
  everyday-transport returns, not as the default.
- **Electric transition leaving people behind.** *Mitigation:* prioritise charging for those
  without driveways and outside cities.

---

## Consultation questions

1. Is connectivity-first the right priority, or should a small number of megaprojects still
   take precedence?
2. Should bus franchising powers be offered to every area, or only to combined authorities?
3. What is the right first phase for northern east-west rail?
4. How should fares be reformed to be simpler and fairer without losing revenue?
5. Who should pay for charging infrastructure, and how do we make sure it reaches everyone?
