# Trade and the EU Relationship

*A forward-looking proposal, judged by what works, not by left or right.*

> **Summary for the reader**
> - **The problem:** trade friction with our largest market has added cost and paperwork,
>   hitting smaller firms hardest and complicating Northern Ireland, while our wider trade
>   strategy drifts.
> - **The options:** rejoin the single market or customs union, get closer without rejoining,
>   make only targeted fixes, or prioritise divergence.
> - **Lead option:** **closer alignment, not rejoin.** Cut friction through a veterinary and
>   plant-health deal, youth mobility, and mutual recognition, while staying out of the single
>   market and customs union.
> - **Timeline:** negotiate the practical deals early; build the wider strategy over a parliament.
>
> *Costings are indicative; the detailed figures live in the budget files.*

---

## The problem

Trade is not an abstraction. It decides whether our firms can sell and whether our shelves stay
stocked.

**Friction with the EU has a real cost.** New checks, paperwork, and barriers since leaving the
single market have added cost and delay for exporters, and the burden falls hardest on smaller
firms that cannot absorb it.[^smes] For some, exporting to Europe is no longer worth the effort.

**Northern Ireland remains delicate.** The arrangements that keep goods moving while protecting
the single market and the peace settlement need to work smoothly in practice, not just on paper.

**The wider strategy is adrift.** Trade, industrial policy, and food standards are too often run
in separate silos, so deals are struck without a clear sense of what we are trying to build.

**The do-nothing baseline.** Leave avoidable barriers in place and firms keep quietly giving up
on European markets, smaller exporters keep losing out, and the relationship stays a source of
friction rather than a managed, productive one.

---

## Evidence base

- **Practical deals can cut friction without rejoining.** A veterinary and plant-health (SPS)
  agreement, mutual recognition of professional qualifications, and a youth-mobility scheme are
  well-established tools that reduce barriers while leaving the single market and customs union.
  *Directly transferable*, since these are standard features of trade relationships.
- **Smaller firms need active support.** Where exporters get help with paperwork and access,
  trade holds up better. *Transferable* as a matter of competent delivery.

---

## Options appraisal

**Option 1: Rejoin the single market or customs union.** *Pros:* the biggest cut in friction.
*Cons:* reopens a settled and divisive question, and brings obligations the public did not
vote for. Not the lead here.

**Option 2: Closer alignment, not rejoin *(lead option)*.** Reduce friction through targeted,
practical deals while staying outside the single market and customs union. *Pros:* real gains for
exporters and consumers without reopening the constitutional fight. *Cons:* requires give and
take and steady diplomacy. **Recommended.**

**Option 3: Targeted fixes only.** Fix Northern Ireland and a few irritants, otherwise leave the
relationship as is. *Pros:* low effort. *Cons:* leaves most of the avoidable friction in place.

**Option 4: Prioritise divergence.** Treat regulatory freedom as the priority. *Pros:* maximum
flexibility. *Cons:* adds friction with our largest market for uncertain gain.

---

## The proposals (under the lead option)

1. **A veterinary and plant-health agreement.** Cut checks on food and agricultural trade, which
   would ease pressure on exporters and on the Northern Ireland arrangements, in step with the
   future environment and farming chapter and the
   [food procurement](../welfare/bulk-food-procurement.md) chapter.
2. **A youth-mobility scheme.** Let young people work and travel between Britain and Europe for a
   set period, a reciprocal arrangement that fits the two-way thinking in
   [seasonal migration](../immigration/seasonal-migration.md).
3. **Mutual recognition of qualifications.** Make it easier for professionals to work across
   borders, helping services trade as well as goods.
4. **Make Northern Ireland work smoothly.** Treat the practical operation of the arrangements as
   a standing priority, not a one-off fix.
5. **Join trade up with strategy.** Align trade deals with our industrial and food strategies, and
   give smaller exporters real help with access and paperwork.

---

## Costs and benefits

Cutting friction lowers costs for exporters and consumers and supports smaller firms, at the
price of steady negotiation and some give and take. The fiscal and trade modelling belongs in the
budget files. The strategic benefit is a managed, productive relationship rather
than a running argument.

---

## Delivery and timeline

- **Years 1-2:** negotiate the veterinary deal and youth-mobility scheme; stabilise the Northern
  Ireland arrangements.
- **Years 2-4:** mutual recognition and exporter support; align trade with industrial and food
  strategy.
- **Years 4-10:** a settled, lower-friction relationship that supports trade both ways.

---

## Risks and mitigations

- **Reopening the Brexit argument.** *Mitigation:* be clear this is closer working, not rejoining,
  and keep out of the single market and customs union.
- **Negotiations stalling.** *Mitigation:* start with the deals that are in both sides' interest,
  such as veterinary checks.
- **Alignment limiting future freedom.** *Mitigation:* choose alignment where the trade gain is
  clear and keep it proportionate.

---

## Consultation questions

1. How far should we align to cut friction, and where should we keep regulatory freedom?
2. What should a youth-mobility scheme with Europe look like?
3. What would make the Northern Ireland arrangements work smoothly in practice?
4. How do we best support smaller exporters back into European markets?
