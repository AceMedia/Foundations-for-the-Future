# Financial Services and the City

*A forward-looking proposal, judged by what works, not by left or right.*

> **Summary for the reader**
> - **The problem:** financial services are a real national strength and a big source of tax, but
>   too little of that capital reaches firms and regions that need it, and it goes into property
>   speculation instead of productive investment.
> - **The options:** lighter-touch competitiveness, tougher stability regulation, or a deliberate
>   push to channel capital into productive investment.
> - **Lead option:** **channel more long-term capital into business and infrastructure**, keep the
>   stability lessons of 2008, and protect consumers.
> - **Timeline:** reform incentives over a parliament.
>
> *Figures here are first-draft and will be checked in the budget-accuracy pass (#21).*

---

## The problem

The City is one of Britain's genuine strengths, yet the wider economy gets too little from it.

**Capital goes to the wrong places.** Too much investment flows into property and overseas assets
rather than into British firms and infrastructure, as the
[economy chapter](./entrepreneurship-and-renewal.md) sets out.

**Scale-up finance is thin.** Promising firms struggle to raise the growth capital that would let
them stay and scale here, so they sell early or move abroad.

**Stability and consumers still need protecting.** The lessons of 2008 must not be forgotten, and
consumers need protection from poor products and scams.

**The do-nothing baseline.** Leave this and Britain keeps an economy that is good at moving money
and poor at investing it, with capital chasing property while productive firms go without.

---

## Evidence base

- **Productive investment of long-term capital works.** Countries such as Canada and Australia
  channel pension and insurance capital into infrastructure and growth while protecting savers, as
  the [pensions chapter](../welfare/pensions-and-ageing.md) notes. *Transferable*, with safeguards.
- **Stability is not optional.** The 2008 crisis showed the cost of weak regulation. *Transferable*
  as a permanent lesson.
- **Consumer protection builds trust.** Clear rules against poor products and scams keep the system
  trusted. *Transferable* as basic competence.

---

## Options appraisal

**Option 1: Lighter-touch competitiveness.** Cut rules to compete. *Pros:* attractive to firms.
*Cons:* risks the stability lessons of 2008 and does not fix where capital goes.

**Option 2: Tougher stability regulation.** Prioritise stability above all. *Pros:* safety.
*Cons:* on its own it does not direct capital to productive use.

**Option 3: Channel capital to productive investment *(lead option)*.** Direct more long-term
capital into firms and infrastructure, keep the stability rules, and protect consumers. *Pros:*
turns a financial strength into real-economy investment, with stability and protection intact.
*Cons:* needs careful design and incentives. **Recommended.**

---

## The proposals (under the lead option)

1. **Direct long-term capital to productive use.** Encourage pension and insurance capital into
   British business and infrastructure, with savers protected and no reckless compulsion, in step
   with the [pensions chapter](../welfare/pensions-and-ageing.md).
2. **Fix scale-up finance.** Make it easier for growing firms to raise capital at home, so they stay
   and scale here, linking to [science and technology](../technology/science-technology-and-ai.md).
3. **Keep the stability rules.** Maintain the protections that guard against another 2008, and
   resist a race to the bottom dressed up as competitiveness.
4. **Protect consumers.** Strong, clear rules against poor products and scams, so people can trust
   the system.
5. **Spread the benefit.** Direct more finance and financial-sector jobs beyond London, tied to the
   regional argument in the [economy chapter](./entrepreneurship-and-renewal.md).

---

## Costs and benefits

This is mostly about incentives and rules rather than spending. The benefit is a financial sector
that invests in the real economy, keeps growing firms here, and stays stable and trusted. Figures
belong in the budget-accuracy pass (#21).

---

## Delivery and timeline

- **Years 1-2:** reform the rules so long-term capital can invest productively with safeguards; fix
  scale-up finance.
- **Years 2-5:** embed productive investment and consumer protection; spread financial activity to
  the regions.
- **Years 5-10:** a financial sector that invests in Britain, safely and widely.

---

## Risks and mitigations

- **Stability traded for competitiveness.** *Mitigation:* keep the 2008 lessons and resist a race to
  the bottom.
- **Savers put at risk.** *Mitigation:* productive investment with savers first and no reckless
  compulsion.
- **Capital still favouring property.** *Mitigation:* align tax and incentives, in step with the
  [tax chapter](./tax-and-fiscal-reform.md).

---
