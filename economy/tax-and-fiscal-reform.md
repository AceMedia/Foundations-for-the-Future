# Tax and Fiscal Reform

*A forward-looking proposal, judged by what works, not by left or right.*

> **Summary for the reader**
> - **The problem:** the tax system is complicated, distorting, and unfair. Work is taxed more
>   heavily than wealth, council tax still rests on 1991 property values, and business rates
>   punish the high street. The overall burden is near its highest since the 1940s, yet it
>   falls in the wrong places.
> - **The options:** leave it alone, cut and flatten, or rebalance away from work and toward
>   wealth and property while simplifying.
> - **Lead option:** **shift tax off work and onto wealth and property.** Lower the taxes on
>   earnings, replace council tax with a proportional property tax, and move capital gains
>   closer to income-tax rates, with a simpler system overall.
> - **Timeline:** legislate over a parliament, phase the transition to avoid cliff edges.
>
> *Costings are indicative; the detailed figures live in the budget files.*

---

## The problem

Tax is how a country pays for what it values, and ours has drifted into something few would
design on purpose.

**Work is taxed harder than wealth.** Income from a job carries income tax and National
Insurance, while income from assets is often taxed more lightly. That penalises the people who
earn their living and rewards those who already hold wealth.

**Council tax is stuck in 1991.** In England, council tax bands are still based on property
values from 1991, so the system bears little relation to what homes are actually worth. It is
regressive, with cheaper homes often paying a higher share of their value than expensive ones.[^counciltax]

**Business rates punish the high street.** A tax on physical premises falls hardest on shops
and hospitality while large online operations with little floorspace get off lightly.

**The burden is high and badly placed.** The overall tax take is near its highest level since
the 1940s,[^burden] yet it lands disproportionately on earned income. A high burden that also
drags on work and investment is the worst of both worlds.

**The do-nothing baseline.** Leave this and the distortions deepen: work stays over-taxed,
property taxation drifts further from reality, the high street keeps shrinking, and the system
loses public consent because it so obviously is not fair.

---

## Evidence base

- **Other countries tax property closer to its real value.** Many comparable economies levy
  property taxes on up-to-date valuations, which is fairer and less distorting than frozen
  1991 bands. *Transferable*, with care over transitions for people who are asset-rich but
  cash-poor.
- **Taxing work less and assets more is a recognised pro-growth move.** Bodies such as the
  Institute for Fiscal Studies have long argued that shifting tax off work and onto land and
  property improves both fairness and efficiency. *Transferable* as a direction of travel.

---

## Options appraisal

**Option 1: Leave it alone.** *Pros:* no upheaval. *Cons:* every problem above gets worse.
The do-nothing baseline.

**Option 2: Cut and flatten.** Lower the overall burden with simpler, flatter rates. *Pros:*
pro-growth, simple. *Cons:* hard to fund without deep spending cuts, and does not fix the
work-versus-wealth imbalance.

**Option 3: Rebalance off work, onto wealth and property *(lead option)*.** Lower the taxes on
earnings, replace council tax with a proportional property tax, reform business rates, and move
capital gains closer to income-tax rates, all within a simpler system. *Pros:* fairer, more
pro-work, less distorting, and it modernises a frozen system. *Cons:* property tax reform
creates winners and losers and must be phased carefully. **Recommended.**

---

## The proposals (under the lead option)

1. **Tax work less.** Reduce the tax on earned income, with National Insurance and income tax
   simplified and eventually merged, so the system is transparent and work pays.
2. **Replace council tax with a proportional property tax.** Charge a percentage of an
   up-to-date property value rather than frozen 1991 bands, with protections for people who are
   asset-rich but cash-poor (for example deferral until sale). Roll stamp duty, which gums up
   the housing market, into the same reform.
3. **Reform business rates.** Rebalance the tax on premises so the high street is not punished
   relative to large low-footprint online operations.
4. **Move capital gains toward income-tax rates.** Treat income from assets more like income
   from work, so wealth is not taxed more lightly than effort.
5. **Simplify.** Strip out reliefs and complexity that mostly benefit those who can afford
   advisers, making the whole system easier to understand and harder to game.

**A deliberate choice on wealth taxes.** The shift onto wealth here runs through the proportional
property tax and capital-gains alignment, not through an annual wealth tax or new inheritance taxes.
A standing wealth tax is hard to value and administer, tends to drive capital and people abroad for a
modest yield, and most countries that tried one have since abandoned it. Taxing property where it sits
and treating gains like income achieves the same rebalancing more cleanly, and inheritance tax is left
broadly as it is rather than expanded.

This is about fairness and a system that supports work, not about raising the overall take.

---

## Costs and benefits

The aim is a fairer mix at a similar overall level, not a bigger tax burden. The gains are a
system that rewards work, treats property honestly, supports the high street, and is simpler to
comply with. Detailed revenue and distributional modelling belongs in the budget files
 and a matching budget file, since property and capital reforms create clear winners and
losers that must be shown openly.

---

## Delivery and timeline

- **Years 1-2:** legislate the direction; begin a national property revaluation; simplify
  income tax and National Insurance.
- **Years 2-5:** phase in the proportional property tax with transitional protections; reform
  business rates and capital gains.
- **Years 5-10:** a settled, simpler system that taxes work less and wealth more fairly.

---

## Risks and mitigations

- **Property tax hitting the asset-rich but cash-poor.** *Mitigation:* deferral until sale,
  caps, and transitional relief.
- **Revaluation backlash.** *Mitigation:* phase changes, publish who gains and loses, and be
  honest that it is about fairness.
- **Capital flight or behaviour change.** *Mitigation:* sensible rates and design, learning
  from where similar reforms have worked.
- **Complexity creeping back.** *Mitigation:* a standing commitment to simplification and
  fewer reliefs.

## Consultation questions

1. What rate and design should a proportional property tax take, and what protections are
   needed for low-income homeowners?
2. Should National Insurance and income tax be merged, and how fast?
3. How far should capital gains move toward income-tax rates?
4. How do we reform business rates without simply shifting the burden somewhere unfair?
