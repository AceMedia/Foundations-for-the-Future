# Courts and the Legal System

*A forward-looking proposal, judged by what works, not by left or right.*

> **Summary for the reader**
> - **The problem:** the court backlog leaves victims, defendants, and witnesses waiting years,
>   legal aid has been cut to the point many cannot get representation, and civil and family justice
>   is slow and hard to access.
> - **The options:** keep firefighting, or clear the backlog and rebuild access to justice.
> - **Lead option:** **clear the backlog and keep it down, restore workable legal aid, and improve
>   civil and family justice**, including more mediation.
> - **Timeline:** attack the backlog early; rebuild access over a parliament.
>
> *Costings are indicative; the detailed figures live in the budget files.*

---

## The problem

Justice delayed is justice denied, and the system that delivers it is clogged.

**The backlog is huge.** The Crown Court backlog reached a record of around 74,000 cases at the end of
2024 and has kept rising, with Ministry of Justice modelling warning it could pass 104,000 by 2029 and
some trials already listed for 2030.[^backlog] Cases wait years to be heard, leaving victims,
defendants, and witnesses in limbo, and undermining the rehabilitation and policing reforms in the
[justice](./rehabilitation-through-public-service.md) and [policing](./policing-and-crime.md)
chapters.

**Legal aid has been cut.** Reductions have left many people unable to get representation, so they
face the system alone or give up on valid claims.

**Civil and family justice is slow.** Disputes that could be resolved quickly drag on, at cost to
families and businesses.

**The do-nothing baseline.** Leave this and the backlog grows, victims wait longer, more people go
unrepresented, and confidence in justice falls, dragging down the rest of the justice agenda.

---

## Evidence base

- **Backlogs can be cleared with capacity and process reform.** More sitting days, better case
  management, and digital scheduling cut delays. *Transferable* as a matter of investment and
  competence.
- **Mediation resolves many disputes faster.** Family and civil mediation can settle cases without
  full court hearings. *Transferable* where it does not deny people their day in court.
- **Legal aid underpins fair trials.** Representation is part of a fair system, not a luxury.
  *Transferable* as a principle.

---

## Options appraisal

**Option 1: Keep firefighting.** Manage the backlog with short-term measures. *Pros:* avoids hard
investment. *Cons:* the backlog and the access problem persist. The do-nothing baseline.

**Option 2: Clear the backlog and rebuild access *(lead option)*.** Invest to clear and keep down
the backlog, restore workable legal aid, and improve civil and family justice with more mediation.
*Pros:* faster justice, fair representation, and a system that supports the wider justice reforms.
*Cons:* needs sustained investment in courts and legal aid. **Recommended.**

---

## The proposals (under the lead option)

1. **Clear the backlog and keep it down.** Invest in sitting days, judges, and case management, with
   digital scheduling, to clear the backlog and stop it rebuilding.
2. **Clear it without gutting trial by jury.** Trial by jury is a civil-liberties principle worth
   protecting for serious cases. Where the Leveson review's intermediate court (a judge with
   magistrates) helps, keep it to the least serious either-way offences, rather than the broad
   curtailment of juries the government has floated.[^leveson]
2. **Restore workable legal aid.** Rebuild legal aid so people can get representation, with a
   settlement that keeps the professions viable.
3. **Improve civil and family justice.** Speed up civil and family cases, with more mediation where
   it resolves disputes fairly and faster.
4. **Join up with policing and rehabilitation.** Make sure court reform supports, rather than
   undercuts, the [policing](./policing-and-crime.md) and
   [rehabilitation](./rehabilitation-through-public-service.md) reforms.
5. **Support victims and witnesses.** Reduce the waits and stresses that currently drive victims and
   witnesses to drop out.

---

## Costs and benefits

Clearing the backlog and restoring legal aid cost money, but a clogged justice system carries its
own large costs in delayed cases, dropped prosecutions, and lost confidence. The figures belong in
the budget files.

---

## Delivery and timeline

- **Years 1-2:** invest to clear the Crown court backlog; begin restoring legal aid.
- **Years 2-5:** keep the backlog down with process reform; improve civil and family justice and
  mediation.
- **Years 5-10:** timely justice with fair representation and strong victim support.

---

## Risks and mitigations

- **Backlog rebuilding after a one-off push.** *Mitigation:* permanent capacity and better case
  management, not just a surge.
- **Legal aid settlement too thin to work.** *Mitigation:* fund it enough to keep the professions
  viable.
- **Mediation used to deny a day in court.** *Mitigation:* keep mediation as an option, not a
  barrier to justice.

---

## Consultation questions

1. What capacity and process reforms would clear the backlog and keep it down?
2. What does a workable legal aid settlement look like?
3. Where should mediation be expanded, and where must court access be protected?
4. How do we best support victims and witnesses through the system?
5. How far should trial by jury be protected, and where is an intermediate court justified?
