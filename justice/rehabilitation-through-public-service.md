# Rehabilitation Through Public Service: Work That Pays Back and Builds Futures

Prison should protect the public, punish serious wrongdoing, and reduce future crime. At the moment it is too often doing only the first two badly and the third inconsistently. Too many people leave custody with no job, no stable routine, no recognised qualification, and no practical route into lawful work.

That is wasteful. It is also dangerous. If prison releases people with fewer prospects than when they entered, the public pays twice: once for custody, then again through reoffending, homelessness, addiction, court backlogs, victim harm, and further imprisonment.

Britain should build a **Public Service Rehabilitation Corps** for low-risk people in custody and people on community sentences. The principle is simple: suitable offenders should be able to reduce costs, repay society, gain qualifications, and move into real work by contributing to areas where Britain already has shortages: food production, public kitchens, logistics, social care support, estates maintenance, recycling, retrofit, and community health infrastructure.

This must be a rehabilitation programme, not a cheap labour scheme.

---

## The problem

The prison system is expensive and crowded. HMPPS reported net expenditure of **£5.6 billion** in 2024/25, with prisons accounting for about **£3.9 billion** of resource spending. Parliamentary research using Ministry of Justice data put the 2023/24 overall cost per prisoner in England and Wales at **£53,801** a year, with the overall cost per prison place at **£56,987**.

Outcomes after release remain weak. Ministry of Justice employment statistics show that in the year to March 2024 only **19.3%** of offenders were employed six weeks after release from custody, rising to **31.1%** after six months. The 2024/25 figures were affected by Probation Reset and are not directly comparable, but still showed that most prison leavers were not in work six months after release.

Reoffending remains a large public cost. The January to March 2024 proven reoffending cohort had an overall reoffending rate of **28.9%**. Adults released from custody or starting court orders had a rate of **37.1%**, and adults released from custodial sentences of less than 12 months had a rate of **66.0%**.

That is the policy failure this chapter addresses: prison should not be a holding pen followed by a cliff edge. It should be a controlled route back into lawful work wherever public safety allows.

---

## What already exists

This idea does not start from zero.

HMPPS already has the **New Futures Network**, which connects prisons with employers and supports prison leavers into jobs. It supports prison industries, HMP Academies, and release on temporary licence work placements.

Prisoners can already work outside prison on **Release on Temporary Licence** where they are suitable and risk assessed. The Prisoners' Earnings Act allows governors to apply a levy of up to **40%** on eligible wages over £20 per week for low-risk prisoners working outside prison. In 2024/25, an average of **1,080** active prisoners worked under arrangements subject to the levy, earning **£22.0 million** before levy deductions and raising **£3.9 million** for Victim Support.

Community Payback also already exists. Courts in England and Wales can impose unpaid work requirements, usually **40 to 300 hours**, as part of community or suspended sentence orders. Government proposals in the 2025 Sentencing Bill would allow reductions in unpaid work hours as an incentive for sustained compliance and earlier completion.

The missing piece is scale, purpose, and integration. Work exists, but it is not yet treated as a national workforce and rehabilitation pathway tied into food security, social care, health support, and skills policy.

---

## The core proposal

Create a **Public Service Rehabilitation Corps** with four pathways.

### 1. Food production and public kitchens

Low-risk prisoners and people on community sentences could support the national food system through:

- prison gardens, market gardens, polytunnels, and horticulture training;
- food packing, labelling, storage, and distribution for public procurement;
- preparation of meals for prisons, community kitchens, shelters, schools, and care settings where appropriate;
- food waste reduction, composting, recycling, and redistribution logistics;
- farm placements under release on temporary licence for suitable prisoners close to release.

This links directly to the bulk food procurement model in this repo. Food work is practical, visible, and useful. It teaches routine, hygiene, teamwork, logistics, machinery safety, and basic business discipline.

*[See the bulk food procurement framework](../welfare/bulk-food-procurement.md)*

### 2. Care support and health infrastructure

There is a major shortage of domestic social care workers, but this pathway must be tightly safeguarded.

Prisoners should not be placed unsupervised with vulnerable people. Violent, sexual, coercive, safeguarding, or exploitation-related offenders should be excluded from any care-facing route. Nobody should enter direct care work without proper checks, training, supervision, and regulatory clearance.

But there are useful and ethical ways to build a care pathway:

- accredited "new to care" training before release;
- dementia-awareness, first aid, manual-handling, food hygiene, infection-control, and communication qualifications;
- supervised non-personal support roles after release, such as meals, transport, maintenance, laundry, gardens, activity setup, and community companionship schemes;
- progression into paid care assistant roles only after DBS checks, references, probation approval, employer acceptance, and safeguarding training;
- NHS and care supply-chain work inside prisons, such as laundry, textiles, furniture repair, equipment cleaning, admin support, and logistics where security allows.

This would not solve social care staffing on its own. It would create a disciplined route for a small but meaningful number of people who are suitable, motivated, and willing to enter a socially useful career.

*[See the end-of-life and dementia care framework](../health/end-of-life-and-dementia-care.md)*

### 3. Retrofit, repair, and estates maintenance

Prisons can train people for work Britain badly needs:

- insulation and basic retrofit support;
- painting, decorating, plastering, joinery, and grounds maintenance;
- repair of public furniture, bikes, wheelchairs, mobility aids, and community equipment;
- recycling, reuse, and public estate maintenance;
- flood resilience, drainage maintenance, and civil resilience tasks under supervision.

This connects justice policy to housing, health, and resilience. Someone who leaves prison with a construction card, a safety certificate, and a real work history is less likely to drift back into theft, drugs, or informal cash work.

### 4. Community Payback with skills, not just punishment

Unpaid work should remain punitive and reparative, but it should also build employability.

Community Payback projects should include:

- food growing and community kitchens;
- care-home gardens and public realm improvements;
- repairs for community centres, youth clubs, and charities;
- environmental cleanup, recycling, and local resilience projects;
- accredited training modules counted within a limited share of hours where this improves completion and job outcomes.

The public should see the work. Victims and communities should see that the sentence produces visible repair, not just attendance on a spreadsheet.

## Sentence incentives

Sentence incentives should be cautious, capped, and earned.

They should also be proportionate. Incentives should reflect both the seriousness of the offence and the effort, reliability, and public value required by the task.

For custody, suitable low-risk prisoners could earn additional release preparation privileges or a limited sentence credit where they:

- complete accredited training;
- maintain attendance and behaviour standards;
- pass drug testing where relevant;
- complete a verified public-service placement;
- have an employer, college, or apprenticeship route ready on release;
- remain risk assessed as suitable.

This should never apply automatically, and it should not apply to serious violent, sexual, domestic abuse, coercive control, terrorism, exploitation, or high-risk safeguarding cases. Public protection comes first.

A simple proportionality rule should apply:

| Case type | Access to programme | Incentive ceiling |
|---|---|---|
| Low-level non-violent offences | Broad access if risk assessment is clear | Training credit, privileges, Community Payback reductions, limited sentence credit where lawful |
| Non-violent theft or similar acquisitive offending | Access where behaviour and risk are stable | Earned incentives tied tightly to restitution, work, and employment route |
| Anything above that seriousness | Cautious case-by-case access only | Mainly training, release preparation, and privileges unless independently approved |
| Serious violence, sexual offending, domestic abuse, coercive control, exploitation, terrorism, or high safeguarding risk | Excluded from public-facing and care-facing routes | No sentence credit through this scheme |

For community sentences, the existing direction of travel is sensible: allow a modest reduction in required unpaid work hours for sustained compliance and early completion. But the reduction should be tied to meaningful completion, not box-ticking. A person who finishes a food-production, retrofit, or care-support pathway early and well should be rewarded more than someone who repeatedly misses appointments.

The incentive structure could be:

- **attendance and behaviour credit** for reliable participation;
- **qualification credit** for passing recognised training;
- **public value credit** for completing priority community projects;
- **employment credit** for moving into verified work, apprenticeship, or further training;
- **victim and community contribution** through existing levy or reparation routes.

The goal is not to make punishment disappear. It is to make punishment produce fewer future victims.

---

## Pay, levies, and fairness

The programme must not undercut ordinary workers.

Rules should be clear:

- external paid work should meet normal employment law and minimum wage rules;
- the existing victims levy should remain protected;
- any additional levy should be transparent and capped;
- prisoners should retain enough earnings to build release savings, support dependants, and avoid immediate destitution;
- public bodies and private contractors should not use prison labour to replace paid staff or break strikes;
- placements should be additional, supervised, and tied to training or resettlement.

The Prisoners' Earnings Act already proves the principle that low-risk prisoners in outside work can contribute financially. But the current scale is small. Expanding from roughly 1,000 active workers to 5,000-10,000 over time would not transform the national budget by itself, but it could create tens of millions in levy receipts, more release savings, better victim support, and stronger employment outcomes.

The largest savings would come from reduced reoffending and shorter unnecessary custody, not from the direct value of the labour.

## Safeguards

This policy only works if it is trusted. Safeguards should be built into law and contract design.

### Eligibility

Eligible participants should normally be:

- low risk of serious harm;
- low risk of absconding;
- behaviourally stable in custody or compliant in the community;
- close enough to release for work to support resettlement;
- willing to participate where the route is paid work or training;
- matched to work that is safe for them, staff, victims, and the public.

For lower-level non-violent offending, access can be relatively broad once risk assessment is clear. For anything above non-violent theft or similar low-harm acquisitive offending, access should become progressively more cautious and case-by-case. The more serious the offence or the more sensitive the setting, the stronger the supervision, exclusion, and independent sign-off should be.

### Exclusions

Direct care-facing roles should exclude people with relevant histories of:

- sexual offending;
- domestic abuse or coercive control;
- serious violence;
- exploitation, trafficking, modern slavery, or abuse of vulnerable people;
- safeguarding breaches;
- offences that create unacceptable risk in the proposed setting.

### Oversight

Every pathway should have:

- probation and prison sign-off;
- employer vetting;
- safeguarding checks;
- health and safety standards;
- union and workforce consultation where public services are involved;
- published data on participation, completion, employment, breaches, reoffending, and complaints.

## Implementation plan

### Year 1: pilots

Start with 10-15 prisons and probation regions:

- open prisons and resettlement prisons for ROTL work;
- community sentence cohorts for food, retrofit, and maintenance projects;
- partnerships with councils, NHS trusts, care providers, farms, food distributors, charities, and further education colleges;
- clear eligibility rules and independent evaluation.

### Year 2: priority public-service pathways

Expand successful pilots into:

- food production and public kitchens;
- care-support training and non-personal care roles;
- retrofit and public estate repair;
- logistics, laundry, and equipment repair for NHS and social care supply chains.

### Year 3: national commissioning

Create national commissioning standards:

- a central register of approved public-service placements;
- standard training modules and qualifications;
- a wage, levy, and release-savings model;
- employer guarantees for interviews or apprenticeships;
- shared data between prisons, probation, DWP, colleges, and employers.

### Year 4+: scale and budget integration

Treat the programme as part of national workforce planning:

- link food work to welfare procurement;
- link care training to the adult social care workforce pathway;
- link retrofit training to housing reform;
- link release employment to probation performance metrics;
- include reoffending reductions and custody days avoided in budget scoring.

## Budget impact

This should be treated as a medium-term saving and workforce policy, not an instant cash generator.

Potential benefits:

- lower reoffending if more prison leavers move into stable work;
- fewer recalls where release is tied to employment, housing, and supervision;
- reduced prison pressure through better use of community and earned-release routes for low-risk people;
- lower recruitment costs for employers in care, food, logistics, and repair sectors;
- lower public procurement costs where prison industries supply useful goods ethically;
- more victim-support and reparation funding through earned-wage levies.

The budget file should therefore score this cautiously:

| Mechanism | Fiscal effect |
|---|---:|
| Direct wage levies from expanded ROTL work | Low tens of millions if scaled nationally |
| Reduced reoffending and recalls | Potentially material, but model cautiously |
| Avoided custody for suitable low-risk cases | High per-person saving where prison is safely avoided |
| Public procurement support | TBD, depends on contracts and safeguards |
| Better employment after release | Dynamic savings through tax, lower benefits, and fewer victims |

The right target is not "prisoners pay for the NHS". That is too crude and too small. The right target is: **people who can safely work should repair public value, gain qualifications, and leave prison less likely to return**.

## Why this belongs in Foundations for the Future

This repo argues for practical systems that reduce downstream failure. Rehabilitation through public service fits that logic.

Food procurement needs workers and logistics. Social care needs people with patience, routine, and training. Housing retrofit needs practical skills. Prisons need purpose and routes out of reoffending. Communities need visible repair.

A Public Service Rehabilitation Corps connects those needs. It gives punishment a productive shape: repay harm, learn discipline, build skill, earn trust, and leave with a real path into work.
