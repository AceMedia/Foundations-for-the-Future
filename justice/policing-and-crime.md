# Policing and Crime

*A forward-looking proposal, judged by what works, not by left or right.*

> **Summary for the reader**
> - **The problem:** confidence in policing has fallen, charge rates for many crimes are low,
>   neighbourhood policing has been hollowed out, and serious violence and domestic abuse remain
>   stubborn.
> - **The options:** more enforcement, more prevention, or both together with restored
>   neighbourhood policing.
> - **Lead option:** **rebuild visible neighbourhood policing, raise charge rates, and pair
>   enforcement with prevention**, without drifting into mass surveillance.
> - **Timeline:** rebuild neighbourhood policing early; build prevention over a parliament.
>
> *Costings are indicative; the detailed figures live in the budget files.*

---

## The problem

The basic deal is that people are kept safe and crimes are dealt with. In too many places that deal
has frayed.

**Confidence has fallen.** Trust in policing has dropped after a run of high-profile failures and the
Casey review's findings on culture and vetting, which makes everything harder, since policing depends
on public consent and cooperation.

**Charge rates are low.** Only about 7.3% of all offences end in a charge or summons, and for most
theft the figure is lower still, so victims feel the system does not respond.[^outcomes]

**Fraud is the biggest crime and the least policed.** Fraud now accounts for around 41% of all crime
against individuals, the single largest category, and rose by about a third in a year, yet it gets a
tiny share of policing.[^fraud] Crime has moved online faster than policing has followed.

**Neighbourhood policing was hollowed out.** The visible, local policing that prevents crime and
builds trust was cut back, and it is the thing people most want back.

**Serious violence and domestic abuse persist.** Knife crime, serious violence, and domestic abuse
remain stubborn and need prevention as well as enforcement.

**The do-nothing baseline.** Leave this and confidence keeps falling, charge rates stay low, and
the temptation grows to reach for mass-surveillance tools that erode liberty without fixing the
basics.

---

## Evidence base

- **Neighbourhood policing works.** Visible, local policing prevents crime and rebuilds trust.
  *Transferable* as the foundation to restore.
- **Prevention reduces serious violence.** Treating serious violence partly as a public-health
  problem, alongside enforcement, reduces it. *Transferable*, linking to
  [health](../health/nhs-and-public-health.md) and [mental health](../health/mental-health.md).
- **Mass surveillance is not the answer.** Blanket tools such as untargeted live facial recognition
  trade liberty for little, and sit badly with the framework's [civil liberties](../governance/civil-liberties.md)
  position. *Relevant* to keeping policing both effective and free.

---

## Options appraisal

**Option 1: Enforcement-led.** More officers and tougher enforcement alone. *Pros:* visible
response. *Cons:* without prevention, demand keeps rising.

**Option 2: Prevention-led.** Focus on the causes of crime. *Pros:* addresses root causes.
*Cons:* on its own it leaves victims feeling unprotected now.

**Option 3: Neighbourhood policing plus prevention *(lead option)*.** Rebuild visible local
policing, raise charge rates, and pair enforcement with prevention, without mass surveillance.
*Pros:* restores trust, responds to victims, and tackles causes. *Cons:* needs officers,
investigation capacity, and patience. **Recommended.**

---

## The proposals (under the lead option)

1. **Rebuild neighbourhood policing.** Put visible, local officers back into communities, the
   single thing that most rebuilds trust and prevents crime.
2. **Raise charge rates.** Invest in investigation so more crimes that matter to people, theft,
   assault, burglary, end in a charge.
3. **Tackle serious violence and domestic abuse.** Combine enforcement with prevention, treating
   serious violence partly as a public-health issue and taking domestic abuse seriously.
4. **Keep drugs a health issue.** Maintain the health-led approach to drugs set out in
   [health](../health/nhs-and-public-health.md), freeing police time for crimes with victims.
5. **Police fraud and cyber crime properly.** Resource the investigation of fraud and online crime in
   proportion to its scale, with a stronger national capability and better joint work with banks and
   platforms, since it is now the largest crime type and the least addressed.
6. **Rebuild standards and vetting.** Act on the Casey review: proper vetting, misconduct that
   actually leads to removal, and a culture that earns consent, especially from women and minority
   communities.
7. **Effective, not intrusive.** Use technology in targeted, accountable ways, and reject untargeted
   mass surveillance such as blanket live facial recognition, in line with
   [civil liberties](../governance/civil-liberties.md).

## Costs and benefits

Neighbourhood policing and better investigation cost money, but they cut crime, rebuild trust, and
reduce the larger downstream costs of crime and reoffending, which ties to the
[rehabilitation chapter](./rehabilitation-through-public-service.md). Figures belong in the
budget files.

---

## Delivery and timeline

- **Years 1-3:** rebuild neighbourhood policing; invest in investigation to raise charge rates.
- **Years 3-7:** scale prevention of serious violence; embed the health-led drugs approach.
- **Years 7-10:** higher confidence, higher charge rates, and lower serious violence.

---

## Risks and mitigations

- **Falling back on enforcement alone.** *Mitigation:* fund prevention as part of the core, not an
  add-on.
- **Surveillance creep.** *Mitigation:* targeted, accountable technology only, no blanket facial
  recognition.
- **Workforce and morale.** *Mitigation:* invest in officers, training, and standards.

## Consultation questions

1. How do we rebuild neighbourhood policing fastest?
2. What raises charge rates for the crimes people care most about?
3. What prevention best reduces serious violence and domestic abuse?
4. Where is the line on police use of surveillance technology?
5. How do we resource fraud and cyber policing in proportion to their scale?
6. What will it take to rebuild standards, vetting, and public trust after the Casey review?
