# International Comparisons

This file benchmarks the framework's proposals against countries that have tried similar reforms,
with the figures that make each comparison concrete. The point is to credibility-check the savings
ranges in the other budget files, not to claim other countries transfer wholesale.

---

## Housing

- **Finland (Housing First).** Giving homeless people a permanent home first, with support, saves
  society an estimated **€15,000 per person per year**, and up to €52,000 in the heaviest cases,
  through reduced emergency healthcare, crisis services, and criminal-justice contact.[^finland] The
  honest caveat is that intensive on-site support is itself costly (around €14,800 per person a
  year), so the net saving depends on how support is designed. Finland is the only EU country where
  homelessness is still falling. *Transferable* as the case that securing housing first is cheaper
  than managing the crisis; backs [housing-savings.md](./housing-savings.md) and the welfare chapter.
- **Vienna (social housing).** About **60%** of Viennese live in social or limited-profit housing,
  and the city directly owns roughly **220,000** municipal flats, the largest public stock of any
  European city; rents are stable and capped (a two-bedroom flat was about €542 a month in
  2023).[^vienna] *Transferable* in the mixed-income, long-term stewardship model; the caveat is
  that Vienna built its stock over a century, so Britain would rebuild over decades.

## Welfare and care

- **Germany (long-term care insurance, Pflegeversicherung).** Care is funded by a dedicated
  social-insurance contribution of **3.6% of gross salary**, split between employer and employee,
  with a small surcharge for childless adults.[^germanyltc] *Transferable* as a transparent,
  shared way to fund care without forcing individuals to spend down their assets; the model for the
  [adult social care](../health/adult-social-care.md) and end-of-life chapters.
- **Japan (long-term care insurance, Kaigo Hoken).** A mandatory public scheme that everyone aged
  40 and over pays into funds care for older people.[^japanltc] *Transferable* as the same
  shared-cost principle, with contributions beginning in mid-life.

## Health

- **Portugal (drug decriminalisation).** Decriminalising possession and investing in treatment cut
  drug deaths, HIV infections, and the prison population without raising overall use.[^portugal]
  Note this is *decriminalisation*, not a commercial legal market. *Transferable* as the basis for
  treating drugs as health; the vice chapter additionally proposes a regulated, taxed cannabis
  market on the Canadian model, which is a different and bigger step.

## Justice

- **Norway (rehabilitation prisons).** Norway's two-year reoffending rate is around **20%**,
  against roughly **60%** in England and Wales, and its prisons run at about 73% capacity versus
  104% here.[^norway] *Transferable* as evidence that rehabilitation and humane conditions cut both
  reoffending and cost; anchors [justice-savings.md](./justice-savings.md) and the rehabilitation
  chapter.

## Governance

- **Estonia (digital government).** Digital signatures and e-government save Estonia about **2% of
  GDP a year** (roughly $500m) and around **five working days per person per year**.[^estonia]
  *Transferable* in the service design; the framework adopts the efficiency lessons while rejecting
  the mandatory national digital ID that Estonia built its system on.

---

## How these credibility-check the headline ranges

The savings ranges in the other budget files are deliberately conservative against these benchmarks.
The housing figures sit well below Finland's per-person saving scaled to the UK caseload; the
justice reoffending case is anchored on the Norway gap rather than assumed; the care-funding chapters
point at the German and Japanese contribution rates rather than inventing a UK number; and the
governance savings are a small fraction of Estonia's 2%-of-GDP result. Where a UK proposal goes
*beyond* a comparator (a commercial cannabis market versus Portuguese decriminalisation, or rebuilding
Vienna-scale public stock from a low base), the files say so rather than borrowing the comparator's
evidence.
