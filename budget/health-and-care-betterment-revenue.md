# Health and Care Betterment Fund Revenue

This appendix separates **earmarked vice-tax receipts** from **service reform savings**.

That distinction matters. If the state chooses to tax regulated vice more honestly and recycle part of the proceeds into health and care, that is a funding decision, not an efficiency saving. It should therefore sit alongside the savings files, not inside them.

## Core receipt base

The cleanest starting point is the vice-duty base that already exists in the UK tax system.

| Revenue stream | Official current scale | Source basis | Policy note |
|---|---:|---|---|
| Alcohol duties | **£11.9bn** in 2025/26 | OBR estimate | Large and relatively stable cash base |
| Tobacco duties | **£8.0bn** in 2025/26 | OBR estimate | Large base, but expected to decline over time as smoking falls |
| Betting and gaming duties | **£4.0bn** in 2025/26 | OBR estimate | Smaller base, but OBR expects receipts to rise over its forecast |
| **Core vice-duty base** | **£23.9bn** | Sum of the above | Main base for earmark scenarios |

There is also a separate gambling-harm funding stream:

| Separate stream | Official current scale | Source basis | Treatment |
|---|---:|---|---|
| Statutory gambling levy | **Just under £120m** in its first year | DCMS | Already ring-fenced for gambling-harm research, prevention, and treatment. Do not count this twice inside the broader fund. |

This means the main funding question is not whether Britain already taxes vice. It does. The question is whether a published share of those receipts should be routed visibly into health, recovery, social care, and end-of-life dignity.

---

## Direction of travel in the current tax base

The current vice-duty base is not perfectly stable, so the fund should be designed around that reality.

| Stream | Current official direction | Funding implication |
|---|---|---|
| Alcohol duties | OBR expects cash receipts to keep rising, while staying broadly flat as a share of GDP | Suitable as part of the recurring base |
| Tobacco duties | OBR expects cash receipts to fall over the forecast period as consumption declines | Useful in the short and medium term, but do not build permanent promises on tobacco alone |
| Betting and gaming duties | OBR expects receipts to rise over its forecast | Growing but smaller base; also sits alongside the separate gambling levy |

So the right funding rule is:

- use a **percentage earmark of actual receipts**, not a fixed cash promise detached from the tax base;
- use a **rolling average** for recurring commitments;
- treat upside from any new regulated market as **additional** rather than already spent.

## Earmark scenarios

The policy already assumes vice-tax receipts should go first to a **Health and Care Betterment Fund**. These scenarios show what that means in cash terms if applied to the current **£23.9bn** duty base.

| Earmark share of existing vice duties | Annual receipts redirected |
|---:|---:|
| 5% | **£1.195bn** |
| 10% | **£2.390bn** |
| 15% | **£3.585bn** |
| 20% | **£4.780bn** |

Rounded for public-facing use, that is roughly:

- **£1.2bn** at 5%;
- **£2.4bn** at 10%;
- **£3.6bn** at 15%;
- **£4.8bn** at 20%.

These are **earmarked receipts**, not savings. They should sit outside the repo's core savings range.

## Recommended allocation rule

The current framework uses this split:

- **40% national health and recovery**
- **25% social care and end-of-life dignity**
- **20% local community health**
- **15% prevention and medical access**

Applied to the earmark scenarios above:

| Scenario | Health and recovery (40%) | Social care and end-of-life dignity (25%) | Local community health (20%) | Prevention and medical access (15%) |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 5% earmark = £1.195bn | £0.478bn | £0.299bn | £0.239bn | £0.179bn |
| 10% earmark = £2.390bn | £0.956bn | £0.598bn | £0.478bn | £0.359bn |
| 15% earmark = £3.585bn | £1.434bn | £0.896bn | £0.717bn | £0.538bn |
| 20% earmark = £4.780bn | £1.912bn | £1.195bn | £0.956bn | £0.717bn |

This is enough to turn the fund from a slogan into a budget mechanism.

In practical terms:

- the **social care and end-of-life share** can contribute to dementia supplements, care caps, dignity allowances, hospice-at-home, and the End-of-Life Support Passport;
- the **health and recovery share** can support addiction treatment, mental health, alcohol treatment, smoking cessation, and pain-management alternatives;
- the **local community health share** can support community kitchens, neighbourhood health programmes, recovery spaces, youth provision, and other visible local supports;
- the **prevention and medical access share** can support prescribed medical access, early intervention, and targeted public-health programmes.

---

## Local distribution rule

The local share should not be left to annual ministerial discretion.

Within the **20% local community health** allocation, a simple formula should be published:

- **70% by population**
- **20% by deprivation and health-harm indicators**
- **10% by care pressure and service demand**, such as older population share, delayed discharge pressure, or addiction-treatment demand

This keeps the fund legible while still directing more money to places carrying higher health and care burdens.

If a local area hosts regulated vice sales, it should see a visible local return in health, recovery, and community infrastructure rather than only sending receipts to the centre.

---

## What each earmark level can credibly support

| Earmark | What it can plausibly do |
|---|---|
| **5%** | Fund food, warmth, adaptation, support-passport, and dignity-allowance measures, while making a material contribution to palliative and recovery services |
| **10%** | Fund the lighter dignity package plus a major contribution to dementia support and community end-of-life care |
| **15%** | Moves close to the scale of previous social-care cap cost markers and could underwrite a much more serious national care package |
| **20%** | Creates room for a large care-and-dignity settlement, depending on final design and what is funded from general taxation alongside it |

This does **not** mean vice receipts should carry the whole burden of social-care reform. It means they can visibly finance a meaningful share of the repair.

## Market-specific treatment

### Alcohol

Alcohol is already a large receipt stream. It is the clearest source for a recurring earmark.

The policy design should preserve the existing distinction between:

- lower-strength and higher-strength products; and
- supervised social drinking in pubs and venues versus bulk discount retail.

The revenue lesson is simple: alcohol can help fund the recurring base, but the tax system should still reflect relative harm and support safer, regulated social settings.

### Tobacco

Tobacco should remain heavily taxed, but the fund must recognise that this base is not durable forever. OBR expects tobacco-duty receipts to decline over time as smoking falls and substitution changes.

That means:

- tobacco can support the fund now;
- tobacco should not be treated as a permanently rising revenue source;
- long-run commitments should be set against a broader vice-duty basket, not tobacco alone.

### Betting and gaming

Betting and gaming duties already raise around **£4bn**. Alongside that, the statutory gambling levy has already introduced a separate ring-fenced stream for gambling-harm work.

The clean rule is:

- **gambling levy** = dedicated gambling-harm stream;
- **betting and gaming duties** = part of the broader Health and Care Betterment Fund.

That avoids muddling a harm-specific levy with the wider vice-duty basket.

## Future regulated adult-use market scenarios

The framework also allows for future regulated adult-use markets, especially cannabis. But those revenues should be treated more cautiously than existing duties.

Official evidence shows the underlying demand is real. ONS reported that in year ending March 2025:

- **6.5%** of people aged 16 to 59 in England and Wales used cannabis in the last 12 months;
- **1.1%** used hallucinogens.

So a regulated market would not be creating demand from nothing. It would be attempting to move existing demand out of illegal supply and into a licensed, taxed system.

But the budgeting rule should still be conservative:

- do **not** bank adult-use cannabis receipts as core funding before legal design exists;
- do **not** bank psychedelic-market receipts at all before a much narrower regulatory model is decided;
- score future markets only once legislation, tax structure, licensing, and expected legal-market capture are clear.

The correct formula is:

```text
Annual adult-use receipts = legal sales x effective duty rate x legal-market capture rate
```

That means future market scenarios can be discussed now, but should remain **TBD** in the central funding line until the policy is much more concrete.

## Medical access and low-duty treatment

Prescribed medical products should not be taxed like recreational vice.

The NHS position is already narrow: medical cannabis can only be prescribed by a specialist hospital doctor, or under specialist supervision, and only for a small number of patients.

That supports a simple rule:

- **prescribed medical cannabis and similar clinically justified products should face low or zero vice duty**;
- adult-use products should carry the fuller vice-duty burden;
- any future domestic medical supply should be treated as part of the medical system, not as a disguised recreational market.

This matters for fairness. The state should not tell patients they may receive a specialist-prescribed product and then tax them as if they were ordinary vice consumers.

## Funding discipline

The fund should operate under five discipline rules:

1. **No double counting with savings.** Earmarked receipts stay separate from NHS, housing, welfare, or justice savings.
2. **No double counting the gambling levy.** It is already separately ring-fenced.
3. **No scoring future cannabis or psychedelic receipts as core funding before legal design.**
4. **No taxing prescribed medical access as if it were recreational vice.**
5. **No over-promising on declining tobacco receipts.**

For recurring commitments such as dementia support or care guarantees, the safest rule is:

- fund the recurring baseline from no more than **75% of the trailing three-year average** earmark yield;
- use any excess or new-market upside for pilots, one-off capital, reserves, or service expansion after receipts are proven.

That makes the fund more fiscally honest.
