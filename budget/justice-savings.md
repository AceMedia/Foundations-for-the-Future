# Justice Savings and Rehabilitation Costings

This appendix models the fiscal logic behind [rehabilitation-through-public-service.md](../justice/rehabilitation-through-public-service.md). The aim is to show where savings are realistic, where direct revenue is modest, and where further Ministry of Justice modelling is needed.

The central point is simple: prison work should not be sold as a way to make prisoners "pay for the NHS". The direct value of labour and levies is too small for that. The serious fiscal case is that structured work, qualifications, and release employment can reduce reoffending, recalls, welfare dependency, and future custody pressure.

---

## Current baseline pressures

| Pressure | Current evidence | Why it matters |
|---|---:|---|
| HMPPS spending | HMPPS net expenditure in 2024/25: **£5.6bn**, with prisons around **£3.9bn** of resource spending | Custody is a high-cost public service |
| Cost per prisoner | 2023/24 overall cost per prisoner in England and Wales: **£53,801/year** | Every avoided prison year has high potential value |
| Cost per prison place | 2023/24 overall cost per prison place: **£56,987/year** | Capacity pressure drives expensive emergency decisions |
| Employment after release | In the year to March 2024, **19.3%** employed six weeks after release and **31.1%** after six months | Most prison leavers still lack stable work |
| Reoffending | January-March 2024 proven reoffending rate: **28.9%** overall; **37.1%** for adults released from custody or starting court orders | Reoffending creates victim, court, police, probation, and prison costs |
| Short custodial sentences | Adults released from custodial sentences under 12 months had **66.0%** proven reoffending | Short custody is a priority target for alternatives and structured resettlement |
| Existing paid work levy | In 2024/25, **1,080** active prisoners under levy arrangements earned **£22.0m**, raising **£3.9m** for Victim Support | Paid work already exists but at small scale |
| Community Payback | Courts can impose **40-300 hours** unpaid work | There is already a reparative work route outside custody |

## What the programme funds

The **Public Service Rehabilitation Corps** would support low-risk people in custody and people on community sentences through:

- food production, kitchens, packing, storage, and logistics;
- care-support training and non-personal support roles;
- retrofit, repairs, recycling, and public estate maintenance;
- Community Payback projects tied to qualifications and completion;
- employer-linked release pathways;
- wage levies, victim support, and release savings.

This requires funding for training, supervision, transport, safeguarding, equipment, insurance, employer coordination, and evaluation.

Eligibility should follow the policy line in the justice chapter:

- broadest access for low-level non-violent offences after risk assessment;
- cautious but possible access for non-violent theft and similar acquisitive offending where behaviour is stable;
- case-by-case access only for anything above that seriousness;
- exclusion from public-facing and care-facing routes for serious violence, sexual offending, domestic abuse, coercive control, exploitation, terrorism, or high safeguarding risk.

Sentence incentives should be proportional to both the offence and the effort required by the task. Higher-effort, higher-value work can earn more credit, but only within a ceiling set by public safety and offence seriousness.

## Savings mechanisms

| Mechanism | Fiscal effect | Confidence |
|---|---|---|
| Lower reoffending | Avoided police, court, victim, probation, prison, and social costs | Plausible, needs cohort modelling |
| Fewer recalls | More stable release with work, routine, and supervision | Plausible, needs HMPPS data |
| Avoided custody for suitable low-risk cases | Community sentence or earned-release route avoids high custody cost | Strong per-person value if public safety is maintained |
| Better employment after release | Higher tax receipts, lower benefit dependency, fewer crisis interventions | Plausible, dynamic |
| Wage levies | Direct victim-support/reparation receipts from paid work | Real but modest |
| Public procurement support | Lower costs for food, repair, recycling, and equipment work | TBD and must avoid undercutting workers |

## Direct wage levy scenarios

The current levy data gives a useful scale check.

In 2024/25:

- active prisoners under levy arrangements: **1,080**;
- gross earnings: **£22.0m**;
- levy raised: **£3.9m**.

Approximate implied averages:

| Measure | Approximate value |
|---|---:|
| Gross earnings per active prisoner | £20,370/year |
| Levy raised per active prisoner | £3,611/year |
| Levy as share of gross earnings | 17.7% |

Illustrative scale-up:

| Active paid workers | Gross earnings at current average | Levy at current average | Policy meaning |
|---:|---:|---:|---|
| 2,500 | £50.9m | £9.0m | Modest expansion |
| 5,000 | £101.9m | £18.1m | Serious national route |
| 10,000 | £203.7m | £36.1m | Large but still modest against prison budget |
| 20,000 | £407.4m | £72.2m | Ambitious, requires major employer capacity |

Budget treatment:

- **Direct levy receipts:** low tens of millions if scaled.
- **Victim support value:** important, but not a main Exchequer saving.
- **Release savings:** prisoners should keep enough earnings to reduce destitution on release.

## Custody-day avoided scenarios

The biggest direct fiscal lever is avoiding unnecessary custody for suitable low-risk cases or reducing future custody through lower reoffending.

Using the 2023/24 overall cost per prisoner of **£53,801/year**:

| Custody years avoided | Gross notional value |
|---:|---:|
| 500 years | £26.9m |
| 1,000 years | £53.8m |
| 2,500 years | £134.5m |
| 5,000 years | £269.0m |
| 10,000 years | £538.0m |

Important caution:

- not all avoided custody is cashable immediately;
- prison fixed costs remain unless capacity can be closed or pressure reduced;
- some value is capacity release rather than direct budget saving;
- public safety must decide eligibility, not savings targets.

Budget treatment:

- **Gross value:** use cost per prisoner as a scale marker.
- **Cashable saving:** likely lower, especially in early years.
- **Capacity benefit:** still valuable if it avoids emergency prison expansion, overcrowding, or unsafe churn.

## Reoffending reduction scenarios

The programme should be judged by whether it reduces reoffending among participants compared with matched cohorts.

Illustrative model:

```text
Avoided reoffences = participant cohort x baseline reoffending rate x relative reduction
```

Example for 10,000 participants with a 37.1% baseline adult custody/court-order reoffending rate:

| Relative reoffending reduction | Avoided reoffenders | Budget meaning |
|---:|---:|---|
| 5% | 186 | Small but useful |
| 10% | 371 | Meaningful if sustained |
| 15% | 557 | Strong programme effect |
| 20% | 742 | Very strong, should be evidenced before claiming |

For short-sentence cohorts, where baseline reoffending can be much higher, the potential effect is larger, but so is delivery risk.

Budget treatment:

- model separately for custody leavers, community orders, suspended sentence orders, and short sentences;
- include victim costs, police, courts, probation, prisons, health, housing, and welfare;
- do not count savings until an evaluated pilot shows effect size.

## Programme cost blocks

Savings only matter after delivery costs.

| Cost block | What it includes | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Training | Food hygiene, horticulture, logistics, construction cards, care-awareness modules, first aid | Should use recognised qualifications |
| Supervision | Prison staff, probation, workplace mentors, safeguarding leads | Highest recurring cost |
| Transport | ROTL travel, community project transport, secure movement where needed | Depends on local geography |
| Equipment | PPE, tools, kitchens, storage, horticulture, repair workshops | Can overlap with procurement programmes |
| Employer coordination | New Futures Network-type brokerage, interviews, placements | Essential for jobs after release |
| Safeguarding and vetting | DBS where relevant, risk assessment, exclusions, monitoring | Especially important for care-adjacent work |
| Evaluation | Matched cohort tracking, reoffending, employment, breaches | Needed before claiming savings |

Illustrative per-participant annual cost bands:

| Model | Annual cost per participant | When it fits |
|---|---:|---|
| Light | £1,000-£2,000 | Classroom training, basic community projects |
| Standard | £3,000-£5,000 | Regular supervised work with qualifications |
| Intensive | £6,000-£10,000 | ROTL, employer placements, high supervision, transport |

Budget treatment:

- start with pilots, not national assumptions;
- include supervision honestly;
- separate custody participants from community-sentence participants;
- avoid comparing unpaid work hours directly with market labour rates.

## Public procurement and service value

The programme can support public procurement, but only under strict rules.

Possible outputs:

- food growing, packing, and distribution;
- public kitchen support;
- furniture repair;
- mobility aid cleaning and repair;
- recycling and reuse;
- grounds maintenance;
- retrofit support under qualified supervision;
- NHS and social care supply-chain support.

Budget treatment:

- count only verified public procurement savings;
- subtract supervision, transport, and training costs;
- avoid displacement of paid workers;
- do not use prison labour to break strikes or undercut local businesses;
- publish contracts and social value metrics.

---

## Suggested pilot model

### Phase 1: 10-15 pilot areas

Target:

- open prisons and resettlement prisons;
- community sentence cohorts;
- areas with food procurement, retrofit, or care-support shortages;
- employers willing to offer interviews or apprenticeships.

Metrics:

- attendance;
- completion;
- qualifications;
- breaches and recalls;
- employment at 6 weeks and 6 months;
- reoffending at 12 and 24 months;
- victim-support levy raised;
- participant release savings;
- public procurement value;
- participant and employer feedback.

### Phase 2: scale what works

Only scale pathways that show:

- lower breach rates;
- better employment;
- safe safeguarding record;
- credible cost per successful outcome;
- no evidence of worker displacement.

## Indicative budget presentation

Suggested line for [summary-of-savings.md](./summary-of-savings.md):

| Area | Mechanism | Budget treatment |
|---|---|---|
| Justice rehabilitation | Public-service work, skills, ROTL employment, Community Payback, sentence compliance incentives | Direct levy income modest; larger savings TBD through lower reoffending, fewer recalls, and custody days avoided |

Suggested wording:

> Justice rehabilitation should be scored cautiously. Wage levies may raise low tens of millions if scaled, but the main fiscal case is avoided future offending and custody pressure. Savings should only be counted after evaluated pilots show reoffending, employment, and recall improvements.

## Next modelling tasks

- Split target cohorts by custody status, sentence length, offence category, risk level, and release window.
- Build a proportional incentive matrix by offence seriousness, task difficulty, qualification level, and public value.
- Estimate eligible population for ROTL paid work and community-sentence public-service pathways.
- Model direct levy receipts at 2,500, 5,000, 10,000, and 20,000 active workers.
- Estimate programme cost per participant by pathway.
- Build a custody-days avoided model with cashable and non-cashable capacity value separated.
- Model reoffending reduction using matched cohorts and conservative effect sizes.
- Estimate employment tax and benefit effects at 6 weeks, 6 months, 12 months, and 24 months.
- Create safeguards metrics: breaches, absconds, complaints, safeguarding incidents, worker displacement.
- Link justice work outputs to food procurement, care-support, retrofit, and public repair programmes.
