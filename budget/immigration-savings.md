# Immigration and Asylum Savings

This file collects **fiscal savings and avoided costs** from immigration and asylum reform:
faster, fairer processing; ending reliance on asylum hotels; enforcement aimed at smugglers
rather than migrants; and expanded legal work routes. It backs the proposals in
[`../immigration/immigration.md`](../immigration/immigration.md).

---

## Key mechanisms

- **End asylum-hotel reliance.** Faster decisions move people out of expensive accommodation
  and into either settled status or removal.
- **Clear the backlog.** Cut the per-case cost of years in limbo.
- **Target smugglers, not migrants.** Reduce repeat removals, detention, and enforcement churn.
- **Expanded legal work routes.** Tax and National Insurance from legal work, and less grey
  economy.

---

## Verified anchor figures

- Asylum support cost around **£4.0bn in 2024-25**, with Home Office accommodation around
  **£2.7bn**.[^support]
- Around **36,300** people were in asylum hotels by September 2025, down about 35% from a peak
  near **56,000** in September 2023; hotels fell from 400 to 210, which the government says cut
  the annual cost by about **£500m**.[^hotels]
- The backlog awaiting an initial decision was around **70,000** by mid-2025, down from a 2023
  peak of about **175,000**; a further **51,000** awaited an appeal hearing.[^backlog]
- Hotel accommodation costs roughly **£150 per person per day**.[^support]
- The Rwanda scheme cost hundreds of millions of pounds while removing very few people.[^rwanda]

---

## Indicative saving categories

| Mechanism | Baseline pressure | Indicative annual saving |
|---|---|---|
| End asylum-hotel reliance | ~£2.7bn accommodation within ~£4bn support[^support] | £0.8–1.2bn |
| Streamlined casework and appeals | caseworker and tribunal overhead | £0.2–0.3bn |
| Enforcement aimed at smugglers | repeat removals, detention | £0.1–0.2bn |
| Tax from expanded legal work | grey economy displaced | £0.5bn+ *(dynamic)* |

**Indicative net saving ~£1–2bn/yr**, after upfront investment in caseworkers and judges
(roughly £0.2–0.3bn) and in expanded legal routes and resettlement (roughly £0.1–0.2bn).

---

## Reinvestment, not just banking

Per the framework, part of the saving should be reinvested **upstream** in defence, logistics,
and humanitarian capacity rather than simply banked, because upstream capacity is usually cheaper
than years of inland accommodation and repeat removals. See
[`../defence/defence-and-aid-resilience.md`](../defence/defence-and-aid-resilience.md).

## Next steps

- Cross-link to courts and tribunal capacity, since the asylum-appeals backlog sits in the
  tribunals system (see [`../justice/courts-and-legal-system.md`](../justice/courts-and-legal-system.md)).
