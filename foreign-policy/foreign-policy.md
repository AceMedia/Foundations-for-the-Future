# Foreign Policy and Britain's Global Role

*A forward-looking proposal, judged by what works, not by left or right.*

> **Summary for the reader**
> - **The problem:** Britain's global strategy is unsettled. Aid has been cut and reorganised, our
>   role since leaving the EU is still being defined, and foreign policy, defence, trade, and
>   migration are too often run in separate silos.
> - **The options:** set aid by a fixed percentage, or by strategic goals and results.
> - **Lead option:** **a joined-up strategy with strategic, results-based aid**, focused on
>   preventing crises upstream, working with allies, and tying foreign policy to defence, trade,
>   and migration.
> - **Timeline:** set the strategy early; build capability over a parliament and beyond.
>
> *Figures here are first-draft and will be checked in the budget-accuracy pass (#21).*

## The problem

A coherent global strategy underpins both our security and our prosperity, and right now it is
hard to point to one.

**Aid has been cut and churned.** Spending was reduced from 0.7% to 0.5% of national income and
the development department was merged into the Foreign Office,[^aid] leaving programmes disrupted
and our influence diminished, without a clear account of what the money is now for.

**Our role is undefined.** Since leaving the EU, Britain's place in the world is still being worked
out, between Atlantic alliance, European neighbourhood, and global reach.

**Everything is siloed.** Foreign policy, defence, trade, and migration are run separately, so we
react to events rather than shaping them, and miss the chances to act upstream that the
[defence and resilience](../defence/defence-and-aid-resilience.md) and
[immigration](../immigration/immigration.md) chapters point to.

**The do-nothing baseline.** Leave this and Britain keeps spending on downstream failure,
crises we could have helped prevent, repeat removals, emergency responses, while its global voice
fades.

---

## Evidence base

- **Upstream prevention is cheaper than downstream failure.** Helping stabilise regions and
  prevent crises costs less than dealing with their consequences at home, the core argument of the
  [defence and resilience chapter](../defence/defence-and-aid-resilience.md). *Transferable* as the
  organising idea for aid and foreign policy.
- **Results-based aid focuses spending.** Judging aid by measurable outcomes rather than a spending
  total directs money to what works. *Transferable*, with honest evaluation.
- **Alliances multiply influence.** Working through NATO and with close partners gives Britain
  reach it cannot have alone. *Transferable* as the basis of strategy.

---

## Options appraisal

**Option 1: A fixed aid percentage.** Set aid as a fixed share of national income. *Pros:* clear
and predictable. *Cons:* focuses on the input rather than the result, and the headline figure
becomes the argument instead of the impact.

**Option 2: Strategic, results-based aid *(lead option)*.** Set aid by strategic goals and
measurable results, concentrated on preventing crises upstream and tied to defence, trade, and
migration. *Pros:* spending follows impact and strategy, and joins up the levers of national power.
*Cons:* needs honest evaluation so "strategic" does not become an excuse to cut. **Recommended.**

---

## The proposals (under the lead option)

1. **Set aid by strategy and results.** Direct development spending to clear goals, above all
   preventing crises upstream, and judge it by measurable outcomes rather than a fixed percentage.
2. **Join up the levers.** Run foreign policy, defence, trade, and migration as one strategy, so
   diplomacy, aid, and capability reinforce each other.
3. **Invest in prevention and capability.** Build the logistics, engineering, and field-medicine
   capacity to help upstream early, as set out in
   [defence and resilience](../defence/defence-and-aid-resilience.md).
4. **Work through alliances.** Anchor strategy in NATO and close partnerships, and rebuild soft
   power through culture, education, and example.
5. **Be honest about results.** Publish what aid achieves, so results-based does not become a cover
   for simply spending less.

## Costs and benefits

Results-based, prevention-focused spending aims to get more from each pound and to avoid the far
larger downstream costs of crises that reach our shores. The figures and the balance between aid,
defence, and diplomacy belong in the budget-accuracy pass (#21).

## Delivery and timeline

- **Years 1-2:** set the joined-up strategy and the results framework for aid; rebuild upstream
  capability.
- **Years 2-5:** concentrate aid on prevention and impact; deepen alliances and soft power.
- **Years 5-10:** a coherent global role that shapes events rather than reacting to them.

---

## Risks and mitigations

- **"Strategic" used as cover for cuts.** *Mitigation:* publish results and hold spending to its
  stated goals.
- **Siloed delivery returning.** *Mitigation:* a single strategy and shared objectives across
  departments.
- **Losing influence by under-spending.** *Mitigation:* measure influence and impact, not just the
  total, and invest where it counts.

4. How do we rebuild soft power most effectively?
