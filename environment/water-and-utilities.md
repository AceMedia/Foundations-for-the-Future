# Water and Utilities

*A forward-looking proposal, judged by what works, not by left or right.*

> **Summary for the reader**
> - **The problem:** water companies have paid out dividends and loaded up debt while sewage
>   pours into rivers and seas and pipes age. People pay the bills and still get the pollution.
> - **The options:** public-interest ownership, tougher regulation while keeping private owners,
>   or a move to mutual and regional models.
> - **Lead option:** **bring water into public-interest ownership**, run it for service and the
>   environment rather than shareholders, and rebuild trust through transparency and investment.
> - **Timeline:** transition over a parliament, with investment ramping from the start.
>
> *Costings are indicative; the detailed figures live in the budget files.*

---

## The problem

Water is a natural monopoly and a basic essential. You cannot shop around for a different set of
pipes, which makes how it is owned and regulated a public question, not just a commercial one.

**Sewage in the rivers.** Spills of raw sewage into rivers and seas have become a national
scandal, a daily sign that the system is not delivering the basics.

**Money out, investment short.** Since privatisation, water companies have paid large dividends
and taken on heavy debt, while investment in pipes and treatment has lagged what is needed.[^water]

**Bills with no choice.** Customers pay rising bills to a monopoly they cannot leave, and then
watch it pollute. That is corrosive to trust in the whole model.

**The do-nothing baseline.** Leave this and the spills continue, the investment gap grows, debt
piles up, and the public concludes, fairly, that a private monopoly is extracting value while the
environment and bill-payers lose.

---

## Evidence base

- **Public and non-profit water works elsewhere.** Many countries, and Welsh Water's not-for-profit
  model at home, run water for service and reinvestment rather than shareholder return.
  *Transferable* as evidence that water does not need private equity owners to function.
- **Regulation alone has not fixed it.** Years of fines and targets have not stopped the spills,
  which suggests the ownership model, not just the rulebook, is the problem. *Relevant* to choosing
  the lead option below.

---

## Options appraisal

**Option 1: Public-interest ownership *(lead option)*.** Bring water into public or non-profit
ownership, run for service and the environment, with profits reinvested rather than paid out.
*Pros:* aligns the system with the public interest, ends the dividend-while-dumping problem, and
lowers the cost of capital. *Cons:* transition is complex and must be handled at fair value and
without rewarding failure. **Recommended.**

**Option 2: Tough regulation, keep private.** Keep private owners but with real penalties, binding
investment duties, and a far tougher regulator. *Pros:* avoids a transition. *Cons:* the same
incentives remain, and regulation alone has not delivered.

**Option 3: Mutual and regional models.** Move toward customer-owned mutuals or regional
public-benefit bodies over time. *Pros:* local accountability and reinvestment. *Cons:* slower and
patchier to set up. *A possible route into the lead option.*

---

## The proposals (under the lead option)

1. **Bring water into public-interest ownership.** Move water companies to public or non-profit
   ownership, at fair value and without rewarding failure, run for service and the environment.
2. **Reinvest, do not extract.** Profits go back into pipes, treatment, and cleaner rivers rather
   than out to shareholders, lowering the cost of capital over time.
3. **End the sewage scandal.** Set hard duties to cut spills, with transparent real-time
   monitoring so the public can see the record.
4. **Honest bills.** Fund the investment backlog fairly over time, with clear protection for
   low-income customers, and show people what their bills are buying.
5. **Join up with the environment.** Run water alongside flood management and river health, in step
   with the future environment and farming chapter.

---

## Costs and benefits

Public-interest ownership lowers the cost of capital and ends the leakage of bill income to
dividends, freeing money for investment. The transition has a cost and must be valued fairly, but
the prize is cleaner rivers, reliable supply, and a model the public trusts. The figures belong in
the budget files.

---

## Delivery and timeline

- **Years 1-2:** legislate the move to public-interest ownership; set hard anti-spill duties and
  real-time monitoring.
- **Years 2-5:** complete the transition; ramp investment in pipes and treatment.
- **Years 5-10:** measurable falls in spills and a restored, trusted water system.

---

## Risks and mitigations

- **Transition cost and legal complexity.** *Mitigation:* fair-value transfer, phased, without
  rewarding failure or excess debt.
- **Investment still falling short.** *Mitigation:* binding investment duties and transparent
  monitoring.
- **Bills rising too fast.** *Mitigation:* phase the catch-up and protect low-income customers.

---

## Consultation questions

1. What is the right model: full public ownership, non-profit, or regional mutuals?
2. How should companies be valued in the transition, without rewarding failure?
3. What anti-spill duties and monitoring would actually end the scandal?
4. How do we fund the investment backlog fairly across bill-payers?
