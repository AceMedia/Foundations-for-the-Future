# Environment, Farming and Land

*A forward-looking proposal, judged by what works, not by left or right.*

> **Summary for the reader**
> - **The problem:** farmers are caught between volatile prices and an unfinished subsidy shift,
>   food security is exposed, nature is in decline, and flooding is a growing bill.
> - **The options:** pay mainly for food, mainly for nature, or for both together.
> - **Lead option:** **pay farmers for nature and food together**, so the countryside produces
>   both secure domestic food and a recovering environment.
> - **Timeline:** settle the subsidy framework early; build over a decade.
>
> *Costings are indicative; the detailed figures live in the budget files.*

---

## The problem

Land has to do several jobs at once: grow our food, hold water back from towns, store carbon, and
support nature. The system meant to manage that is mid-transition and struggling.

**Farming is squeezed.** Margins are tight, prices are volatile, and the move away from the old
area-based subsidies to a new system has left many farmers uncertain about how they will be paid.

**Food security is exposed.** Britain imports a large share of its food, which leaves it
vulnerable to shocks, and domestic production needs to be viable to be secure.

**Nature is in decline.** Britain is one of the more nature-depleted countries, with falling
wildlife and degraded habitats.

**Flooding and water are linked to land.** How land upstream is managed shapes flood risk and
river health downstream, connecting directly to [water and utilities](./water-and-utilities.md).

**The do-nothing baseline.** Leave this and farming stays precarious, food security weakens,
nature keeps declining, and flood bills rise, while the subsidy transition drifts.

---

## Evidence base

- **Paying for public goods works.** Schemes that pay farmers for environmental outcomes (clean
  water, flood prevention, habitats, carbon) can deliver nature recovery. *Transferable*, provided
  they also keep farming viable.
- **Food and nature are not opposites.** Well-designed farming produces food and supports nature
  together, through better soil, hedgerows, and water management. *Transferable* as the basis for
  a balanced settlement.
- **Upstream land management cuts flooding.** Slowing water in the hills protects towns below.
  *Transferable*, linking land, water, and flooding.

---

## Options appraisal

**Option 1: Food production first.** Prioritise output and farm viability, environment secondary.
*Pros:* food security and farm incomes. *Cons:* misses the chance to reverse nature decline and
manage flooding.

**Option 2: Nature and public goods first.** Pay mainly for environmental outcomes. *Pros:* nature
recovery. *Cons:* risks farm viability and food security if pushed too far.

**Option 3: Nature and food together *(lead option)*.** Pay farmers for both food production and
environmental public goods, designed so the two reinforce each other. *Pros:* secure food, viable
farms, recovering nature, and lower flood risk. *Cons:* needs careful design so neither goal is
squeezed out. **Recommended.**

---

## The proposals (under the lead option)

1. **A subsidy framework that pays for both.** Settle a clear system that rewards farmers for
   producing food and for delivering environmental public goods (clean water, flood prevention,
   habitats, healthy soil, carbon), so both are funded rather than traded off.
2. **Back food security.** Support viable domestic production and shorter supply chains, in step
   with [bulk food procurement](../welfare/bulk-food-procurement.md) and
   [seasonal migration](../immigration/seasonal-migration.md).
3. **Reverse nature decline.** Fund habitat restoration and cleaner rivers, working with the
   [water](./water-and-utilities.md) reforms.
4. **Manage land for flooding.** Pay for upstream land management that slows water and protects
   towns, reducing the flood bill.
5. **Hold polluters to account.** Apply real duties and penalties for pollution of land and water,
   alongside the water-ownership reforms.

---

## Costs and benefits

Paying for nature and food together costs roughly what the old subsidies did, but buys more: secure
food, viable farms, recovering nature, and lower flood and clean-up bills. The figures belong in
the budget files.

---

## Delivery and timeline

- **Years 1-2:** settle the subsidy framework that pays for food and nature; give farmers
  certainty.
- **Years 2-5:** scale habitat restoration, soil and water measures, and upstream flood
  management.
- **Years 5-10:** secure food, viable farming, and measurable nature recovery.

---

## Risks and mitigations

- **One goal squeezing out the other.** *Mitigation:* design payments so food and nature reinforce
  each other, and monitor both.
- **Farmers left uncertain.** *Mitigation:* settle the framework quickly and keep it stable.
- **Schemes too complex to use.** *Mitigation:* make them simple enough that ordinary farms can
  take part.

---

## Consultation questions

1. How should payments balance food production and environmental outcomes?
2. What would most improve food security without harming nature?
3. Where should upstream flood management be targeted?
4. How do we keep the schemes simple enough for small and tenant farmers?
