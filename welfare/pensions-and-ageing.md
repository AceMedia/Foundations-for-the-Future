# Pensions and Ageing

*A forward-looking proposal, judged by what works, not by left or right.*

> **Summary for the reader**
> - **The problem:** Britain is ageing, and the way we fund retirement is not built for it. The
>   triple lock is costly and unpredictable, many people are not saving enough, and a vast pool
>   of pension capital sits underused.
> - **The options:** keep the triple lock, drop it, or replace it with a steadier, fairer link.
> - **Lead option:** **replace the triple lock with a smoothed earnings link (a "double lock")**,
>   lift private saving to adequate levels, and put pension capital to productive use without
>   risking savers.
> - **Timeline:** legislate the new link early; build saving adequacy over a decade.
>
> *Figures here are first-draft and will be checked in the budget-accuracy pass (#21).*

## The problem

A longer-living population is good news, but it reshapes the public finances, the labour
market, and the care system, and our pensions settlement has not kept up.

**The triple lock is costly and unpredictable.** Uprating the state pension by the highest of
earnings, inflation, or 2.5% each year was meant as a temporary catch-up. It has become a
permanent ratchet that makes long-term spending hard to plan and can rise faster than the wages
that pay for it.[^triplelock]

**Many people are not saving enough.** Automatic enrolment got more people saving, but minimum
contributions are still too low for a comfortable retirement, so a saving gap is building up
behind the headlines.

**Pension capital sits idle.** Britain holds one of the largest pools of pension and insurance
capital in the world, yet too little of it flows into productive domestic investment.

**The do-nothing baseline.** Leave this and the settlement becomes unaffordable and unfair
between generations: workers pay more for a system they may not be able to rely on, while saving
shortfalls surface only when it is too late to fix them.

---

## Evidence base

- **Smoothed uprating is the norm abroad.** Most comparable systems uprate pensions by earnings
  or inflation, without an arbitrary 2.5% floor that can outpace wages. *Transferable* as a
  fairer, steadier rule.
- **Productive pension investment works.** Countries such as Canada and Australia channel
  pension capital into infrastructure and growth while protecting savers. *Transferable*, with
  strong safeguards and no compulsion that puts retirements at risk.

---

## Options appraisal

**Option 1: Keep the triple lock.** *Pros:* protects pensioner incomes and is popular.
*Cons:* costly, unpredictable, and unfair between generations. The do-nothing baseline.

**Option 2: Drop indexation guarantees.** Uprate at the government's discretion. *Pros:*
cheapest. *Cons:* leaves pensioners exposed and breaks trust.

**Option 3: A smoothed earnings link *(lead option)*.** Link the state pension to earnings and
inflation, but remove the 2.5% floor, so the pension keeps pace with the country without
ratcheting ahead of wages. Pair it with stronger private saving and productive use of pension
capital. *Pros:* affordable, predictable, fair between generations, still protects pensioners.
*Cons:* lower than the triple lock in some years, so it must be explained honestly. **Recommended.**

---

## The proposals (under the lead option)

1. **Replace the triple lock with a double lock.** Uprate the state pension by the higher of
   earnings or inflation, dropping the arbitrary 2.5% floor, so it tracks the country fairly and
   sustainably.
2. **Lift private saving to adequacy.** Raise automatic-enrolment contributions over time toward
   a level that actually funds a decent retirement, with care for lower earners.
3. **Put pension capital to work.** Encourage pension funds to invest more in domestic
   infrastructure and growth, on the Canadian and Australian model, with safeguards that always
   put savers first and no reckless compulsion.
4. **Tie the pension age to longevity, transparently.** Review the state pension age against life
   expectancy openly and with long notice, recognising that healthy life expectancy varies by
   region and occupation.
5. **Support working longer by choice.** Help older people who want to keep working to do so,
   rather than treating retirement as a single cliff edge.

---

## Costs and benefits

A double lock is more affordable and predictable than the triple lock while still protecting
pensioner incomes. Stronger private saving reduces future reliance on the state, and productive
investment of pension capital supports growth. The fiscal detail belongs in the budget-accuracy
pass (#21) and a matching budget file.

---

## Delivery and timeline

- **Years 1-2:** legislate the double lock; set out the path for automatic-enrolment increases.
- **Years 2-5:** phase in higher saving; reform the rules so pension funds can invest
  productively with safeguards.
- **Years 5-10:** a sustainable, predictable settlement with rising saving adequacy.

---

## Risks and mitigations

- **Pensioner incomes falling behind.** *Mitigation:* an earnings-or-inflation link still
  protects real value; only the 2.5% floor goes.
- **Saving increases squeezing low earners.** *Mitigation:* phase rises and protect the
  lowest paid.
- **Pressure to raid pension capital recklessly.** *Mitigation:* savers first, strong
  safeguards, and no compulsion that risks retirements.
- **Pension-age changes hitting manual workers hardest.** *Mitigation:* account for regional
  and occupational differences in healthy life expectancy.

---

## Consultation questions

1. Is an earnings-or-inflation double lock the right replacement, or should the link be set
   differently?
2. How high should automatic-enrolment contributions rise, and over what period?
3. How do we encourage productive investment of pension capital without putting savers at risk?
4. How should the state pension age reflect differences in healthy life expectancy?
