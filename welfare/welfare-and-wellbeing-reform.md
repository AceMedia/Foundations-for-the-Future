# Welfare and Well-being Reform

*A forward-looking proposal, judged by what works, not by left or right.*

> **Summary for the reader**
> - **The problem:** welfare is run as cash-and-hope. People are handed money and left to juggle
>   rent, food, and bills, which looks simple but is wasteful and leaves too many insecure. Around
>   £30 billion a year of housing support flows largely to private landlords, fuel poverty is
>   widespread, and food bank use has become permanent.
> - **The options:** keep cash-and-hope, give more cash through a universal basic income, or provide
>   the essentials directly.
> - **Lead option:** **secure the essentials directly** (guaranteed housing, covered bills, bulk
>   food), with a well-being budget and personal-development support, treating welfare as investment
>   in people.
> - **Timeline:** shift from crisis management to direct provision over a decade.
>
> *Figures here are first-draft and will be checked in the budget-accuracy pass (#21).*

## The problem

Welfare in Britain is treated as a set of financial transactions: hand out money, and expect people
to manage housing, food, bills, and everything else from it. What looks efficient on paper is
wasteful and damaging, leaving people struggling to survive rather than stable enough to live with
dignity and contribute back.

**Housing support leaks to landlords.** Britain spends around £30 billion a year on housing support,
much of it flowing to the private rental sector for housing the state does not own.[^hb] Since Right
to Buy, 2.6 million council homes were sold with only a fraction replaced.[^rtb]

**Essentials are insecure.** About 2.73 million households were in fuel poverty in England in 2024,[^fuel]
and cold homes cost lives and around £1.3 billion a year in NHS treatment.[^cold]

**Hunger has become normal.** The Trussell Trust handed out 2.99 million emergency food parcels in
2022/23, more than double 2015/16, in a wealthy country.[^food]

**Sanctions cost more than they save.** Punitive work-capability assessments and sanction regimes
have been found to cost more to administer than they save, while harming mental health.[^sanctions]

**The do-nothing baseline.** Leave this and the money keeps subsidising landlords and crisis
services, people stay insecure, and the state pays repeatedly for failure it could have prevented.

---

## Evidence base

- **Direct provision works abroad.** Finland has provided free school meals since 1948, improving
  outcomes and reducing inequality.[^finland] Centralised public food procurement keeps costs and
  waste down. *Transferable* as the model for securing essentials.
- **Support beats sanctions.** Denmark offers personalised training and coaching rather than blanket
  sanctions, and the OECD found it more effective at moving people into work with dignity.[^denmark]
  *Transferable* as the approach to personal development.
- **Well-being investment pays back.** France's chèques vacances scheme reduces social exclusion and
  supports local economies.[^ancv] Loneliness costs UK employers around £2.5 billion a year.[^lonely]
  *Transferable* as the case for a well-being budget.

---

## Options appraisal

**Option 1: Cash-and-hope status quo.** Hand out money and leave people to manage. *Pros:* simple.
*Cons:* wasteful, insecure, and it subsidises landlords and crisis services. The do-nothing baseline.

**Option 2: Universal basic income.** Give everyone more cash with fewer conditions. *Pros:* simple
and unconditional. *Cons:* in a market short of affordable housing and energy, more cash can be
swallowed by rent and bills without fixing supply, and it does not secure the essentials people
actually need. Not the lead.

**Option 3: Direct provision *(lead option)*.** Secure housing, bills, and food directly, with a
well-being budget and personal-development support. *Pros:* guarantees stability, cuts waste and
landlord subsidy, improves health, and frees people's cash for things that improve their lives.
*Cons:* needs the housing and food systems built to deliver it. **Recommended.**

---

## The proposals (under the lead option)

1. **Guarantee housing.** Redirect housing support into council housing the state owns, with rents
   covered within the welfare budget, building a public asset and cutting homelessness, in step with
   the [housing chapter](../housing/housing-policy.md).
2. **Secure essential bills.** Cover electricity, gas, and water within the welfare budget so people
   are not at the mercy of volatile energy markets, preventing cold-home harm and crisis spending.
3. **Provide food through bulk procurement.** Extend the state's existing bulk food systems to
   households, with tailored nutrition and dietician input, cutting cost, ill health, and waste, in
   step with [bulk food procurement](./bulk-food-procurement.md).
4. **A well-being budget.** A dedicated allowance for community, recreation, and personal growth,
   because people need more than food and shelter, with light accountability rather than punishment.
5. **Personal development, not sanctions.** Replace punitive assessments and sanctions with training,
   apprenticeships, volunteering, and coaching, and for those who cannot work, support focused on
   quality of life, linking to [disability policy](./disability-policy.md).
6. **Easy, non-punitive recovery and medical relief.** Build confidential routes into addiction
   treatment, treat relapse as a health setback, and ensure clinically prescribed cannabis and
   related products face low or zero vice duty so patients are not priced like leisure consumers, in
   step with [vice taxation](../health/vice-taxation-and-betterment.md).

## Costs and benefits

Securing essentials directly cuts the money wasted on landlord subsidies, preventable NHS costs, and
emergency food aid, and shifts welfare from crisis management to building public assets and human
capacity. People are left with more disposable income, not less, because rent, food, and bills are
covered. The figures belong in the budget-accuracy pass (#21) and the welfare-savings file.

---

## Delivery and timeline

- **Years 1-3:** begin redirecting housing support into council housing; pilot direct food provision
  and the well-being budget; replace sanctions with development support.
- **Years 3-7:** scale direct provision of housing, bills, and food; embed recovery support.
- **Years 7-10:** a welfare system that secures essentials directly and invests in people.

---

## Risks and mitigations

- **Direct provision feeling paternalistic.** *Mitigation:* secure essentials but expand cash freedom
  through the well-being budget.
- **Delivery systems not ready.** *Mitigation:* build alongside the housing and food chapters and
  phase the rollout.
- **Well-being budget misuse.** *Mitigation:* light accountability and positive reinforcement, not
  punishment.
