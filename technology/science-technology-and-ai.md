# Science, Technology, Innovation and AI

*A forward-looking proposal, judged by what works, not by left or right.*

> **Summary for the reader**
> - **The problem:** Britain invents things and commercialises them abroad, scale-up capital is
>   thin, R&D is unevenly spread, and rules for AI and data lag the technology.
> - **The options:** light-touch targeted rules, strong precautionary regulation, or matching the
>   strongest global standard.
> - **Lead option:** **pro-innovation regulation with targeted guardrails**, turning research into
>   companies that stay here, finishing connectivity, and building cyber resilience, with no
>   national digital ID.
> - **Timeline:** set the approach early; build capability over a decade.
>
> *Costings are indicative; the detailed figures live in the budget files.*

---

## The problem

Technology is where much of the future's growth and productivity will come from, and Britain keeps
giving its lead away.

**We invent here and scale abroad.** World-class research too often becomes companies and jobs in
other countries, because scale-up capital and support are thin at home.

**R&D is uneven and under-leveraged.** Britain spends around 2.8% of GDP on research and development,
but it is concentrated in a few places and too little of it turns into companies; the UK rejoined the
EU's Horizon Europe research programme in 2024, which helps, but there is no clear national R&D mission
or target.[^rd] This connects to the regional argument in the
[economy chapter](../economy/entrepreneurship-and-renewal.md).

**Rules lag the technology, especially on AI.** AI and data regulation often arrives late and by
accident, and the hardest question, the risks from frontier foundation models, is barely addressed.
Britain set up an AI Safety Institute but has no settled regime, while the EU has its AI Act and the US
acts by executive order.[^ai]

**Connectivity and cyber are unfinished.** Broadband and mobile coverage are still patchy in
places, and cyber resilience across government and critical services needs strengthening.

**The do-nothing baseline.** Leave this and we keep exporting our best ideas, regulate by accident,
and stay exposed on cyber, while the regions miss out on the growth.

---

## Evidence base

- **Pro-innovation, sector-based regulation can work.** Addressing specific, real harms through the
  right regulators, rather than one heavy upfront regime, gives certainty while protecting people.
  *Transferable*, reviewed as the technology moves.
- **Scale-up capital makes the difference.** Where founders can raise growth capital at home,
  companies stay. *Transferable*, linking to [financial services](../economy/financial-services.md)
  and pension capital.
- **Privacy and innovation are compatible.** Good technology policy does not require a national
  identity database, in line with the framework's [civil liberties](../governance/civil-liberties.md)
  position. *Transferable* as a principle.

---

## Options appraisal

**Option 1: Pro-innovation, targeted guardrails *(lead option)*.** Light-touch, sector-based rules
that back innovation while addressing clear harms, reviewed as the technology evolves. *Pros:*
certainty for innovators, protection where it is needed, room to grow. *Cons:* must stay alert so
real harms are not missed. **Recommended.**

**Option 2: Precautionary, strong upfront rules.** A heavy statutory regime set out in advance.
*Pros:* clear limits. *Cons:* risks freezing innovation and pushing it abroad.

**Option 3: Match the strongest global standard.** Align with the most robust international regime.
*Pros:* certainty and trust. *Cons:* may import rules that do not fit Britain's strengths.

---

## The proposals (under the lead option)

1. **Regulate AI pro-innovation, with eyes on frontier risk.** Use existing regulators and
   sector-based rules for everyday AI and data harms, with no national digital ID and strong privacy.
   For the genuinely new risk, frontier foundation models, give the AI Safety Institute real teeth and
   work with allies on shared standards, rather than a heavy upfront regime that freezes ordinary
   innovation.[^ai]
2. **Turn research into companies that stay.** Back scale-up capital, including productive use of
   pension capital, so founders grow their firms here rather than selling early or moving abroad.
3. **Spread R&D across the country.** Fund research and innovation in the regions, tied to the
   [economy](../economy/entrepreneurship-and-renewal.md) and the universities named there.
4. **Finish connectivity.** Complete broadband and mobile coverage, including rural areas, as basic
   infrastructure.
5. **Build cyber resilience.** Strengthen cyber security across government and critical services, in
   step with [defence and resilience](../defence/defence-and-aid-resilience.md).
6. **Back R&D, compute, and sovereign capability.** Set a clear R&D ambition, invest in the compute and
   data infrastructure that modern science and AI need, support semiconductor and other sovereign
   capabilities, stay in Horizon Europe, and tie it to global-talent visas (see
   [immigration](../immigration/immigration.md)).

## Costs and benefits

Backing scale-up and R&D and finishing connectivity are investments that grow the economy and keep
high-value jobs here, while pro-innovation rules avoid the cost of driving innovation abroad. The
figures belong in the budget files.

---

## Delivery and timeline

- **Years 1-2:** set the pro-innovation regulatory approach; rule out a national digital ID; back
  scale-up capital.
- **Years 2-5:** spread R&D funding; complete connectivity; strengthen cyber.
- **Years 5-10:** more research commercialised at home, across the country, with resilient systems.

---

## Risks and mitigations

- **Real harms slipping through light rules.** *Mitigation:* targeted guardrails and active
  regulators, reviewed as technology moves.
- **Capital still leaving.** *Mitigation:* fix scale-up finance and pension investment together.
- **Cyber attacks on critical services.** *Mitigation:* invest in resilience and standards.

---

## Consultation questions

1. What is the right balance of pro-innovation rules and guardrails for AI?
2. How do we make sure scaled-up firms stay and grow in Britain?
3. How do we spread research strength beyond a few cities?
4. Where is cyber resilience weakest, and how do we fix it?
5. How should frontier AI be regulated, and what powers should the AI Safety Institute have?
6. What R&D ambition and compute investment should Britain set?
